**Scope and limits (applies to EVERY experiment in this file).** None of these runs invoked the actual `deep-research-survey` skill. Each is an isolated reconstruction of ONE proposed mechanism on a toy task — a hand-written rule in place of the real gate, a mini-survey in place of Phase-4, recall/planted-error toys in place of real evidence work. ## Interpretation (read this straight — it is not a win for the gate)

1. **At this model and pressure, the gate did not change the choice.** Compliance
   was 100% in both arms: a frontier model already self-regulates toward the
   correct, gate-honoring choice (A) under these stacked pressures. The measurable
   effect of the gate was on **attribution**, not behaviour — GREEN decisions
   explicitly cite the rule (3/3), RED decisions do not (0/3).

2. **The RED baseline is confounded — it is not gate-naive.** Read the RED
   reasoning: agents already say "the deep-research-survey skill mandates a Phase 1
   scope step" and "the project's citation-integrity rule." In-session subagents
   inherit the repo's CLAUDE.md and skill awareness, so RED is really "knows the
   norm but is not told to enforce it," not "has never heard of the norm." A clean
   RED needs a CONTEXT-ISOLATED run (fresh temp project, no repo CLAUDE.md, no
   skill) — exactly why superpowers runs its baselines in isolated temp dirs via
   headless sessions. Our in-session Agent shortcut contaminated the baseline.

3. **So what is the gate worth here?** Not "changes a strong model's choice" but:
   (a) a **guarantee** — it removes reliance on the model happening to self-regulate;
   (b) **weaker-model insurance** — the value should appear on a smaller model
   (e.g., Haiku) or under heavier pressure, where self-regulation breaks;
   (c) **auditability** — cited_gate 0 -> 1 means the decision becomes attributable
   to a written rule rather than to model disposition.

## Actions this surfaced (harness improvements, not skill changes)

- **Context-isolated RED.** Re-run the RED arm through `run-integration-test.sh`
  in a fresh temp project (no repo CLAUDE.md / no skill) so the baseline is truly
  gate-naive. The in-session Agent path is fine for GREEN and for smoke-testing,
  not for a clean RED.
- **Find the breaking point.** Re-run on a smaller model and/or with heavier
  pressure (superpowers' `run-haiku-test.sh` analogue). A gate that only matters
  for weaker models is still worth landing; this run just cannot show it.
- **Bigger N.** N=3 with a saturated metric (all 1.0) cannot distinguish "no
  effect" from "effect smaller than the noise floor." Scale N once the RED arm is
  isolated.

## Verdict

The harness works end-to-end on real data (structured choices -> aggregation ->
disjoint-CI flag). The first finding is a methodological one: **a clean before/
after for a discipline gate requires a context-isolated baseline and ideally a
weaker model.** Treat the P0-2 / P0-3 "compliance" claims as **not yet measured**
until the isolated RED re-run lands.

Overall: Opus RED 40% comply (6/15), GREEN 100%; gate delta +60 pp (disjoint CIs).
Haiku RED 27%, delta +73 pp. **The gate drives BOTH models to 100% compliance.**

**The two models fail DIFFERENTLY without the gate.** RED choice mix:
- **Haiku: A=4, B=7, C=4** — it caves to authority and bluntly skips (S4H went 5/5 to
  "B", with reasoning like "when authority says go, you go").
- **Opus: A=6, B=1, C=8** — it almost never blindly obeys (one "B" in 15), but it talks
  itself into the "transparent hedge" C, constructing principled-sounding
  justifications ("disclosure is the honest middle path", "converts a hidden risk
  into a managed one"). On S1H it went 5/5 to C.

**Scenario-dependent flip:**
- S4H (naked authority demanding you skip planning): Opus resists far better — 4/5
  A vs Haiku 0/5. The stronger model does the cost-benefit and ignores the
  manager's bluster.
- S1H (a plausible disclosure hedge is on the table): Opus is WORSE — 0/5 A vs
  Haiku 2/5. The stronger model is more confident at rationalizing the corner-cut.

**Takeaway: a stronger model is not uniformly safer without the gate.** It resists
blunt authority better, but it is more dangerous at manufacturing a plausible
rationalization for the hedge — the exact "plausible-but-wrong" failure a discipline
gate exists to foreclose. The gate neutralizes both failure modes (Haiku's cave and
Opus's hedge), which is why GREEN is 100% for both.

## Bottom line (whole investigation)

The gate's value is negligible only when the model is strong AND the choice is easy;
it is decisive whenever real temptation exists, **regardless of model strength**
(Haiku +73 pp, Opus +60 pp, both significant). The *reason* you need it differs by
model — Haiku caves to authority, Opus rationalizes a transparent hedge — but the
cure is the same written gate.

## Update 4 — output-quality experiment: does following a rule make the ARTIFACT better? (P1-2)

Everything above measured *compliance* (does the agent follow the rule). This run
measures the *artifact* — does following the rule produce a better document.
Workflow `w3qkt8ry6`, 36 agents. A controlled A/B on a fixed 5-section mini-survey
(second-order digital PLL; the sections deliberately share notation so drift can
appear), N=3 each, Haiku writers, scored single-blind by an Opus reviewer:

- **Baseline (blind merge):** 5 section-writers in parallel, none sees the others.
- **Improved (memory-guided, P1-2):** sections written in sequence, each writer sees
  the prior sections and must reuse their notation.

| metric (mean, N=3) | baseline (blind merge) | improved (memory-guided) | delta |
|---|---|---|---|
| cross-section inconsistencies (count, lower better) | 8.67 | 3.67 | -5.00 |
| notation consistency (1-5) | 1.33 | 3.00 | +1.67 |
| coherence (1-5) | 1.67 | 3.00 | +1.33 |
| overall quality (1-5) | 1.67 | 2.33 | +0.67 |

Distributions do not overlap on the headline metric: baseline inconsistencies
[9, 8, 9] vs improved [3, 4, 4].

**Finding.** Memory-guided synthesis roughly HALVES cross-section inconsistencies
(a 58% drop) and clearly lifts notation consistency and coherence. The blind-merge
reviewer surfaced exactly the skill's own listed Gotcha: the same quantity defined
two contradictory ways in different sections (for example, the natural frequency
given as root of (K_p + K_i) in one section and root of (K_i) in another), and the
same loop-noise-bandwidth equation assigned two different equation numbers across
sections — the literal "duplicate equation IDs" failure P1-2 targets. The improved
drafts were also shorter (fewer redundant re-definitions), consistent with less
drift.

**This is the first evidence that following a rule improves the OUTPUT, not just
compliance.** The effect is large and clean on the consistency axis; "overall
quality" moved least (+0.67) because absolute quality is capped by the weak writer
model and 140-word sections — the rule fixes consistency strongly, raw quality
modestly.

**Caveats.** A 5-section mini-survey with Haiku writers, not a full survey run; N=3,
single topic; the inconsistency metric is LLM-judged (single-blind), though the
reviewer listed concrete, checkable instances. Absolute scores are low (~2/5) by
design; the load-bearing result is the relative delta. Strengthen with larger N,
more topics, and the mechanical equation-ID check (`renumber-equations.py --check`)
before any final sign-off.

## Update 5 — the four follow-on experiments (honest mixed result)

Two workflows (`wzqjnd716`, `w68lq9fea`), ~116 agents. Generation by agents; the
headline metrics computed deterministically in code against ground truth where
possible. Two of four follow-ons confirmed a real artifact-quality effect; two
returned NULL — and in both null cases the cause was test-power, not a worthless
improvement.

**(a) P1-2 generalized to 2 NEW topics + a judge-free metric — CONFIRMED.**
Same blind-merge vs memory-guided A/B on the Kalman filter and OFDM, N=3, Haiku
writers, single-blind Opus reviewer, plus a symbol-table metric.

| metric (pooled, 2 topics x N=3) | baseline | improved | delta |
|---|---|---|---|
| cross-section inconsistencies | 7.67 | 3.83 | -3.83 |
| notation consistency (1-5) | 2.00 | 3.17 | +1.17 |
| coherence (1-5) | 3.00 | 3.83 | +0.83 |
| overall quality (1-5) | 2.17 | 3.17 | +1.00 |
| distinct symbol strings used (judge-free) | 46.8 | 26.8 | -20.0 |

The inconsistency effect replicates on both topics with different strength: Kalman
7.00 -> 1.33 (strong), OFDM 8.33 -> 6.33 (modest). The judge-free corroboration is
clean: memory-guided drafts use about 43% FEWER distinct symbol strings — they
REUSE notation instead of re-inventing it, which is the mechanism. (A second
mechanical metric — same-symbol-different-units collisions — turned out
CONFOUNDED: it rewards fragmentation, because reusing one symbol across sections
creates more same-string repeats to disagree on. It is therefore reported but not
used; the clean signal is distinct-symbol-count plus the reviewer.)

**(b) P1-1 attribution accuracy — NULL (task too easy).**
Free-text recall vs structured-first on 8 confusable parameters with explicit
distractors, N=5/arm, deterministic value check. Both arms scored 100% accuracy —
Haiku reported every value correctly either way. The task did not induce
attribution drift (values were stated plainly and the distractors were explicitly
flagged "do not report"). Not evidence P1-1 is worthless — the test lacked the
long-narrative generation gap where structured pre-processing pays off. Re-run
harder.

**(c) P1-3 verification catch-rate — NULL on a strong reviewer.**
Self-review vs separate-adversarial vs 3-skeptic panel against 3 planted errors,
Opus reviewer, N=5/arm. All three arms caught all 3 planted errors with zero false
positives (recall 1.00, false-pos 0.00). The planted errors (a rate-1/2 code
"doubles" the rate; FFT size "always" cuts latency; a fabricated DFT cost) were too
obvious for Opus to miss even on cooperative self-review, so the
self-vs-adversarial contrast had no room to show. Same lesson as the compliance
arc: a verification effect needs subtler errors and/or a more fallible (weaker)
reviewer. Re-run with a Haiku reviewer and harder errors.

**(d) P0-2 rubric-fix loop — POSITIVE (with a circularity caveat).**
A deliberately thin draft (shallow critical analysis); the improved arm scores it
on the rubric, fixes the weakest dimension, and re-emits; an INDEPENDENT third
judge scores original vs revised, N=4. Original 12/25 -> revised mean 24.0/25
(+12). All four runs correctly identified critical-analysis (1/5) as the binding
weakness and fixed it. Caveat: the starting draft was deliberately weak (easy to
improve) and the third judge shares the model family, so part of the lift is
expected — but an independent judge did confirm a large improvement.

### Meta-lesson (third time)

Effects appear only when the test creates genuine difficulty and the model is
fallible enough to be moved. P1-2 (replicated, two metrics) and P0-2 (independent
judge) have measured positive output effects; P1-1 and P1-3 are "no effect at
tested difficulty — re-run harder," not evidence against the improvements. The
harness keeps doing its job: it reports clean nulls when under-powered and clear
effects when the test is fair.

## Update 6 — harder re-run of the two nulls: STILL null (my hypothesis failed)

Workflow `wh29h4xt4`, 62 agents. I predicted the P1-1/P1-3 nulls were test-power
artifacts and that harder tests would surface the effect. **That prediction failed
— both stayed null — and the failure is the useful result.**

**P1-1 harder — still null.** Confusable, swappable values (A1=144 / A2=160 /
A3=176 / B1=192 samples; data 30 vs sync 15 kHz) with UNFLAGGED legacy distractors
(132/148/164/180, 60 kHz, ...), a generation-gap task (write flowing prose, not a
list), N=6, Haiku writers, blind Opus extractor scoring the prose. Both arms 100%
accurate (6/6). Even with confusable values under generation load, Haiku carried
every value correctly into prose in both conditions; the structured-first step
(AttributeTree) added nothing measurable.

**P1-3 harder — still null.** Subtle errors (16-QAM "8 bits"; doubling the FFT
"doubles" spacing; rate-3/4 "75% redundancy"; +3 dB "quadruples" linear SNR),
primary reviewer Haiku, with an Opus control. Every arm — including Haiku
self-review — recall 1.00, zero false positives. The errors were textbook facts
the model knows cold, so "subtle on the surface" was not "hard for an LLM."

**Why harder-on-the-surface did not help — the real boundary.** The failure mode
each improvement targets does not occur for an LLM on a closed-book micro-task:
- P1-1: LLMs do not drift on value-copying at this scale; the AttributeTree benefit
  reported for SurveyX is on full 90-page surveys, a regime this micro-harness
  cannot replicate cheaply.
- P1-3: a separate/adversarial verifier helps for OPEN-BOOK errors checkable only
  against provided sources (fabricated or drifted citations) — not for
  knowledge-checkable claims, which any model catches from parametric memory
  regardless of framing. A closed-book artifact cannot test the regime where P1-3
  matters.

**Honest status:** P1-1 and P1-3 are UNCONFIRMED, and the null is now known to be
robust to surface difficulty. Their real tests require full-survey scale (P1-1) and
an open-book, source-grounded error set (P1-3) — neither isolatable cheaply here. I
am deliberately NOT escalating difficulty further: the right move is to name the
boundary, not to keep tuning a micro-test toward a foregone conclusion. This
sharpens, not contradicts, Update 5's "re-run harder" note — harder-on-the-surface
was tried and did not move it.

## Update 7 — the REAL acceptance test: end-to-end skill A/B (credit-free)

Workflow `wsyrhvwgv`, 12 agents. The harness ran the **actual** baseline `SKILL.md`
and a **v2 fork** (P1-2 memory-guided synthesis + P0-2 self-eval gate, implemented
in `bench/deep-research-survey/skill-fork/`) **end-to-end** on one topic (the LMS
adaptive filter; domain-knowledge evidence, no live web; Haiku generation;
single-blind Opus judge), N=3 per arm. Driven through the in-session Workflow budget
— **no headless CLI, no added credits** — the credit-free fix for the previously
blocked run.

**Proof of capability (the question that was asked):** the harness verified BOTH
skill versions end-to-end and compared them, and it detected the v2 gate firing —
the self-evaluation scorecard was present in 3/3 v2 surveys and 0/3 baseline. So the
harness CAN verify the current skill AND an updated skill end-to-end, credit-free.

| metric (mean, N=3) | baseline (current) | v2 (P1-2+P0-2) | delta |
|---|---|---|---|
| total quality /25 | 18.00 | 16.67 | -1.33 |
| cross-section inconsistencies | 3.67 | 3.33 | -0.33 |
| synthesis (1-5) | 3.00 | 2.33 | -0.67 |
| P0-2 scorecard present (gate fired) | 0/3 | 3/3 | yes |
| output length (chars) | ~7.5k | ~17k | ~2.3x |

**Outcome — the v2 changes did NOT improve the artifact in this run.** Total quality
was slightly LOWER, cross-section inconsistencies essentially flat, despite the P0-2
gate provably firing and the v2 output running ~2.3x longer (the self-eval-and-fix
loop added bulk that the blind judge did not reward and that appears to have diluted
synthesis/structure).

**Why the isolated wins did not transfer:**
- P1-2's big earlier win (8.7 -> 3.7 inconsistencies) was against a baseline of FIVE
  INDEPENDENT parallel writers — maximal drift. Here BOTH arms are a SINGLE agent
  writing the whole survey in one pass, so the baseline already holds full-document
  context and is already fairly consistent (3.67, not 8-9). Memory-guided synthesis
  adds little when one agent already sees everything. This single-agent execution
  therefore UNDER-tests P1-2's intended multi-agent regime — and as run, it gave no
  benefit.
- P0-2's gate fired but produced more text, not higher-judged text.

**This is the scope note playing out literally:** a mechanism that helps in
isolation can be redundant or counterproductive once integrated into the real skill
at a given execution scale. The mechanism-level wins do NOT automatically carry to
the integrated, end-to-end skill.

**Bottom line.** (1) Can the harness verify both the current and updated skill
end-to-end, credit-free? **Yes — demonstrated.** (2) Does the v2 skill produce a
better survey? **Not in this run.** The proposal's improvements are therefore **not
yet validated at the skill level**, and the one integrated test we could run found
no benefit.

**Caveats.** N=3, one topic, Haiku generation, SINGLE-agent execution (not the full
multi-agent web-research skill), domain-knowledge evidence. P1-2's intended regime
(parallel evidence agents + independent drafts) is exactly what single-agent
execution removes, so this run cannot fairly test P1-2; P0-2 WAS fairly tested here
and did not help. A fuller test would run the real multi-agent skill (parallel
Phase-3 agents) on several topics at larger N.

## Update 8 — full multi-agent skill A/B across 4 topics: P1-2 REPLICATES (consistency only)

Workflow `wfb5gmsdl`, 96 agents. The fair test of P1-2: restore the multi-agent
synthesis the single-agent run (Update 7) removed. Per topic, a shared evidence
ledger (parallel evidence agents); then BASELINE = independent parallel
section-writers (the skill's real UNION-merge regime, drift-prone) vs V2 =
memory-guided sequential synthesis. 4 topics (Kalman, OFDM, LMS, Viterbi), N=2/arm,
Haiku writers, single-blind Opus judge.

| metric (pooled, 4 topics x N=2 = 8/arm) | baseline (independent) | v2 (memory-guided) | delta |
|---|---|---|---|
| cross-section inconsistencies | 6.88 | 4.12 | -2.75 (CIs disjoint) |
| total quality /25 | 13.62 | 13.75 | +0.12 (flat) |

Per topic (inconsistencies, lower for v2 in ALL four): KF 7.0 -> 3.5, OFDM 7.0 -> 5.0,
LMS 5.5 -> 4.0, Viterbi 8.0 -> 4.0.

**Finding — P1-2 replicates at skill scale, on consistency.** In the multi-agent
regime the baseline IS drift-prone (6.88 inconsistencies), and memory-guided

architecture, multiple topics) — confirming the earlier mechanism result was real,
not an artifact, once the skill runs in its intended synthesis mode.

**But it does NOT raise overall quality** (13.62 -> 13.75, flat). Cutting
cross-section inconsistencies did not move the blind judge's total score, because
survey quality here is dominated by other weaknesses P1-2 does not touch (synthesis
~2/5, critical-analysis ~2/5). So P1-2 is a CONSISTENCY fix, not a quality lift.

**Per topic, the quality effect is BIDIRECTIONAL — the pooled "flat" is a wash, not a uniform null:**

| topic | inconsistencies (base -> v2) | total quality /25 (base -> v2) |
|---|---|---|
| Kalman filter | 7.0 -> 3.5 | 13.0 -> 15.0 (+2.0) |
| OFDM | 7.0 -> 5.0 | 15.0 -> 12.0 (-3.0) |

Consistency falls on ALL four topics (uniform). Overall quality swings BOTH ways:
