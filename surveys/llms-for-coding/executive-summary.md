## 1 Executive Summary

<a id="p-1-executive-summary-1"></a><!-- para:1-executive-summary-1 --> Large language models for code are built on the same Transformer architecture and next-token objective as general LLMs, yet they form a distinct field — because code has a property natural language lacks: it is *executable*, and its correctness can be *verified* by running it. This single property is the thread that runs through everything in this survey. It is why evaluation is execution-based, why reinforcement learning for code can use a cheap and ungameable reward, why test-time methods can check their own work, and why autonomous agents that edit-and-test are the field's current frontier.

<a id="p-1-executive-summary-2"></a><!-- para:1-executive-summary-2 --> **The arc.** The field moved in five years from understanding to generation to reasoning to agency (Section 4). Encoder models like CodeBERT <!-- cite:2 --> [[2]](references.md#ref-2) gave way to Codex <!-- cite:1 --> [[1]](references.md#ref-1), which in 2021 reframed code generation as a functional-correctness problem and introduced the HumanEval benchmark and the pass@k metric. An open-model wave followed — CodeGen, InCoder, Code Llama, StarCoder, DeepSeek-Coder, Qwen2.5-Coder <!-- cite:3 --> [[3]](references.md#ref-3), <!-- cite:4 --> [[4]](references.md#ref-4), <!-- cite:6 --> [[6]](references.md#ref-6), <!-- cite:8 --> [[8]](references.md#ref-8), <!-- cite:10 --> [[10]](references.md#ref-10), <!-- cite:11 --> [[11]](references.md#ref-11) — that matched and then closed the gap to closed models, culminating in open mixture-of-experts systems comparable to GPT-4-class models <!-- cite:43 --> [[43]](references.md#ref-43). Two shifts then redefined the frontier: reasoning models trained by reinforcement learning on verifiable rewards reached competition-level coding (DeepSeek-R1 at the 96.3rd Codeforces percentile; o3 at the 99.8th <!-- cite:25 --> [[25]](references.md#ref-25), <!-- cite:26 --> [[26]](references.md#ref-26)), and autonomous agents reframed the task from "a function" to "a real GitHub issue" (Section 12).

<a id="p-1-executive-summary-3"></a><!-- para:1-executive-summary-3 --> **Key takeaways.**

- <a id="p-1-executive-summary-4"></a><!-- para:1-executive-summary-4 --> **Data is the moat** (Section 6). Architecture is largely shared with general LLMs; what separates strong code models is corpus construction — licensing, deduplication, quality filtering, decontamination — and the phi-1 result that data quality can reshape the scaling curve <!-- cite:12 --> [[12]](references.md#ref-12).
- **Fill-in-the-middle is standard** (Sections 3, 7). A simple document transform gives causal models the ability to infill — essential for editing — at no measurable cost to ordinary generation <!-- cite:5 --> [[5]](references.md#ref-5).
- **The verifiable reward is the key enabler** (Sections 8, 9). Because tests are an executable oracle, code supports reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR), which underlies both execution-feedback RL and the reasoning-model breakthroughs <!-- cite:19 --> [[19]](references.md#ref-19), <!-- cite:20 --> [[20]](references.md#ref-20), <!-- cite:25 --> [[25]](references.md#ref-25).
- **Agents are the frontier, and the interface matters** (Section 12). A purpose-built agent-computer interface, not just a bigger model, drives much of agentic performance <!-- cite:32 --> [[32]](references.md#ref-32); resolved-rate numbers are *(model × scaffold × date)* products.
- **Evaluation is a moving target** (Section 13). The benchmark lineage from HumanEval to SWE-bench to LiveCodeBench is a story of patching test inadequacy, contamination, and unrealism — and a leaderboard number is not a capability claim.
- **State-of-the-art numbers need an asterisk** (Section 15). The late-2025 frontier band near 80% on SWE-bench Verified is real progress wrapped in documented contamination caveats; this survey reports it as a dated estimate, not a fact.

Sections 5–9 follow the training pipeline from data to reasoning. Sections 10–12 cover serving, retrieval, and agents. Section 13 treats evaluation. Sections 14–18 turn to practice: tradeoffs, the state of the art, safety, a decision framework, and open problems. A reader who wants the practical bottom line can jump to the decision checklist in Section 17; a reader who wants the intellectual core should read Section 2 and Section 9, where the executable-reward property is introduced and then cashed out. Every external claim and number in this survey is traceable to a source acquired and read for it, per the project's citation-integrity discipline.
