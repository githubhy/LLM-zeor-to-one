## 15 State of the Art and Practice

<a id="p-15-state-of-the-art-and-practice-1"></a><!-- para:15-state-of-the-art-and-practice-1 --> This section is a dated snapshot, and it is written with deliberate caution. Code-model leaderboards move in weeks, and as of this writing some public 2026 leaderboards list model identities and scores that cannot be verified against primary system documentation. The discipline here, following the citation-integrity rule this survey is built on, is to state the *progression* from primary sources and to present frontier figures only as explicitly dated, caveated estimates — never as load-bearing facts.

<!-- sec:15.1 -->
### <a id="sec-15.1"></a>15.1 The Progression Curve

<a id="p-151-the-progression-curve-1"></a><!-- para:151-the-progression-curve-1 --> The single most vivid measure of progress is SWE-bench Verified, because it is realistic and execution-based (Section 13). The primary-anchored points: DeepSeek-Coder-V2 reported about 12.7% on SWE-bench in mid-2024 <!-- cite:43 --> [[43]](references.md#ref-43); DeepSeek-R1 reported 49.2% on SWE-bench Verified in early 2025 <!-- cite:25 --> [[25]](references.md#ref-25). By late 2025, public leaderboards report a frontier band around 80% on SWE-bench Verified — but this figure carries a documented **contamination caveat**: the 500 Verified tasks predate their own publication and appear in training corpora, and reporting indicates frontier models can reproduce some gold patches verbatim, so the ~80% band is a dated web estimate, not a primary-verified capability number <!-- cite:49 --> [[49]](references.md#ref-49). On the contamination-resistant LiveCodeBench (Section 13), which time-windows fresh problems, the frontier band is reported near 90% <!-- cite:39 --> [[39]](references.md#ref-39), <!-- cite:49 --> [[49]](references.md#ref-49); because its construction resists memorization, it is the more trustworthy of the two for raw capability, though again the specific model identities behind 2026 snapshots are only partially verifiable. The function-level classics (HumanEval, MBPP) are saturated and no longer discriminate among frontier systems — the survey by Jiang et al. explicitly flags HumanEval as an unrepresentative de-facto standard <!-- cite:44 --> [[44]](references.md#ref-44).

### <a id="sec-15.2"></a>15.2 What Is Actually Deployed

<a id="p-152-what-is-actually-deployed-1"></a><!-- para:152-what-is-actually-deployed-1 --> Three deployment modes are live in production simultaneously (Section 5): inline autocomplete, chat/assistant, and autonomous agents. The most consequential recent shift is the rise of the **agent**: industry security tracking notes that coding agents are now the dominant category of deployed agentic AI, with the fastest-growing agentic tools being coding-focused <!-- cite:52 --> [[52]](references.md#ref-52). On the model side, the open-weight ecosystem (the StarCoder, DeepSeek-Coder, and Qwen-Coder lines of Sections 4 and 6) tracks the frontier closely enough that many production deployments are open-weight, chosen for cost, latency control, and the ability to self-host — while the very top of the agentic leaderboards is typically held by proprietary frontier models paired with mature scaffolds. The practical reality is a portfolio: small fast models for autocomplete, mid-size open or frontier models for chat, and the strongest available model for hard agentic tasks where solve rate justifies cost (Section 14).

### <a id="sec-15.3"></a>15.3 Reading State-of-the-Art Numbers Honestly

<a id="p-153-reading-state-of-the-art-numbers-honestly-1"></a><!-- para:153-reading-state-of-the-art-numbers-honestly-1 --> Three rules make SOTA claims survivable. First, **a benchmark number is a (model × scaffold × date) product** (Section 12): an agentic resolved-rate conflates the base model, the harness, and the moment in time, and none of the three can be inferred from the number alone. Second, **prefer contamination-resistant, primary-sourced figures**: a LiveCodeBench-style time-windowed score or a number in a vendor's own system card is worth more than a static-benchmark leaderboard entry, and far more than a leaderboard whose model identities cannot be confirmed. Third, **carry the asterisk**: where this survey cites a frontier band, it marks it dated and caveated rather than laundering it into a clean fact. The honest summary of the state of the art in 2026 is that code models have moved from solving toy functions to resolving a substantial fraction of real repository issues in a few years, that the open-weight frontier is close behind the proprietary one, and that the headline numbers are real progress wrapped in genuine measurement uncertainty — which is precisely why evaluation methodology (Section 13) is itself an open problem (Section 18).
