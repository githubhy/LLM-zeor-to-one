## Q1: Induction heads — previous-token-head → induction-head two-step mechanism, phase-change/bump during training, model sizes

- **Finding:** Induction heads are pairs of attention heads in *separate layers* that implement pattern completion on a repeated sequence `[A][B] ... [A] -> [B]`: a **previous-token head** in an earlier layer writes information about the token at position $t-1$ into the residual stream at position $t$; an **induction head** in a later layer uses this to attend back to the position *after* the earlier occurrence of the current token and copies its content forward, predicting the token that followed `[A]` last time. Olsson et al. (2022) argue induction heads may be the primary mechanistic driver of most in-context learning (ICL) in transformers of any size, with strong causal (ablation) evidence in small attention-only models and correlational evidence in larger models with MLPs.
- **Mechanism / derivation notes:**
  1. **Step 1 (previous-token head, layer $\ell$):** attends from position $t$ to position $t-1$ (a "shift by one" attention pattern) and copies the token/positional identity of $t-1$ into the residual stream at $t$ via its OV circuit.
  2. **Step 2 (induction head, layer $\ell' > \ell$):** its QK circuit uses the *current* token at the query position (say token `A` at position $t$) to search back over the context for previous positions whose "previous-token" info (written by step 1) matches `A` — i.e., it finds the earlier occurrence of `A` at position $s$; its OV circuit then copies forward the token at $s+1$ (`B`) into the output logits at $t$, boosting $P(\text{next}=B)$.
  3. Composition is via **K-composition**: the induction head's key vectors are constructed (in part) from the previous-token head's output (an instance of the QK/OV "virtual weights" framework from the *A Mathematical Framework for Transformer Circuits* companion paper), so the two heads form a genuine multi-layer circuit rather than two independent heads.
  4. **Phase change / "bump":** Plotting per-token training loss reveals a period where the loss curve improves markedly faster than the surrounding trajectory ("bump"); before the bump, a model's loss on later tokens in a context plateaus after ~50 tokens; after the bump, loss keeps improving out to hundreds of tokens, indicating a sudden gain in long-range in-context copying ability. This transition co-occurs (is causally/temporally linked) with the formation of induction heads, and the same bump appears not just in loss-vs-token-position curves but as a qualitative shift in ICL score.
  5. Evidence is multi-pronged (six lines of evidence in the paper): argued to hold "for models of any size" but strongest causal (ablation) evidence is on small, attention-only models; large models (with MLPs) show correlational (co-occurrence-in-time) evidence for the same phase change.
- **Values:** Phase-change / bump occurs in a token window of roughly $2.5\times10^9$ to $5\times10^9$ training tokens early in training — for large models this can be as little as ~1-2% of the way through training (per WebSearch-derived synthesis of the paper's training-curve section; **not yet verified against a direct quote from the primary source** — flagged below). Pre-bump loss plateaus after ~50 tokens of context; post-bump loss keeps improving to ~500+ tokens of context (same caveat).
- **Sources:**
  - Olsson, C., Elhage, N., Nanda, N., Joseph, N., et al. (2022), "In-context Learning and Induction Heads," Transformer Circuits Thread, https://transformer-circuits.pub/2022/in-context-learning-and-induction-heads/index.html (also arXiv:2209.11895) | tier=Primary | confidence=H (mechanism); confidence=M (exact numeric token-window values — derived from a WebSearch summary, not a direct verbatim quote from the PDF; needs a verbatim-quote check pass)
  - Elhage, N., Nanda, N., Olsson, C., et al. (2021), "A Mathematical Framework for Transformer Circuits," Transformer Circuits Thread (QK/OV composition formalism referenced for the K-composition point above; found by inference from search context, not independently re-verified this pass) | tier=Primary | confidence=M
  - Rohan Hitchcock, "Talk on In-Context Learning and Induction Heads" (seminar notes), https://rohanhitchcock.com/notes/2023-4-6-slt-seminar-induction-heads.html | tier=Explainer | confidence=L (secondary gloss, used only for cross-check, not for any load-bearing number)
- **Gaps/caveats:** The exact numeric token-window ($2.5$–$5\times10^9$ tokens) and the "~50 tokens plateau / ~500 tokens post-bump" figures came back from a WebSearch synthesis rather than a verbatim-quoted passage of the PDF — treat as **M-confidence, re-verify against the actual PDF figure/caption before citing a specific number in survey prose**. ## Q2: The IOI circuit (Wang et al. 2022, arXiv:2211.00593) — end-to-end circuit, head classes, head count

- **Finding:** GPT-2 small (12 layers × 12 heads) performs Indirect Object Identification (IOI) — e.g., "When Mary and John went to the store, John gave a drink to ___" → "Mary" — via a circuit of **26 attention heads spanning 7 functional classes**, discovered via path patching / causal interventions. The circuit recovers the large majority of the logit-difference behavior of the full model (a commonly cited figure is **~87%** of the model's logit-difference performance restored by the identified circuit — flagged M-confidence below since the search summary that produced it looked internally inconsistent).
- **Mechanism / derivation notes:** End-to-end causal story, in serial+parallel stages:
  1. **Duplicate Token Heads** (act at the S2 position, i.e. the second mention of the subject name) attend back to S1 (the first mention) and write a "this name is a duplicate" signal into the residual stream.
  2. **Induction Heads / Previous-Token Heads** can also feed the duplicate-detection signal forward (an application of the induction mechanism from Q1) so that duplicate/positional information about S1 reaches the S2 token position.
  3. **S-Inhibition Heads** (active at the END position, where the model must produce its output) attend to the S2 position and read out the duplicate-token signal; they then modify the **query** vectors of the downstream Name Mover Heads — shifting attention *away* from the (duplicated, subject) name and toward the correct (indirect object) name.
  4. **Name Mover Heads** (late layers, e.g. reported heads around layer 9-10) attend from END to the IO name position and copy that name's token identity into the output logits (their OV circuit is approximately a copying operation).
  5. **Negative Name Mover Heads** do the opposite — they attend to and *suppress* (write negative logit contribution for) a candidate name, acting as a calibration/anti-copying counterweight.
  6. **Backup Name Mover Heads** are heads that do *not* normally contribute to copying the IO name, but causally take over the Name Mover role (their attention/OV output for the IO name increases) when the primary Name Mover heads are ablated/knocked out — evidence for built-in redundancy in the circuit.
  7. The classes compose as a DAG: Duplicate Token / induction heads → S-Inhibition heads → (Negative) Name Mover heads → backup heads as a redundancy layer, all converging on the END-token residual stream that feeds the unembedding.
- **Values:**
  - GPT-2 small architecture: 12 layers, 12 heads/layer (144 heads total); circuit uses **26 heads across 7 classes** — direct search-result statement: "encompassing 26 attention heads grouped into 7 main classes."
  - S-Inhibition heads reported at layer.head indices **7.3, 7.9, 8.6, 8.10** (search-confirmed for 7.3/7.9 explicitly; 8.6/8.10 returned as part of the same list but not independently re-confirmed by a second source this pass).
  - Circuit performance: search summary states the circuit "recovers approximately 87% of the logit difference of the full model on the IOI task" — **this number appeared alongside an internally-confused restatement ("faithfulness score of 0.46... 13% of F(M)=3.56") that looks like a garbled secondary paraphrase, not a direct quote.** Treat the 87% figure as **unverified / M-L confidence** pending a direct read of the paper's Section on circuit faithfulness.
  - Only ~1.1% of GPT-2 small's total attention heads participate directly in the circuit per a search-result paraphrase (26/144 ≈ 18%, which does NOT match "1.1%" — **this figure is flagged as likely wrong/garbled and should NOT be used**; 26/144 ≈ 18.1% is the internally-consistent arithmetic).
- **Sources:**
  - Wang, K., Variengien, A., Conmy, A., Shlegeris, B., Steinhardt, J. (2022), "Interpretability in the Wild: a Circuit for Indirect Object Identification in GPT-2 small," arXiv:2211.00593, https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.00593 | tier=Primary | confidence=H (circuit structure, class names, 26-heads/7-classes count); confidence=L (the 87% recovery figure and the "1.1%" figure — both came from a search-engine paraphrase with an internal arithmetic inconsistency, not a verbatim quote)

## Q3: Grokking / modular addition (Nanda et al. 2023, arXiv:2301.05217) — Fourier-multiplication / trig-identity algorithm, progress measures

- **Finding:** A small (1-layer, attention+MLP) transformer trained to compute $a + b \bmod p$ (with $p = 113$) is fully reverse-engineered: it embeds inputs $a, b$ as points on a small set of circles (via a sparse subset of Fourier-basis frequencies), computes the sum $a+b \bmod p$ by **composing rotations** (i.e., using trig angle-addition identities), and reads off the answer via a further trig identity. The paper defines two **progress measures** — restricted loss and excluded loss — that track gradual internal circuit formation *underneath* a test/train loss curve that (per the "grokking" phenomenon) looks abrupt, revealing three training phases: memorization → circuit formation → cleanup.
- **Mechanism / derivation notes (signal-processing-native — DFT / trig-identity framing):**
  1. **Embedding as rotation:** each input token value $x \in \{0, ..., p-1\}$ is embedded (via the learned embedding matrix, when projected onto a small number of key Fourier frequencies $w_k$) as a 2D point $(\cos(w_k x), \sin(w_k x))$ on a circle of angular frequency $w_k = 2\pi k/p$ — i.e. a discrete-Fourier-basis feature, echoing the DFT basis vectors $e^{i 2\pi k x/p}$ but represented in real (cos/sin) coordinates.
  2. **Composition = rotation addition:** the attention+MLP block combines the two rotated embeddings for $a$ and $b$ using the angle-addition trig identities $\cos(w_k a)\cos(w_k b) - \sin(w_k a)\sin(w_k b) = \cos(w_k(a+b))$ (and the paired sine identity), so that multiplying/combining the circle-embeddings for $a$ and $b$ produces a new point at angle $w_k(a+b)$ — i.e. the sum is computed by "rotating" one embedding by the other's angle, done in parallel across several key frequencies $k$.
  3. **Readout:** the output logits for each candidate answer $c$ are formed (via the unembedding + another trig identity) as something proportional to $\sum_k \cos(w_k(a+b-c))$, which is maximized (constructively across the several frequencies) exactly when $c \equiv a+b \pmod p$ — a matched-filter / correlation-style readout across the small set of key frequencies.
  4. **Progress measures** (defined to track this circuit's formation despite a flat/abrupt visible loss curve):
     - **Restricted loss:** ablate (zero out) all *non*-key Fourier frequencies, keeping only the frequencies the final circuit uses, and measure loss — this isolates "how good is the generalizing (Fourier-multiplication) mechanism alone."
     - **Excluded loss:** ablate the key frequencies (the ones the circuit actually needs) and measure loss on the *training* set — this isolates "how much is the model still relying on memorization outside the generalizing circuit."
  5. **Three training phases:** (a) **memorization** — train loss drops (model memorizes training examples), test loss stays high, excluded loss stays low (model isn't yet relying on the generalizing circuit); (b) **circuit formation** — restricted loss begins dropping *before* the visible test-loss drop (i.e., the generalizing Fourier circuit is being built gradually, invisibly to the raw test-loss curve), while excluded loss rises (the model is starting to lean on the not-yet-complete circuit) and train/test loss remain visually flat; (c) **cleanup** — weight decay drives out the now-redundant memorization solution (which has higher weight norm than the compact Fourier circuit), sum of squared weights sharply drops, restricted loss keeps falling, excluded loss plateaus, and test loss suddenly drops — this is the "grokking" moment as visible in the standard train/test loss plot, even though the mechanism was assembled gradually beforehand.
- **Values:** Modulus $p = 113$ (the specific modular-addition task studied). Otherwise: no additional precise numeric hyperparameters (embedding dim, number of key frequencies, exact epoch/step counts for each phase boundary, weight-decay coefficient) were captured this pass — see Gaps.
- **Sources:**
  - Nanda, N., Chan, L., Lieberum, T., Smith, J., Steinhardt, J. (2023), "Progress measures for grokking via mechanistic interpretability," arXiv:2301.05217, ICLR 2023, https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.05217 | tier=Primary | confidence=H (mechanism: Fourier/rotation embedding, trig-identity composition, restricted/excluded loss definitions, 3-phase structure); confidence=M (exact $p=113$ value — corroborated across multiple independent search results, so higher confidence than the IOI numbers above, but not yet checked against a verbatim PDF quote)
  - Neel Nanda, "Grokking" (author's own blog summary), https://www.neelnanda.io/grokking-paper | tier=Primary (author-authored secondary summary) | confidence=H
  - AI Interpretability wiki summary, https://aiinterpretability.miraheze.org/wiki/Progress_measures_for_grokking_via_mechanistic_interpretability | tier=Explainer | confidence=M (cross-check only)

## Q4: Other reverse-engineered circuits — greater-than circuit (Hanna et al. 2023) and docstring circuit (Heimersheim & Janiak)

- **Finding — Greater-than circuit (Hanna, Liu, Variengien (2023), arXiv:2305.00586, NeurIPS 2023):** GPT-2 small can complete "The war lasted from the year 1732 to the year 17__" with a plausible two-digit end-year > the start year (i.e., it has learned an approximate "greater-than" comparison over the last two digits of a year). The paper isolates a circuit where a set of **attention heads** — reported set includes heads at (5,1), (5,5), (6,1), (6,9), (7,10), (8,8), (8,11), (9,1) — operating at the final token position attend back to the "YY" (two-digit start-year) token and move/copy that start-year value into the residual stream at the prediction site, effectively signaling "this is the reference number to compare against" to downstream computation. Then **MLP layers 8, 9, 10, and 11** (late-layer MLPs) perform the actual comparison: MLP9 and MLP10 in particular show an upper-triangular output pattern over candidate end-years — i.e. they directly boost logits for every candidate year strictly greater than YY and suppress logits for years $\le$ YY — implementing the greater-than operation as a learned monotonic (thresholding) nonlinearity rather than an explicit subtraction/comparison circuit.
- **Finding — Docstring circuit (Heimersheim & Janiak, ~2023, Alignment Forum / Redwood-Research-adjacent):** A small **4-layer, attention-only** toy transformer (trained on a mix of natural language and Python code) is reverse-engineered on the task of predicting the next argument/variable name inside a Python docstring (e.g., matching a function signature's argument list to the `:param` list in its docstring). The identified circuit is specified purely at the level of attention heads (no MLPs, since the model is attention-only) — search results reported roughly 7-8 named heads (e.g., 0.2, 0.4, 0.5, 1.4, 2.0, 3.0, 3.6 — count/list not fully reconciled, see caveats) composing across the 4 layers via up to three levels of head-to-head composition (K/Q/V-composition in the QK/OV framework sense). The task's evaluation metric is a **logit difference** between the correct next-argument-name token and the highest-logit alternative-argument-name token. This circuit is notable in the literature as a standard benchmark task for **automated circuit discovery** methods (e.g., cited as a test case in the ACDC / "Towards Automated Circuit Discovery" paper and in later "Hypothesis Testing the Circuit Hypothesis" work), precisely because it is small enough to be exhaustively hand-verified yet non-trivial (multi-level composition) as a test of whether an automated method recovers the same circuit a human found by hand.
- **Mechanism / derivation notes:**
  - Greater-than: two-stage circuit — (1) an attention sub-circuit that is essentially a **retrieval/copy operation** (find the YY token, move its value to the output position), directly analogous in spirit to an induction-style "look up a value elsewhere in context and copy it forward" pattern; (2) an MLP sub-circuit that is a **learned step/threshold function** of the retrieved value — MLP output as a function of YY looks like an upper-triangular indicator matrix over (YY, candidate-year) pairs, i.e. $f(\text{YY}, c) \approx \mathbb{1}[c > \text{YY}] \times (\text{boost})$, which is the natural "greater-than" nonlinearity a feed-forward layer can realize without any explicit subtraction.
  - Docstring: a compositional attention-only circuit (no nonlinearity beyond attention softmax + linear OV per head) where heads in later layers query the argument name to output based on positional/content information copied forward through earlier heads — an instance of the same general QK/OV virtual-weights composition machinery used in Q1/Q2, but here fully attention-only and used as an ACDC-style benchmark rather than a "found in the wild" LLM behavior.
- **Values:** Greater-than: attention heads (5,1), (5,5), (6,1), (6,9), (7,10), (8,8), (8,11), (9,1) [layer.head]; MLPs 8, 9, 10, 11 (late-layer). Docstring: 4-layer attention-only model; reported head list 0.2, 0.4, 0.5, 1.4, 2.0, 3.0, 3.6 (search result inconsistently said "8 main heads" while listing only 7 — flagged below); up to 3 levels of composition.
