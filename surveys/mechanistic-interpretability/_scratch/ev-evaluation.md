## Q1: What makes an interpretation good: faithfulness, completeness, and minimality of a circuit; formal/operational definitions and the standard faithfulness metric (logit-difference recovered under knockout)?

- **Finding:** The standard evaluation framework (introduced by Wang et al. 2022 for the IOI circuit, and adopted/formalized further by Conmy et al. 2023's ACDC) scores a discovered circuit $C$ against the full model $M$ on three axes: **faithfulness** (does $C$ alone reproduce $M$'s task performance?), **completeness** (does $C$ contain *all* components $M$ uses for the behavior — i.e. is nothing load-bearing left outside $C$?), and **minimality** (does every node in $C$ matter — i.e. is nothing dead weight left inside $C$?). The operational faithfulness metric is the percentage of the full model's logit difference (correct-token logit minus incorrect-token logit) that survives when the circuit is isolated (rest of the model mean-ablated/knocked out) versus running the full model normally.
- **Mechanism / derivation notes:**
  - Let $F(\cdot)$ denote a scalar task metric evaluated on a model/subgraph — for IOI this is the mean logit difference $\text{logit}(\text{IO}) - \text{logit}(\text{S})$ over the dataset.
  - **Faithfulness**: run the full model $M$ to get $F(M)$; run only the circuit $C$ (all edges/nodes outside $C$ are ablated, typically mean-ablated over a corrupted/reference distribution) to get $F(C)$. Faithfulness is small $\lvert F(M) - F(C) \rvert$, and the standard reporting form is a **recovered percentage**: $F(C)/F(M) \times 100\%$ (or, as later work stresses, $[F(C)/F(M)] \times 100\%$ averaged per-example vs. averaged-then-divided are NOT equivalent and materially change reported numbers — see Gaps below).
  - **Completeness**: for every subset $K \subseteq C$ (not just $K = \emptyset$), compare $F(C \setminus K)$ vs $F(M \setminus K)$ — i.e., knock out the same nodes $K$ from both the isolated circuit and the full model and check the resulting behavior tracks. This guards against the circuit silently omitting a component that only becomes load-bearing once something else is ablated (the completeness axis is precisely what "self-repair"/backup heads violate naively — see Q2).
  - **Minimality**: for every node $v \in C$, there should exist some subset $K \subseteq C \setminus \{v\}$ such that removing $v$ from $C \setminus K$ changes $F$ significantly (i.e., $v$ is never redundant/dead weight in every context) — operationalized by randomly ablating circuit components and checking behavior degrades; a component whose removal never changes anything fails minimality (also called sparsity).
  - ACDC (Conmy et al. 2023) operationalizes discovery itself as a search that greedily removes edges from the full computational graph (starting from the last layer, working backward) while thresholding the resulting drop in $F$ against a faithfulness tolerance — turning "find a circuit satisfying faithfulness+minimality" into an algorithm rather than a manual hypothesis.
  - Miller, Chughtai & Saunders (COLM 2024) show the "logit diff recovered" number is NOT robust to implementation choices: (a) node-ablation vs. edge-ablation give very different numbers (edge ablation can push recovered % close to ~150%, i.e. the isolated circuit can score *higher* than the full model on the metric); (b) order of averaging matters — [mean logit-diff of circuit] / [mean logit-diff of model] ≠ mean over examples of [logit-diff(circuit)/logit-diff(model)]; (c) prompt/dataset format shifts the number.
- **Values:**
  - IOI circuit (Wang et al. 2022, GPT-2 small): full-model mean logit difference $F(M) = 3.56$ (IO predicted over S 99.3% of examples). Reported circuit faithfulness: circuit recovers **87%** of full-model performance (search-engine-extracted summary states "faithfulness score of 0.46, which is 13% of $F(M)=3.56$" — i.e. gap of $\approx 0.46$, recovered $\approx 87\%$; the exact wording/table should be re-verified against the PDF directly, arXiv:2211.00593, before citing the 0.46 figure verbatim — flagged as a re-derivation gap below).
  - Miller/Chughtai/Saunders (COLM 2024): edge-ablation faithfulness on the same circuits reaches **≈150%** recovered (i.e., isolated circuit exceeds full-model logit diff), versus much lower numbers under node-ablation — same circuit, same task, metric swings by >50 percentage points purely from the ablation-unit choice.
- **Sources:**
  - Wang, Variengien, Conmy, Shlegeris, Steinhardt (2022), "Interpretability in the Wild: a Circuit for Indirect Object Identification in GPT-2 small," arXiv:2211.00593 (ICLR 2023) | tier=Primary | confidence=H
  - Conmy, Mavor-Parker, Lynch, Heimersheim, Garriga-Alonso (2023), "Towards Automated Circuit Discovery for Mechanistic Interpretability," arXiv:2304.14997 (NeurIPS 2023) | tier=Primary | confidence=H
  - Miller, Chughtai, Saunders (2024), "Transformer Circuit Faithfulness Metrics Are Not Robust," arXiv:2407.08734 (COLM 2024) | tier=Primary | confidence=H
  - Bilal Chughtai, "Transformer circuit faithfulness metrics are not robust" (author blog summary), bilalchughtai.co.uk/faithfulness/ | tier=Explainer | confidence=M
- **Gaps/caveats:** The exact numeric table in Wang et al. 2022 (0.46 / 13% / 87%) was extracted via a search-engine summary, not read directly from the PDF — re-verify against arXiv:2211.00593 §4 tables before citing the precise decimal in survey prose (WebFetch budget reserved for Q2/Q3; flagged rather than spent here since the qualitative claim — "circuit recovers most but not all logit-diff" — is corroborated across multiple hits). The completeness formal statement ("$F(C\setminus K) - F(M \setminus K)$ small for all $K \subseteq C$") is a paraphrase from search summaries of Wang et al. 2022 Section 4 — the universally-quantified-over-all-subsets form is standard in follow-up literature (e.g. Conmy et al.) but should be checked against the original paper's exact quantifier (all $K$ vs. a sampled/representative set of $K$, since exhaustive subset enumeration is exponential and the paper likely samples). No independent confirmation yet of a single unified "faithfulness score" formula used consistently post-2023 — Miller et al. 2024 is itself evidence the field has NOT converged on one operational metric.

## Q2: Interpretability illusions and self-repair (Bolukbasi et al. 2021 "An Interpretability Illusion for BERT"; McGrath et al. 2023 "The Hydra Effect"; backup heads): why naive ablations mislead and how the model self-repairs.

- **Finding:** Two distinct failure modes make naive single-intervention interpretability conclusions misleading. (1) The **interpretability illusion** (Bolukbasi et al. 2021, BERT): a neuron (or linear combination of neurons) can appear to cleanly encode one simple human concept when tested on a single corpus/dataset, but this is an artifact of that corpus occupying a narrow slice of the embedding-space geometry — testing the same "concept direction" on a different dataset reveals it does not generalize, i.e. the neuron was never really a clean detector for that concept. (2) **Self-repair / the Hydra effect** (McGrath et al. 2023, DeepMind): ablating one attention layer/head causes *other* downstream layers or heads to compensate and restore much of the lost computation — so the measured drop in a metric after knocking out a component systematically *underestimates* that component's true causal importance, because the rest of the network reorganizes around the ablation. Both results mean naive single-component ablation studies can produce plausible-looking but wrong causal stories: illusions overstate how cleanly a component encodes a concept, and self-repair understates how important an ablated component actually was.
- **Mechanism / derivation notes:**
  - **Illusion mechanism (Bolukbasi et al. 2021):** BERT's embedding space has geometric structure (e.g., anisotropy / clustering) such that a probe direction fit on a narrow corpus slice can separate examples along what looks like a single semantic axis, but the direction is actually entangled with corpus-specific correlates. Their methodological fix: always test a claimed concept-encoding direction/neuron against multiple, distributionally-different datasets before trusting it — if the "concept" only holds on one corpus, it is the corpus's narrowness, not the neuron's semantics, doing the work.
  - **Hydra effect mechanism (McGrath et al. 2023):** ablate an attention layer/head at position/context $x$; measure the layer's *direct effect* on the output logits (holding everything downstream fixed) versus its *total effect* (letting downstream layers respond naturally). Self-repair = total effect $\ll$ direct effect in magnitude, because later attention layers shift what they attend to / write, substituting an alternative computational pathway for the ablated one. Two concrete motifs the paper identifies: (a) the Hydra effect proper — other attention heads compensate for an ablated head's contribution to the residual stream; (b) a "counterbalancing" function of late MLP layers that downregulates whatever token the (now-perturbed) computation would otherwise over-predict (a form of confidence/calibration correction, not task-specific repair). A load-bearing enabling condition: fixing the model's final LayerNorm scale factor makes each layer's contribution to the logits linear (via the fixed unembedding), which is how the paper isolates and attributes direct vs. compensatory effects layer-by-layer.
  - **Backup/negative heads in IOI (Wang et al. 2022):** the IOI circuit in GPT-2 small has 3 "Name Mover Heads" that (in the unablated model) directly increase the logit of the correct name (IO) over the distractor (S). When these 3 heads are knocked out, a separate set of "Backup Name Mover Heads" — heads that do *nothing* detectable in the clean forward pass — activate and perform the same IO-boosting function, such that the task's logit difference drops only slightly. This is a concrete instance of self-repair: the backup heads' true causal role (redundant capacity for the task) is invisible under normal-model probing and is only revealed by the ablation itself, meaning the "circuit" as identified in the clean model was already incomplete. There are also "Negative Name Mover Heads" that actively *suppress* the correct answer; Copy Suppression (McDougall et al. 2023, arXiv:2310.04625) later reframes these negative heads as an instance of a *general* copy-suppression mechanism (heads that suppress/inhibit prediction of tokens that already appear earlier in context), not an IOI-specific quirk.
  - **Deeper mechanistic account of self-repair (Rushing & Nanda, ICML 2024, "Explorations of Self-Repair in Language Models"):** systematically ablates individual attention heads in GPT-2 Small (and other model families/sizes) across the full training distribution and finds self-repair is (a) real and general across model families/sizes, but (b) *imperfect* (the original direct effect is not fully restored on average) and (c) *noisy* (degree of compensation varies substantially prompt-to-prompt). They isolate two concrete sub-mechanisms: **(i)** changes in the final LayerNorm's scaling factor after ablation (since LayerNorm normalizes by the residual stream's norm, removing one component's contribution changes the norm and hence rescales every other component's effective contribution to the logits — a purely normalization-driven, not learned-compensation, form of "repair"), and **(ii)** a sparse set of neurons implementing **Anti-Erasure**, which re-write the erased information back into the residual stream via a learned mechanism (a genuine, trained compensatory circuit, distinct from the LayerNorm artifact).
  - **Practical consequence for Q1's faithfulness metric:** because ablation-based metrics (knockout / logit-diff-recovered) are exactly the operationalization self-repair distorts, any faithfulness number computed by ablating a candidate circuit is a *lower bound* on the true importance of the ablated components — the measured effect is diluted by whatever self-repair the rest of the network performs during that same forward pass. This directly connects to why Miller/Chughtai/Saunders (Q1) find node- vs. edge-ablation faithfulness numbers diverge so sharply: different ablation granularities let different amounts of self-repair happen.
- **Values:**
  - IOI Backup Name Mover Heads: knocking out the 3 main Name Mover Heads drops the logit difference by only **~5%** less than expected if there were no compensation — i.e. backup heads recover the large majority of the ablated heads' direct contribution (Wang et al. 2022, arXiv:2211.00593; figure/number as summarized by search-derived secondary source — exact page/table not yet directly re-read from the PDF, flagged below).
  - Hydra effect / self-repair magnitude (McGrath et al. 2023): ablating individual attention layers, downstream layers adaptively compensate to restore **approximately 70%** of the original (direct-effect) computation at the output — search-summary-derived figure; exact section/table in arXiv:2307.15771 not yet independently re-read.
  - Rushing & Nanda (2024): self-repair verified across "a variety of model families and sizes" but characterized as consistently *imperfect* and *noisy* (no single clean percentage reported across models per the retrieved summary — the paper's contribution is the two-mechanism decomposition (LayerNorm rescaling + Anti-Erasure neurons), not a single headline recovery number).
- **Sources:**
  - Bolukbasi, Pearce, Yuan, Coenen, Reif, Viégas, Wattenberg (2021), "An Interpretability Illusion for BERT," arXiv:2104.07143 | tier=Primary | confidence=H
  - McGrath, Rahtz, Kramár, Mikulik, Legg (2023), "The Hydra Effect: Emergent Self-repair in Language Model Computations," arXiv:2307.15771 (Google DeepMind) | tier=Primary | confidence=H
  - Wang, Variengien, Conmy, Shlegeris, Steinhardt (2022), "Interpretability in the Wild: a Circuit for Indirect Object Identification in GPT-2 small," arXiv:2211.00593 | tier=Primary | confidence=H (backup/negative name mover heads, already used in Q1)
  - Rushing, Nanda (2024), "Explorations of Self-Repair in Language Models," arXiv:2402.15390 (ICML 2024 / PMLR v235) | tier=Primary | confidence=H
  - McDougall, Conmy, Rushing, McGrath, Nanda (2023), "Copy Suppression: Comprehensively Understanding an Attention Head in GPT-2 Small," arXiv:2310.04625 | tier=Primary | confidence=M (found via search, not yet deeply read — cited here only for the "negative heads = general copy-suppression" reframing claim)
- **Gaps/caveats:** The two headline percentages (IOI backup-heads "~5% drop", Hydra effect "~70% restored") were both extracted from search-engine summaries rather than directly read from the source PDFs/tables — re-verify against arXiv:2211.00593 (IOI paper, backup name mover section) and arXiv:2307.15771 (Hydra Effect, main results figure) before citing as exact figures in survey prose; WebFetch budget for this evidence round was reserved and not spent since the qualitative claims are corroborated across multiple independent search hits. ## Q3: Interpretability benchmarks (Karvonen et al. 2024/2025 "SAEBench"; Huang et al. 2024 "RAVEL"): what each measures (SAE quality dimensions; disentangling attributes), and how methods rank.

- **Finding:** **SAEBench** (Karvonen et al., arXiv:2503.09532, ICML 2025) is a comprehensive benchmark for *sparse autoencoders (SAEs)* as an interpretability tool: it evaluates 200+ trained SAEs across 8 metrics grouped into 4 capability categories (concept detection, interpretability, reconstruction fidelity, feature disentanglement) — explicitly built to correct the prior field's over-reliance on the unsupervised sparsity–fidelity tradeoff (reconstruction loss at a given sparsity), which the paper argues does not track the properties that actually matter (human-interpretability, disentanglement, downstream usefulness). **RAVEL** (Huang, Wu, Potts, Geva, Geiger, arXiv:2402.17700, ACL 2024) is a benchmark for *any* interpretability/causal-localization method (not SAE-specific) that measures whether a method can find a representation of one entity attribute (e.g., a city's country) that can be surgically intervened on *without* disturbing a causally-entangled but distinct attribute of the same entity (e.g., the city's continent, or the language spoken there) — operationalizing the disentanglement property Q1's "completeness/minimality" axes gesture at, but for feature-level (not circuit-level) claims.
- **Mechanism / derivation notes:**
  - **SAEBench's 8 metrics / 4 categories:** (1) *Concept Detection* — Sparse Probing (do SAE latents linearly recover known concept labels?) and Feature Absorption (does a broad latent "absorb" a narrower one, hiding the narrower concept?); (2) *Interpretability* — an LLM-judge-based auto-interpretability score for whether a latent's activating examples correspond to a human-nameable concept; (3) *Reconstruction* — Loss Recovered (how much of the base model's next-token loss is preserved when activations are passed through the SAE's encode→decode roundtrip, an SAE-specific analog of Q1's faithfulness-under-ablation idea); (4) *Feature Disentanglement* — Unlearning (can a target concept be selectively removed by zeroing/ablating specific latents, without collateral damage to unrelated capabilities?), Spurious Correlation Removal (SCR), Targeted Probe Perturbation (TPP), and RAVEL-as-a-metric (SAEBench directly incorporates the RAVEL protocol as one of its disentanglement sub-metrics, evaluating whether SAE latents provide cleaner attribute-level interventions than raw neurons/directions).
  - **Architecture ranking (SAEBench):** across the 200+ SAEs (varying width — 4k/16k/65k latents; varying target sparsity $L_0 \approx 20$–$640$; multiple architectures — trained on Pythia-160M layer 8 and Gemma-2-2B layer 12), **Matryoshka SAEs** substantially outperform other architectures (TopK, ReLU, JumpReLU, standard/Gated) specifically on the feature-disentanglement metrics (TPP, SCR, RAVEL, Feature Absorption) in the commonly-used $L_0 \in [20, 200]$ operating range, and this advantage *grows with SAE scale* (wider dictionaries). Matryoshka is also near-best (not necessarily #1) on Sparse Probing. This directly falsifies the implicit prior assumption that minimizing the sparsity–reconstruction tradeoff (loss recovered vs. $L_0$) is sufficient — a headline motivation for building the benchmark at all.
  - **RAVEL's scoring construction:** the final **Disentangle Score** is the average of a **Cause Metric** (did the intervention successfully change the targeted attribute, e.g. made the model output "Japan" instead of "France" for a city's country?) and an **Isolation Metric** (did the same intervention *leave unchanged* a different, causally-separate attribute of the same entity, e.g. the city's continent or its language?) — a method scores well only if it can move one fact while provably not disturbing an entangled-but-distinct one. RAVEL brackets every evaluated method between two references: raw **neurons** (an easy/weak baseline — individual neurons are typically highly entangled, so disentangling via raw neurons is hard) and supervised **DAS** (Distributed Alignment Search — a trained, supervised causal-probing method) as a **skyline** (the best-case reference other unsupervised/self-supervised methods are compared against). The paper's own contribution, **MDAS** (Multi-task Distributed Alignment Search — DAS extended to satisfy multiple causal disentanglement criteria jointly rather than one attribute at a time), achieves state-of-the-art RAVEL results on Llama2-7B, beating single-task DAS.
  - **How SAEs rank on RAVEL (as an external, later evaluation, per Chaudhary et al./"Evaluating Open-Source Sparse Autoencoders on Disentangling Factual Knowledge in GPT-2 Small," arXiv:2409.04478, and the AdaptiveK paper, arXiv:2508.17320):** off-the-shelf SAEs generally underperform supervised skylines (DAS/MDAS) on disentanglement — consistent with SAEBench's own finding that unsupervised sparsity-only training does not automatically yield good disentanglement. A more recent SAE variant, **AdaptiveK** (complexity-driven, adaptive sparsity), reports a RAVEL disentanglement score of **0.62**, described as "substantially higher" than contemporary architectures (TopK, Matryoshka) at comparable sparsity — i.e., the architecture ranking is *not yet settled* as of the newest (2025-era) SAE variants, and Matryoshka's SAEBench-reported dominance is being actively contested by newer entrants on the RAVEL sub-metric specifically.
- **Values:**
  - SAEBench: 200+ SAEs evaluated; 3 widths (4k, 16k, 65k latents); 6 sparsity levels ($L_0 \approx 20$ to $\approx 640$); trained on **layer 8 of Pythia-160M** and **layer 12 of Gemma-2-2B**; 8 metrics across 4 categories; 7 SAE architectures/training algorithms compared. (Karvonen et al., arXiv:2503.09532 — figures from search-summary extraction, not yet independently re-read from the PDF's tables.)
  - RAVEL: Disentangle Score = mean(Cause Metric, Isolation Metric); reported per-metric numbers for one SAE evaluation configuration: RAVEL Cause $= 0.655$, RAVEL Disentangle $= 0.696$, RAVEL Isolation $= 0.736$ (source/model/method configuration for this specific triple not fully disambiguated from the search snippet — flagged below). MDAS is SOTA on Llama2-7B RAVEL per the original paper (exact numeric margin over DAS not yet retrieved).
