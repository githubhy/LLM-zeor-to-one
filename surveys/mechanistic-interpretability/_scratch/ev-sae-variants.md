## Q1: Gated SAEs — decoupling feature detection (gate) from magnitude estimation, the L1-shrinkage/bias problem, and the Pareto result

- **Finding:** Gated SAEs (Rajamanoharan et al., DeepMind, Apr 2024) split the standard SAE encoder into two functional paths — a binary **gating** sub-layer that decides *which* dictionary directions are active, and a separate **magnitude** sub-layer that estimates *how large* each active feature's coefficient is. The L1 sparsity penalty is applied only to the gating path's pre-activations, so the magnitude path is never penalized directly, which removes the systematic under-estimation ("shrinkage") that L1 imposes on ordinary ReLU-SAE activations. Trained with a weight-sharing scheme, a Gated SAE reduces to a single linear encoder with a **JumpReLU** activation, at the cost of only 2×M extra parameters (M = dictionary size) versus a baseline SAE. Across GELU-1L, Pythia-2.8B, and Gemma-7B, Gated SAEs give a **Pareto improvement**: better reconstruction fidelity (loss recovered) at any given sparsity level (L0), or equivalently sparser dictionaries at any given fidelity level.
- **Mechanism / derivation notes:**
  - Standard (ReLU) SAE: encoder pre-activation $\pi(x) = W_{enc}(x-b_{dec})+b_{enc}$; feature activations $f(x)=\mathrm{ReLU}(\pi(x))$; reconstruction $\hat{x}=W_{dec}f(x)+b_{dec}$; loss $=\|x-\hat x\|_2^2+\lambda\|f(x)\|_1$. The L1 term penalizes activation *magnitude* directly, which biases the optimal magnitude estimate downward relative to the magnitude that would minimize reconstruction error alone — this is the "shrinkage" pathology.
  - Gated SAE architecture: two encoder pre-activations sharing input, $\pi_{gate}(x)$ (gating path) and a separate magnitude path. The gate produces a binary mask via a Heaviside step function on $\mathrm{ReLU}(\pi_{gate}(x))$ — i.e., which features are "on." The magnitude path estimates the activation size for features the gate turns on. The L1 penalty is applied only to $\mathrm{ReLU}(\pi_{gate}(x))$ (the gate's rectified pre-activations), not to the final magnitude-weighted activations used for reconstruction.
  - An auxiliary task requires the same rectified gate pre-activations to be independently usable by the decoder to produce a good reconstruction — this ties the gate to be reconstruction-relevant, not just sparsity-relevant.
  - With a weight-sharing/tying scheme between the gate and magnitude paths, the two-path architecture collapses algebraically to a single-layer linear encoder followed by a **JumpReLU** nonlinearity (activation is zero below a learned per-feature threshold, and passes through unchanged above it) — this is the architectural bridge from Gated SAEs to the later JumpReLU SAE line (Q3).
  - Net effect: gradient/penalty pressure for sparsity (via the gate) is decoupled from the pressure to get magnitudes right (via the unconstrained magnitude path) — the paper's central mechanism claim.
- **Values:**
  - Weight-sharing parameter overhead: "only 2 × M more parameters than a baseline SAE" (M = dictionary/feature count). Source: arXiv:2404.16014v1 (HTML).
  - Models evaluated: GELU-1L, Pythia-2.8B, Gemma-7B; sites include MLP outputs, attention outputs, residual stream. A specific reported configuration: Layer 20 of Gemma-7B, SAEs trained with equal compute, with baseline SAEs given 50% more learned features than the Gated SAE — Gated SAE still Pareto-dominates. Source: arXiv:2404.16014 (via search synthesis of paper text; exact quote not independently pulled — see gap below).
  - Submission date: arXiv, first submitted 24 Apr 2024, revised 30 Apr 2024.
- **Sources:**
  - Rajamanoharan, S., Conmy, A., Smith, L., Lieberum, T., Varma, V., Kramár, J., Shah, R., Nanda, N. (2024), "Improving Dictionary Learning with Gated Sparse Autoencoders," arXiv:2404.16014 (https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.16014) | tier=Primary | confidence=H
  - arXiv HTML full text (https://arxiv.org/html/2404.16014v1) | tier=Primary | confidence=H
  - AlignmentForum companion post, "Improving Dictionary Learning with Gated Sparse Autoencoders" (https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/vdoiWXeouGsZEYgrg/improving-dictionary-learning-with-gated-sparse-autoencoders) | tier=Strong-secondary (same authors, blog companion) | confidence=M

## Q2: TopK SAEs (Gao et al. 2024, OpenAI) — exact top-k activation, direct L0 control, scaling laws, probe-based eval, auxiliary loss for dead features

- **Finding:** Gao, Dupré la Tour, Tillman, Goh, Troll, Radford, Sutskever, Leike, and Wu (OpenAI, June 2024) replace the ReLU+L1 SAE objective with a **TopK activation**: the encoder pre-activation is computed as usual, but only the $k$ largest entries are kept and everything else is zeroed, which sets the exact L0 sparsity to $k$ directly (no L1 coefficient to tune) and empirically removes the shrinkage bias that L1 introduces. They train autoencoders at large scale (up to 16M latents) on GPT-2 small and on a family of models sharing the GPT-4 architecture (including GPT-4 itself), and fit clean joint scaling laws for reconstruction loss $L(n,k)$ as a function of autoencoder size $n$ (number of latents) and sparsity $k$. Feature quality is evaluated with per-latent logistic **probes** on 61 curated binary-classification datasets; probe score is non-monotonic in $k$ (rises then falls), and TopK generally beats ReLU on this metric. A separate **auxiliary "AuxK" loss** term uses the top-$k_{aux}$ *dead* latents to model the residual reconstruction error, which keeps the dead-latent rate low (7% at the largest 16M-latent scale) without needing resampling heuristics.
- **Mechanism / derivation notes:**
  - Baseline ReLU SAE: $z=\mathrm{ReLU}(W_{enc}(x-b_{pre})+b_{enc})$, $\hat x = W_{dec}z+b_{pre}$, loss $=\|x-\hat x\|_2^2+\lambda\|z\|_1$.
  - TopK SAE: $z=\mathrm{TopK}(W_{enc}(x-b_{pre}))$ — keep only the $k$ largest coordinates of the pre-activation, zero the rest; no L1 term, no bias-encoder term needed for sparsity control. Because $k$ is set directly, L0 is exactly $k$ per token by construction (contrast with L1-penalized ReLU SAEs, where L0 is only indirectly and noisily controlled via $\lambda$).
  - Because there's no L1 penalty biasing the *surviving* $k$ activations toward zero, TopK avoids the systematic shrinkage that afflicts L1-penalized ReLU SAEs (paper explicitly frames this as a shrinkage fix, same pathology Gated SAEs target from a different angle).
  - Dead-latent auxiliary loss: a latent is flagged "dead" if it hasn't fired for some predetermined number of tokens (typically 10M). The AuxK loss reconstructs the current reconstruction error $e=x-\hat x$ using only the top-$k_{aux}$ dead latents' activations, $\hat e = W_{dec}z_{dead}$, with loss $L_{aux}=\|e-\hat e\|_2^2$; total loss $=L+\alpha L_{aux}$ with $k_{aux}=512,\ \alpha=1/32$ in their setup. This gives dead latents a gradient signal (via being useful for modeling residual error) without changing the main forward pass.
  - Scaling laws: token-budget-to-convergence scales like $\Theta(n^{0.6})$ for GPT-2 small and $\Theta(n^{0.65})$ for GPT-4-family models (n = number of latents); max stable learning rate scales like $1/\sqrt n$. Joint scaling law (their Eq. 3): $L(n,k) = \exp(\alpha+\beta_k\log k+\beta_n \log n+\gamma\log k\log n) + \exp(\zeta+\eta\log k)$, with fitted constants $\alpha=-0.50,\ \beta_k=0.26,\ \beta_n=-0.017,\ \gamma=-0.042,\ \zeta=-1.32,\ \eta=-0.085$. Larger subject (base) models require larger autoencoders to hit the same reconstruction MSE.
  - Probe eval: for each of 61 curated binary-classification datasets, fit a 1-D logistic probe per latent $z_i$ via Newton-Raphson: $\min_{w,b} \mathbb E[y\log\sigma(wz_i+b)+(1-y)\log(1-\sigma(wz_i+b))]$; take the best-scoring latent per task as the feature's probe score. Used as a proxy for "does some single latent linearly encode this human-interpretable concept."
- **Values:**
  - $k_{aux}=512$, $\alpha=1/32$ (AuxK loss weight). Dead-latent threshold: ~10M tokens without firing. Result: "even in our largest (16 million latent) autoencoder only 7% of latents are dead." Quote from arXiv:2406.04093 (HTML fetch).
  - Scaling exponents: token-to-convergence $\Theta(n^{0.6})$ (GPT-2 small), $\Theta(n^{0.65})$ (GPT-4 family); LR $\propto 1/\sqrt n$. Joint law Eq. 3 constants as listed above (Section 3.1.2 / Eq. 3 of the paper).
  - Scale: GPT-2 small SAEs trained on layer-8 (3/4 depth) residual stream, 768-dim; GPT-4-family runs up to a 16M-latent autoencoder trained on 40B tokens; context length 64 tokens for all experiments.
  - Probe eval: 61 curated binary-classification datasets; probe score rises then falls as $k$ increases; TopK "generally achieves better probe scores than ReLU."
- **Sources:**
  - Gao, L., Dupré la Tour, T., Tillman, H., Goh, G., Troll, R., Radford, A., Sutskever, I., Leike, J., Wu, J. (2024), "Scaling and evaluating sparse autoencoders," arXiv:2406.04093 (https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.04093); full text fetched at https://arxiv.org/html/2406.04093v1 | tier=Primary | confidence=H
  - OpenAI CDN copy of the same paper (https://cdn.openai.com/papers/sparse-autoencoders.pdf) | tier=Primary | confidence=H
  - OpenReview listing (https://openreview.net/forum?id=tcsZt9ZNKD) — used only to confirm venue/authorship, not for numeric content | tier=Strong-secondary | confidence=M
- **Gaps/caveats:** All numeric values above (AuxK constants, scaling exponents, joint-law coefficients, dead-latent %, probe dataset count, token/context scale) came from a single WebFetch summarization of the arXiv HTML source rather than direct visual inspection of the PDF's equation/table typesetting — high confidence given the fetch quoted specific section numbers (2.3, 3.1.2, Eq. 3, 4.2, A.2, 5.1), but a synthesizer citing the joint scaling-law constants verbatim in the survey should spot-check them against the PDF equation directly, since OCR/summarization of a dense multi-parameter equation is exactly the kind of case citation-integrity flags as needing re-verification before a load-bearing derivation depends on it.

## Q3: JumpReLU SAEs (threshold activation + STE training); plus BatchTopK and Matryoshka SAEs (one line each)

- **Finding:** JumpReLU SAEs (Rajamanoharan, Lieberum, Sonnerat, Conmy, Varma, Kramár, Nanda; DeepMind, July 2024) replace the ReLU nonlinearity with a **JumpReLU**: a per-feature learned threshold $\theta$ below which the pre-activation is zeroed and above which it passes through unchanged (no compression of the surviving value, unlike ReLU+L1). Because the threshold introduces a discontinuity (a "jump"), it is not differentiable in the ordinary sense, so the model is trained with **straight-through estimators (STEs)**: the forward pass uses the true discontinuous JumpReLU/Heaviside, while the backward pass substitutes a smooth pseudo-derivative (interpretable as a kernel-density estimate of the true gradient) that gives a nonzero gradient signal to $\theta$ whenever the pre-activation falls within a small window $\varepsilon$ of the threshold. Trained directly against an L0 sparsity target (via the Heaviside-counted-active-features term) rather than an L1 proxy, JumpReLU SAEs match or exceed Gated and TopK SAEs on the reconstruction-fidelity/sparsity frontier on Gemma 2 9B. **BatchTopK** (Bussmann, Leask, Nanda, Dec 2024) relaxes TopK's *per-sample* fixed-k constraint to a *batch-level* budget — the same total number of active latents is enforced across a batch on average, but individual tokens can use more or fewer, letting the model allocate more latents to "harder" tokens — and empirically outperforms TopK while being comparable to JumpReLU. **Matryoshka SAEs** (Bussmann et al., Mar 2025) train several nested dictionary prefixes simultaneously (each prefix size must reconstruct the input using only its own first $m_i$ latents), which forces early/small-prefix latents to capture broad, high-frequency concepts and later latents to capture specific refinements — this directly targets the feature-absorption/splitting pathology (Q4) that plagues single-dictionary SAEs, at some reconstruction-performance cost relative to non-hierarchical SAEs.
- **Mechanism / derivation notes:**
  - JumpReLU: $z=\mathrm{JumpReLU}_\theta(\pi(x)) = \pi(x)\odot H(\pi(x)-\theta)$, where $\pi(x)=W_{enc}(x-b_{dec})+b_{enc}$ is the pre-activation, $H$ is the Heaviside step function, $\theta$ a learned positive per-feature threshold vector, and $\odot$ elementwise product. Below threshold the output is exactly 0; above threshold the *raw* pre-activation value passes through (no shrinkage, unlike L1-penalized ReLU where the penalty biases the surviving value downward).
  - Loss = reconstruction term $\|x-\hat x\|_2^2$ + sparsity term $\lambda \sum_i H(\pi_i(x)-\theta_i)$ (i.e., directly penalizing L0 — the count of active features — rather than an L1 magnitude proxy).
  - Training difficulty: both $H(\cdot)$ (in the L0 penalty) and the JumpReLU gate itself have a derivative that is a Dirac delta at the threshold — zero almost everywhere, undefined at $\theta$ — so vanilla backprop gives no useful gradient to $\theta$. Fix: STEs substitute a smooth pseudo-derivative for the backward pass only, active within a window of half-width $\varepsilon$ around the threshold; this pseudo-derivative is shown to correspond to a kernel density estimator (KDE) of the true gradient density, using a kernel $K$ satisfying centered/positive-variance PDF properties.
  - BatchTopK: instead of TopK's per-token top-$k$ selection, select the top-$(k\times\text{batch size})$ activations pooled across the whole batch, then route them back to their originating tokens — this is a soft relaxation of "exactly $k$ active features per token" to "on average $k$ active features per token, with the flexibility to spend more on hard tokens and less on easy ones."
  - Matryoshka: define nested prefix sizes $m_1<m_2<\dots<m_L=M$ (M = full dictionary size); for each prefix, compute a reconstruction using only its first $m_i$ latents and include that reconstruction's loss in the total objective. This forces the shared early latents to be independently sufficient for coarse reconstruction (broad/high-frequency concepts), leaving later latents free to specialize without being incentivized to "absorb" a general feature into a specific one (the failure mode formalized in Q4's feature-absorption discussion).
- **Values:**
  - JumpReLU eval: SAEs trained on the residual stream after layers 9, 20, and 31 of Gemma 2 9B; metrics = delta LM loss and fraction of variance unexplained (FVU); feature-frequency histograms compared at matched L0 ≈ 70 across JumpReLU, TopK, and Gated. Qualitative result: "at any given level of sparsity, JumpReLU SAEs consistently provide more faithful reconstructions than Gated SAEs, and ... reconstructions that are at least as good as, and often slightly better than, TopK SAEs." arXiv submitted 19 Jul 2024.
  - BatchTopK: arXiv:2412.06410 (Bussmann, Leask, Nanda), Dec 2024 — "outperform TopK SAEs while being comparable to JumpReLU SAEs" (qualitative; exact benchmark numbers not pulled within search budget).
  - Matryoshka: arXiv:2503.17547 (Bussmann et al.), Mar 2025 — evaluated on Gemma-2-2B and TinyStories, and in SAEBench on Gemma-2-2B; reported to achieve "competitive reconstruction performance while substantially reducing feature absorption, feature splitting, and feature composition relative to other SAE architectures" (qualitative; exact SAEBench score deltas not pulled within search budget).
- **Sources:**
  - Rajamanoharan, S., Lieberum, T., Sonnerat, N., Conmy, A., Varma, V., Kramár, J., Nanda, N. (2024), "Jumping Ahead: Improving Reconstruction Fidelity with JumpReLU Sparse Autoencoders," arXiv:2407.14435 (https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.14435) | tier=Primary | confidence=H
  - AlignmentForum companion post, "JumpReLU SAEs + Early Access to Gemma 2 SAEs" (https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/wZqqQysfLrt2CFx4T/jumprelu-saes-early-access-to-gemma-2-saes) | tier=Strong-secondary | confidence=M
  - Bussmann, B., Leask, P., Nanda, N. (2024), "BatchTopK Sparse Autoencoders," arXiv:2412.06410 (https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.06410 / ar5iv: https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2412.06410) | tier=Primary | confidence=H (mechanism), M (no exact numbers pulled)

## Q4: SAE pathologies — feature splitting, feature absorption, dead/dense features, activation shrinkage from L1, and "dark matter" / unexplained variance

- **Finding:** SAEs exhibit five distinct, well-documented failure modes. (1) **Feature splitting** (Bricken, Templeton et al., Anthropic "Towards Monosemanticity," Oct 2023): as dictionary size grows, a single coarse feature progressively splits into several more specific children (e.g., one Base64-token feature in a 512-feature dictionary becomes three more specific Base64-related features in a 4,096-feature dictionary) — different dictionary sizes give different "resolutions" of the same underlying concept, with no single size being canonically "correct." (2) **Feature absorption** (Chanin, Wilken-Smith, Dulka, Bhatnagar, Bloom, Sept 2024, "A is for Absorption," arXiv:2409.14507): when features form a hierarchy (e.g., a general "starts with letter A" feature and specific child features like "apple," "ant"), sparsity pressure causes the SAE to let the specific child feature silently absorb the general parent feature's job — the general feature fails to fire on cases it logically should, because the sparser/child feature covers it instead — making seemingly monosemantic high-level features unreliable detectors. (3) **Dead and dense features**: dead features never fire (get no gradient, permanently wasting capacity and slowing/complicating training), while dense (ultra-high-frequency) features fire almost always and are usually uninterpretable "leftover" directions rather than genuine sparse concepts; standard mitigations are resampling dead neurons, "ghost gradients" (an auxiliary loss term that gives dead features a gradient toward fitting the residual error), and initializing the decoder bias at the geometric median of the data. (4) **Activation shrinkage from L1**: the L1 sparsity penalty is a magnitude penalty, so at the optimum the surviving nonzero activations are systematically biased downward (shrunk) relative to the values that would minimize reconstruction error alone — the same problem Gated SAEs (Q1), TopK (Q2), and JumpReLU (Q3) were each explicitly designed to avoid via a mechanism that decouples the sparsity signal from the surviving-activation magnitude. (5) **"Dark matter"/unexplained variance** (Engels, Riggs, Tegmark, Oct 2024, arXiv:2410.14670): even well-trained SAEs leave a substantial reconstruction error that is not noise — roughly half of the error *vector* and >90% of its *norm* is linearly predictable from the original activation itself, and error-norm scaling behavior across SAE sizes is itself predictable (larger SAEs tend to fail on the same contexts smaller SAEs fail on) — meaning a chunk of "residual error" is actually a systematic, structured signal the SAE's sparse dictionary is failing to capture, not simple stochastic noise.
- **Mechanism / derivation notes:**
  - Feature splitting: for a fixed underlying data-generating hierarchy of concepts, increasing SAE width $M$ increases the "resolution" at which the dictionary can carve up feature space; a parent concept direction present at small $M$ is replaced by several more specific directions at larger $M$ that jointly span (approximately) the same subspace/role. This is presented as a property of dictionary learning under L1 sparsity applied to hierarchically structured underlying features, not a bug unique to one training run — it recurs across autoencoder widths in the same base model.
  - Feature absorption: formalized as a failure of the natural expectation that a "high-level" feature (e.g., "starts with A") should fire whenever any of its natural sub-cases do. Empirically, the paper shows precision/recall of a first-letter feature is highly sensitive to the sparsity level L0: low-L0 (very sparse) SAEs tend to learn high-precision/low-recall latents (they under-fire, i.e. get absorbed into children) while high-L0 SAEs learn low-precision/high-recall latents. Mechanistically, absorption is attributed to the same sparsity objective that drives feature splitting — when a more specific feature can explain a token's activation "for free" (it's already needed to fire for other reasons), the SAE's sparsity penalty rewards not also firing the more general/redundant parent feature, at the parent feature's reliability's expense.
  - Dead features: a feature whose ReLU pre-activation is $\le 0$ for every training example gets no gradient (ReLU's derivative is zero there) and never recovers on its own — this is the SAE analogue of classic ReLU-network "dying neuron" pathology. Ghost gradients add an auxiliary loss that gives dead latents a gradient signal proportional to how well they could explain the current residual reconstruction error, without perturbing the main forward pass (directly analogous in spirit to the Q2 TopK "AuxK" auxiliary loss, though from an earlier ReLU-SAE-training lineage). Dense features are the opposite failure: a latent active on nearly every input, which by definition cannot be a sparse, specific concept, and typically indicates an under-trained or degenerate direction (e.g., a norm/scale direction) that the sparsity objective failed to suppress.
  - L1 shrinkage: for the ReLU+L1 SAE objective $\|x-\hat x\|_2^2+\lambda\|z\|_1$, the L1 term's subgradient with respect to an active $z_i$ is a constant $\lambda$ regardless of $z_i$'s size, which (via the stationarity condition at the optimum) pulls every active feature's optimal magnitude below the magnitude that would purely minimize squared reconstruction error — a soft-thresholding-style bias familiar from LASSO regression, here manifesting as systematically low activation values, hence "shrinkage." This is the common thread that Gated (Q1), TopK (Q2), and JumpReLU (Q3) all cite as their explicit target pathology, each solving it by making the L1-style sparsity signal act on a *different* quantity (a gate pre-activation, an index selection, a threshold decision) than the one used for the final reconstruction magnitude.
  - Dark matter: decompose an SAE's reconstruction error $e=x-\hat x$ into a component linearly predictable from $x$ (via a learned linear map) and a residual; the paper finds the predictable component captures ~half the error vector and the large majority of its norm, meaning $e$ is not simply small isotropic noise the dictionary failed to capture by chance, but substantially a *structured*, linearly-recoverable signal — implying the SAE's sparse+nonlinear encoding is failing to represent directions of variation that a linear probe could already see.
- **Values:**
  - Feature splitting: 1× to 256× expansion factors relative to the 512-neuron MLP layer (i.e., 512 to 131,072 features), trained on 8B data points; detailed analysis focused on the 4,096-feature run "A/1"; one Base64-related feature in the "A/0" (smaller) dictionary "splits into three" more specific Base64-related features in "A/1." Quote/paraphrase from search synthesis of transformer-circuits.pub/2023/monosemantic-features.
  - Feature absorption: paper is arXiv:2409.14507, Sept 2024; key qualitative claim — first-letter-feature precision/recall trades off directly with the SAE's L0 sparsity setting (low L0 → high-precision/low-recall; high L0 → low-precision/high-recall); a specific noted caveat is that past layer 17 in Gemma-2-2B, attention has already moved the starting-letter information to the final token position, complicating absorption measurement at deeper layers.
  - Dark matter: "about half of the error vector itself and >90% of its norm" is linearly predictable from the initial activation vector (arXiv:2410.14670, Engels/Riggs/Tegmark, Oct 2024); error-norm scaling across autoencoder sizes described as "remarkably predictable."
