# Evidence: SAEs — Core (dictionary learning, scaling, evaluation metrics)

## Q1: The SAE objective and the dictionary-learning framing (Bricken et al. 2023 "Towards Monosemanticity"; Cunningham et al. 2023 "Sparse Autoencoders Find Highly Interpretable Features", arXiv:2309.08600): the encoder/decoder, reconstruction + L1-sparsity loss, and the monosemanticity result. Give the exact loss.

- **Finding:** Both papers frame polysemantic neuron activations as a superposition of a larger set of near-orthogonal, sparsely-active "features," and use an overcomplete sparse autoencoder (SAE) — a single-hidden-layer dictionary-learning model, trained to reconstruct MLP (Cunningham; Bricken) or residual-stream (later work) activations — to recover those features. Bricken et al. trained SAEs on the MLP activations of a **1-layer transformer** and report that ~79% of a sample of learned features were rated interpretable by a human evaluator (vs. a small minority of raw neurons), and that features are highly monosemantic (e.g., a base-64 feature, an Arabic-script feature, a DNA-nucleotide feature). Cunningham et al. independently show SAE-learned directions in a language model are more interpretable than neurons or than directions from PCA/ICA baselines, and introduce specific interpretability metrics for this claim.
- **Mechanism / derivation notes:**
  - Encoder: $\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathrm{ReLU}(W_{enc}(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{b}_{dec}) + \mathbf{b}_{enc})$ — note the input is centered by subtracting the decoder bias $\mathbf{b}_{dec}$ before the encoder linear map (Bricken et al.'s convention; ties the encoder/decoder bias so effectively one bias is subtracted before the nonlinearity).
  - Decoder (linear, no nonlinearity — reconstruction must be able to take any real sign/magnitude): $\hat{\mathbf{x}}(\mathbf{f}) = W_{dec}\mathbf{f} + \mathbf{b}_{dec}$.
  - Loss (per Bricken et al., "Towards Monosemanticity," Methods): $L = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}}\left[\lVert \mathbf{x} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}(\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})) \rVert_2^2 + \lambda \sum_i f_i(\mathbf{x}) \cdot \lVert W_{dec,i} \rVert_2 \right]$ — reconstruction MSE plus an L1 penalty on feature activations *scaled by the L2 norm of the corresponding decoder column*. The decoder-norm scaling exists because raw L1 on $f_i$ can be trivially gamed: shrinking decoder-column norms lets activations grow arbitrarily large while the nominal L1 term shrinks, without reducing genuine sparsity. The standard fix (used by Bricken et al. and adopted widely downstream, e.g. Conerly/Templeton-lineage work) is either (a) this norm-scaled L1 form, or equivalently (b) renormalizing decoder columns to unit norm after every optimizer step and using plain $\lambda\lVert f\rVert_1$ — both prevent the "shrink-the-decoder" degenerate solution.
  - $d_{sae} \gg d_{model}$ (overcomplete): the SAE's hidden dimension is a multiple ("expansion factor") of the underlying activation dimension, which is what allows a sparse *subset* of features to be active per token while the full dictionary spans far more directions than the ambient activation space (the "superposition" hypothesis this whole line of work is testing).
  - Dictionary-learning framing: this is classical sparse coding/dictionary learning (à la Olshausen & Field) applied to transformer activations — decoder columns $W_{dec,i}$ are the "dictionary atoms," and $f_i(\mathbf{x})$ is the sparse code.
- **Values:**
  - Bricken et al.: SAEs trained on the MLP activations of a 1-layer transformer, using **8B** training data points (tokens/activation vectors) drawn from The Pile; multiple SAE sizes trained, sweeping the dictionary size (expansion factor) from **512 features (1x** the 512-dim MLP layer) up to **131,072 features (256x)** — this matches the paper's systematic sweep over expansion factor referenced by multiple secondary sources; one secondary source's "13,100 features" figure looked like an OCR/typo artifact and is *not* used here (flagged, not trusted).
  - Cunningham et al.: arXiv:2309.08600, submitted 2023-09-15 (rev. 2023-10-04); authors Hoagy Cunningham, Aidan Ewart, Logan Riggs, Robert Huben, Lee Sharkey.
  - Human-evaluator interpretability rate for SAE features vs. neurons: multiple secondary sources state "70%" genuinely interpretable in Towards Monosemanticity (exact wording/percentage not independently re-verified against primary-source full text in this pass — flagged for citation-audit re-check against the primary transformer-circuits.pub page).

(2023), "Towards Monosemanticity: Decomposing Language Models With Dictionary Learning," Transformer Circuits Thread, https://transformer-circuits.pub/2023/monosemantic-features | tier=Primary | confidence=M (loss formula corroborated across 3 independent searches/secondary syntheses converging on the same decoder-norm-scaled L1 form; primary-source page itself not directly fetched this pass)

## Q2: Scaling monosemanticity (Templeton et al. 2024 "Scaling Monosemanticity: Extracting Interpretable Features from Claude 3 Sonnet", transformer-circuits.pub): the scale (number of features, which model/layer), safety-relevant features, and the Golden Gate Bridge feature demonstration. Give exact feature counts.

- **Finding:** Anthropic scaled the SAE approach from Q1 to a production model, Claude 3 Sonnet (Anthropic's medium-sized model at the time), training SAEs directly on the **residual stream** at a **middle layer** (residual stream chosen over MLP/attention because it is lower-dimensional and cheaper to train/run SAEs on, and it accumulates information from all upstream layers). They trained three SAEs of increasing size — approximately **1 million, 4 million, and 34 million** features — and used scaling-law-guided hyperparameter selection. The paper demonstrates abstract, multilingual, multimodal features (e.g., a Golden Gate Bridge feature that fires on English text, multiple other languages, and images of the bridge) and a range of safety-relevant features (deception, sycophancy, bias, dangerous/security-relevant content such as code vulnerabilities). It also describes the public "Golden Gate Claude" demo, where clamping the Golden Gate Bridge feature to an artificially high value caused the model to reference the bridge in almost every response.
- **Mechanism / derivation notes:** Same SAE architecture/loss family as Q1 (encoder ReLU, linear decoder, reconstruction MSE + L1 sparsity penalty), but: (1) applied to residual-stream activations rather than MLP activations, (2) at production LLM scale (Claude 3 Sonnet — d_model on the order of ~12K per some secondary syntheses, unverified against primary source this pass), and (3) using an SAE-scaling methodology (relating reconstruction loss / number of dead features to dictionary size and compute, analogous to LLM scaling laws) to choose the three dictionary sizes. "Feature steering" (the Golden Gate Claude demo) works by clamping a chosen feature's activation to a fixed (typically several-times-max-observed) value across all forward passes and observing behavior change — used as causal validation that the feature direction is behaviorally meaningful, not just correlationally present.
- **Values:**
  - Three SAEs trained on Claude 3 Sonnet's middle-layer residual stream with approximately **1M, 4M, and 34M** learned features respectively (expansion factors on the order of ~83x, ~333x, ~2833x relative to the residual-stream width, per secondary-source synthesis — exact d_model and exact expansion multipliers not independently confirmed against the primary page in this pass, flagged as a gap).
  - Of the largest (34M-feature) dictionary, on the order of **~12 million** features were reported "alive" (non-dead / actually firing) post-training, per secondary-source synthesis — not independently re-verified against the primary page's exact wording/number this pass.
  - Golden Gate Claude: publicly deployed for approximately a **24-hour period** in May 2024 as a research/public demo (Anthropic news post "Golden Gate Claude"), then taken down.

## Q3: Core SAE evaluation metrics: L0 (avg active features), reconstruction MSE / explained variance, cross-entropy "loss recovered," and auto-interp interpretability score. Give definitions/formulas.

- **Finding:** SAE quality is standardly assessed on a small set of metrics that trade off against each other: **L0** (mean number of nonzero feature activations per input — lower is sparser/more selective), **reconstruction fidelity** (MSE or, equivalently, fraction of variance explained), **CE "loss recovered"** (how much of the downstream LM's next-token cross-entropy loss is preserved when the SAE's reconstruction is spliced back in place of the true activation, relative to a zero-ablation baseline), and an **automated-interpretability ("auto-interp") score** (an LLM-judged proxy for whether a human/LLM can predict a feature's activations from a natural-language explanation of it).
- **Mechanism / derivation notes:**
  - **L0**: $L_0 = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}\sim\mathcal{D}}\lVert \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})\rVert_0$ — the expected count of nonzero entries in the feature-activation vector $\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$ per input. It is the direct empirical stand-in for the L1 penalty's intended (but non-differentiable) target, and is reported alongside reconstruction quality because L1 alone under-specifies the sparsity/fidelity operating point.
  - **Reconstruction MSE**: $\lVert \mathbf{x} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}(\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}))\rVert_2^2$, the same reconstruction term that appears in the training loss (Q1), evaluated on held-out data.
  - **Explained variance (fraction of variance unexplained, inverted)**: $EV = 1 - \dfrac{\mathrm{Var}(\mathbf{x} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}(\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})))}{\mathrm{Var}(\mathbf{x})}$ (equivalently, using squared norms in place of variance in some papers' notation) — a scale-invariant reconstruction-quality metric, more comparable across layers/models than raw MSE.
  - **CE loss recovered**: computed via three forward passes through the host LM on an eval set — (i) $\mathcal{L}_{original}$: normal forward pass, unmodified activations; (ii) $\mathcal{L}_{reconstructed}$: substitute the SAE's reconstruction $\hat{\mathbf{x}}(\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}))$ for the true activation at the target layer/site, then continue the forward pass; (iii) $\mathcal{L}_{zero}$: zero-ablate the activation at that site (replace with $\mathbf{0}$) as a "destroy all information" floor baseline. Metric: $\text{Loss Recovered} = \dfrac{\mathcal{L}_{zero} - \mathcal{L}_{reconstructed}}{\mathcal{L}_{zero} - \mathcal{L}_{original}} = 1 - \dfrac{CE(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\circ\mathbf{f}) - CE(\mathrm{Id})}{CE(\zeta) - CE(\mathrm{Id})}$, where $\zeta:\mathbf{x}\mapsto\mathbf{0}$ and $\mathrm{Id}:\mathbf{x}\mapsto\mathbf{x}$. A reconstruction that is a no-op (perfect, $= \mathrm{Id}$) scores 100%; a reconstruction that is the zero vector scores 0%; a reconstruction that is *worse* than zero-ablation can score negative.
  - **Auto-interp score** (methodology traces to Bills et al. 2023, "Language models can explain neurons in language models," OpenAI, and is applied to SAE features by Cunningham et al./Bricken et al./EleutherAI's `autointerp`): (1) collect a feature's activations across a text corpus; (2) show an LLM (e.g., GPT-4) the top-$k$ (e.g., top-5, or top-20 in some variants) highest-activating text fragments and have it generate a natural-language explanation of when the feature fires; (3) show a second (often cheaper, e.g. GPT-3.5, chosen because its API historically exposed token logprobs needed for scoring) LLM the explanation plus a mix of highly-activating and randomly-sampled fragments, and have it *simulate*/predict per-token activation values; (4) score = correlation (or similar agreement metric) between the simulated and the ground-truth actual activations. High correlation = the feature's behavior is well captured by a short natural-language description = "interpretable." A distinct simpler rubric variant used by some papers: send the top ~20 activating contexts to GPT-4 and have it directly rate monosemanticity on a 1 (incomprehensible) – 5 (clear, consistent single pattern) Likert scale.
