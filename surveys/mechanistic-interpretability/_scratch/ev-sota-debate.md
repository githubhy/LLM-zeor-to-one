## Q1: What frontier labs actually do now (2024-2025) — dominant practice per stage (find features -> build circuits -> validate -> apply)?

- **Finding:** Anthropic's pipeline moved SAE (per-layer dictionary learning) -> crosscoders (shared features across layers/models) -> cross-layer transcoders (CLTs) as the "replacement model" substrate for attribution graphs, which are now the primary circuit-tracing artifact applied to production models (Claude 3.5 Haiku). Google DeepMind's dominant practice is: train a large open JumpReLU-SAE suite (Gemma Scope) as the feature-finding stage, and separately use ACDC / attribution-patching-style methods for circuit discovery on small models. OpenAI's stage-1 (find features) is TopK-SAE at large scale (up to 16M latents on GPT-4) plus GPT-4-driven auto-interp for validation/labeling. EleutherAI supplies cross-lab tooling: the Tuned Lens (a per-layer affine-probe upgrade to the logit lens, stage: validate/probe) and Delphi (open-source auto-interp pipeline for SAE/transcoder features, stage: validate), used to interpret features found by others' SAEs at far lower cost than closed-model auto-interp.
- **Mechanism / derivation notes:**
  - **Find features:** dictionary learning on residual-stream/MLP activations, $\mathbf{x} \approx \mathbf{b} + \sum_i f_i(\mathbf{x})\,\mathbf{d}_i$, with an activation function enforcing sparsity — ReLU+L1 (original SAEs), TopK (OpenAI, hard-selects $k$ largest pre-activations, removes L1 shrinkage bias), or JumpReLU (DeepMind/Gemma Scope, a learned per-feature threshold shifting the sparsity penalty off the reconstruction gradient).
  - **Crosscoders / CLTs (Anthropic):** instead of one SAE per layer, a single dictionary reads from / writes to activations at multiple layers (or multiple models) simultaneously, so a feature persisting across layers is represented once instead of once-per-layer duplicated as a near-identity "circuit" edge — this directly simplifies attribution graphs by collapsing spurious cross-layer copy edges.
  - **Build circuits:** (a) attribution graphs — replace attention+MLP nonlinearities with the learned sparse (crosscoder/CLT) features, then compute linear/local attributions between features across layers to build an interpretable causal graph of a specific forward pass; (b) ACDC — iterative greedy edge-pruning over the model's computational (attention-head/MLP-node) graph using activation patching, thresholding on task-metric degradation; (c) Edge Attribution Patching (EAP) — a linear-approximation of activation patching using one forward + one backward pass per edge (gradient x activation-diff), used as a cheap pre-filter before ACDC.
  - **Validate:** auto-interp (OpenAI/Delphi) — an LLM (GPT-4 or open model via Delphi) is shown top-activating text spans for a feature, generates a natural-language explanation, then a second LLM pass "simulates" predicted activation for held-out text and correlates against real activations to score explanation fidelity. Tuned Lens (EleutherAI) trains an affine map per layer (not just reusing the final unembedding) to decode intermediate residual-stream states into vocabulary distributions, correcting for representational rotation/shift across layers that breaks the plain logit lens.
  - **Apply:** attribution graphs applied to real Claude 3.5 Haiku behaviors (poem planning: model pre-selects a rhyming/semantic target word then plans backward to construct the line) — an applied case study, not just a method demo.
- **Values:**
  - OpenAI TopK SAE: up to 16M-feature autoencoder trained on GPT-4 activations. Source: arXiv:2406.04093.
  - Gemma Scope: "more than 400 sparse autoencoders" across Gemma 2 2B/9B (all layers/sublayers) and select Gemma 2 27B layers, "more than 30 million learned features in total," trained on 4-16B tokens each; used "over 20% of the training compute of GPT-3" and saved "about 20 Pebibytes (PiB) of activations to disk." Source: arXiv:2408.05147.
  - Delphi cost comparison: auto-interpreting 1.5M GPT-2 features costs ~USD 1,300 (Llama 3.1 via API) or ~USD 8,500 (Claude 3.5 Sonnet) with the Delphi pipeline, vs. ~USD 200k with prior (OpenAI-style, presumably GPT-4-driven) methods. Source: EleutherAI blog, blog.eleuther.ai/autointerp/.
  - Attribution graphs paper: published March 2025 ("Circuit Tracing: Revealing Computational Graphs in Language Models" per a secondary summary; primary is transformer-circuits.pub/2025/attribution-graphs/). Applied to Claude 3.5 Haiku.
  - Crosscoders paper: published Oct 25, 2024 at transformer-circuits.pub/2024/crosscoders/index.html (confirmed via Anthropic's own Twitter/X announcement dated same window).
  - EAP vs ACDC: EAP requires "only two forward passes and one backward pass" total (vs. ACDC's per-iteration independent inference over the whole graph); EAP alone outperforms ACDC on speed, but running ACDC on EAP's pre-pruned subgraph improves final identification — per Syed et al., arXiv:2310.10348.

## Q2: The SAE debate (2024-2025) — critiques (SAEs vs. simple baselines on downstream tasks/probing), matching-pursuit / end-to-end alternatives, and the argued shift to transcoders and attribution graphs.

- **Finding:** Two 2025 papers — AxBench (Wu et al., Stanford, arXiv:2501.17148) and "Are Sparse Autoencoders Useful? A Case Study in Sparse Probing" (Nanda-affiliated DeepMind interp team, arXiv:2502.16681) — empirically show SAE-derived features lose to simple baselines (prompting, difference-in-means probing, fine-tuning) on steering and concept-detection/probing tasks. This directly triggered a public "deprioritization" announcement from the Google DeepMind mechanistic-interpretability team (Neel Nanda et al., "Negative Results for Sparse Autoencoders on Downstream Tasks and Deprioritising SAE Research," 2025, cross-posted DeepMind Safety Research Medium + AlignmentForum). In parallel, a methods-critique strand argues shallow ReLU/TopK/JumpReLU SAEs mis-model the true (hierarchical, nonlinear, multi-dimensional) feature geometry, motivating matching-pursuit-style unrolled sparse coding (MP-SAE, "From Flat to Hierarchical," Costa et al., NeurIPS 2025, arXiv:2506.03093) as an alternative encoder. A separate strand argues **transcoders** (input->output function approximators per MLP/attention sublayer, rather than same-layer activation reconstructors) are both more interpretable and the natural substrate for circuit tracing ("Transcoders Beat Sparse Autoencoders for Interpretability," Paulo, Shabalin et al., arXiv:2501.18823) — this is the same transcoder mechanism Anthropic's attribution graphs use (cross-layer transcoders / CLTs), so the "shift to transcoders + attribution graphs" is a convergent argument from two directions: empirical interpretability-metric superiority (Paulo & Shabalin) and practical circuit-tracing utility (Anthropic).
- **Mechanism / derivation notes:**
  - **Why SAEs can lose to baselines:** an SAE optimizes reconstruction + sparsity of a static activation snapshot, an objective decoupled from the actual downstream task (probing/steering) it is later repurposed for; a simple supervised difference-in-means direction or a linear probe is fit directly on the task signal, so on in-distribution axes it need not beat the SAE, but the SAE's advantage (unsupervised concept discovery) does not transfer to a task where labeled contrast pairs already exist. AxBench's finding is specifically that for steering, plain prompting/fine-tuning beats all representation-editing methods including SAEs; for concept detection, difference-in-means beats SAEs.
  - **Matching pursuit SAEs:** replace the single-shot linear encoder $\mathbf{f} = \mathrm{TopK}(W_{\text{enc}}\mathbf{x})$ with an iterative greedy residual-pursuit loop — at each step, select the dictionary atom most correlated with the current residual, subtract its contribution, repeat — so early-selected atoms capture coarse/high-level structure and later ones refine detail, yielding a naturally hierarchical (non-flat) code rather than one flat simultaneous top-k selection.
  - **Transcoders vs. SAEs:** an SAE reconstructs a layer's own activations ($\hat{\mathbf{x}} \approx \mathbf{x}$ at the same point in the network), so it does not model the sublayer's actual function; a transcoder instead approximates a component's input-output map ($\hat{\mathbf{y}} \approx \mathrm{MLP}(\mathbf{x})$, i.e. reconstructs the *output* given the *input*), which both sparsifies the computation itself and makes the learned features directly composable into a causal circuit — this is why Anthropic's cross-layer transcoders (CLTs) are the substrate for attribution graphs rather than plain per-layer SAEs.
- **Values:**
  - AxBench: for steering, "prompting outperforms all existing methods, followed by finetuning"; for concept detection, "difference-in-means" is best; "on both evaluations, SAEs are not competitive." Models: Gemma-2-2B and 9B. Source: arXiv:2501.17148 (Wu, Arora, Geiger, Wang, Huang, Jurafsky, Manning, Potts; ICLR 2025 Spotlight per a GitHub paper-notes secondary source).
  - DeepMind deprioritization post: title "Negative Results for Sparse Autoencoders on Downstream Tasks and Deprioritising SAE Research (Mechanistic Interpretability Team Progress Update)," framed as "a list of snippets about research from the DeepMind mechanistic interpretability team that didn't fit a traditional paper format," specifically investigating SAE usefulness for out-of-distribution probing (detecting harmful intent in user prompts). Source: deepmindsafetyresearch.medium.com (cross-post), companion arXiv:2502.16681 paper.
  - Transcoders paper: "transcoder features are significantly more interpretable" than SAE features trained on the same model/data (no single quantitative headline number captured from snippets — see gap below). Source: arXiv:2501.18823.
  - Matching pursuit: MP-SAE "yields more expressive representations and naturally constructs a representational hierarchy" vs. shallow SAEs; accepted at NeurIPS 2025. Source: arXiv:2506.03093.
- **Sources:**
  - Wu, Arora, Geiger, Wang, Huang, Jurafsky, Manning, Potts (2025), "AxBench: Steering LLMs? Even Simple Baselines Outperform Sparse Autoencoders," arXiv:2501.17148 | tier=Primary | confidence=H

A Case Study in Sparse Probing," arXiv:2502.16681, + companion post "Negative Results for Sparse Autoencoders on Downstream Tasks and Deprioritising SAE Research," deepmindsafetyresearch.medium.com | tier=Primary | confidence=H

## Q3: Published open SAE suites and their scale/eval conditions (Gemma Scope, OpenAI GPT-4 SAEs, Neuronpedia) — model, layers, number of SAEs/features, sparsity.

- **Finding:** Gemma Scope (Google DeepMind, arXiv:2408.05147) is the largest fully-open SAE suite: JumpReLU SAEs at every layer and sublayer of Gemma 2 2B and 9B (plus select layers of Gemma 2 27B), >400 SAEs total, >30M features, released on HuggingFace under CC-BY-4.0. OpenAI's GPT-4 SAE work (arXiv:2406.04093) is the largest-scale single-model SAE (16M latents on GPT-4) but the weights themselves are not openly released (paper/methodology is open; per Q1, code-level open tooling for this scale is the community's, e.g. EleutherAI's `sae`/Delphi). Neuronpedia is the open hosting/visualization layer that sits on top of both ecosystems (and now others) — it hosts and serves the actual Gemma Scope SAEs interactively and has since expanded to Llama 3.1-8B, GPT2-Small (v5), and (per current 2026 listings) Gemma-4, Qwen 3.5, and OLMo 3 models — i.e., Neuronpedia is infrastructure, not a distinct SAE-training methodology.
- **Mechanism / derivation notes:**
  - Gemma Scope trains SAEs at 3 sites per layer: (1) attention-head outputs before the final linear transform (concatenated per-head), (2) MLP outputs after RMSNorm, (3) the post-MLP residual stream — i.e. it decomposes both the attention and MLP sublayer contributions, not just the residual stream.
  - Width tiers and training-token budget are coupled: SAEs at width $\le 16$K were trained on 4B tokens, widths in the $32$K–$524$K range on 8B tokens, and the 1M-width SAEs on 16B tokens — larger dictionaries get proportionally more training data, consistent with the "feature splitting" phenomenon (narrower-SAE features decompose into more specialized wider-SAE features).
  - Sparsity is controlled via JumpReLU's learned per-feature threshold (not an L1 penalty), and Gemma Scope ships 3 target-L0 tiers per site/width: "small" ($L_0 \approx 10$–20), "medium" ($L_0 \approx 30$–60), "large" ($L_0 \approx 60$–150) — giving users an explicit sparsity/reconstruction-fidelity dial rather than one fixed operating point.
  - OpenAI's k-sparse (TopK) approach fixes $L_0 = k$ exactly by construction (hard top-k selection on the encoder pre-activations) rather than targeting an L0 band via a threshold, which is the main methodological contrast with Gemma Scope's JumpReLU approach.
- **Values:**
  - Gemma Scope: **>400 SAEs**, **>30 million learned features total**, widths **{16k, 32k...65k/131k, 262k, 524k, 1M}** (exact width grid: $\le 16$K / 32K–524K / 1M tiers per the token-budget mapping above; 131K width specifically confirmed as used "at layers 9, 20, and 31" in a follow-up eval study), 3 sites/layer, target L0 bands {10–20, 30–60, 60–150}, trained on {4B, 8B, 16B} tokens respectively by width tier, "over 20% of the training compute of GPT-3," ~20 PiB of activations saved to disk. Models: Gemma 2 2B and 9B (all layers/sublayers), Gemma 2 27B (select layers). License: CC-BY-4.0, hosted on HuggingFace (`google/gemma-scope`). Source: arXiv:2408.05147.
  - Gemma Scope 2 (successor suite, per LessWrong/DeepMind blog "Announcing Gemma Scope 2"): 3 sites/layer (residual stream, MLP output, attention output), widths **{16k, 262k}**, 2 target-L0 tiers; hosted checkpoint example `google/gemma-scope-2-12b-it` on HuggingFace — i.e., the successor suite extends to a 12B-scale instruction-tuned Gemma variant. Source: deepmind.google/blog/gemma-scope-2-..., huggingface.co/google/gemma-scope-2-12b-it.
  - OpenAI GPT-4 SAE: **16 million latents**, trained for **40 billion tokens** on GPT-4 activations; also trained a comparable suite on GPT-2 small for methodology validation; reports "smooth and predictable scaling" of the reconstruction-vs-sparsity (L0) frontier with autoencoder size, better returns to scale than prior (ReLU+L1) SAE training recipes. Source: arXiv:2406.04093; openai.com/index/extracting-concepts-from-gpt-4/.
  - Neuronpedia: hosts Gemma Scope's full suite interactively (assisted DeepMind with the Gemma Scope interactive demo) plus (as of the 2026 snapshot captured here) Llama 3.1-8B, GPT2-Small (v5), Gemma-4, Qwen 3.5, and OLMo 3 SAEs; "browse multiple terabytes of activations, explanations, and metadata"; supports probes, latents/features, custom steering vectors. Source: neuronpedia.org, docs.neuronpedia.org/sparse-autoencoder.
