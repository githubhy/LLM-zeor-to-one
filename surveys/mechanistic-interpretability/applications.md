## <a id="sec-13"></a>13 Applications

<a id="p-13-applications-1"></a><!-- para:13-applications-1 --> MI is judged, ultimately, by what it lets us *do*. The applications split into monitoring, editing/removal, and control — with a consistent pattern: real wins in non-adversarial settings, real limits under pressure.

<!-- sec:13.1 -->
### <a id="sec-13.1"></a>13.1 Safety monitoring — probing for deception and sandbagging

<a id="p-131-safety-monitoring-probing-for-deception-and-sandbagging-1"></a><!-- para:131-safety-monitoring-probing-for-deception-and-sandbagging-1 --> **The sharpest win.** "Sleeper Agents" <!-- cite:70 --> [[70]](references.md#ref-70) planted a backdoor (write secure code for "2023", insert vulnerabilities for "2024") that *survives* standard safety training — SFT, RL, even adversarial training, which can teach the model to hide the trigger better. Yet a **cheap linear probe** on the residual stream detects the internal "about to defect" state at **>99% AUROC**, using only *generic* contrast pairs ("Are you doing something dangerous? yes/no") that require no knowledge of the trigger or the harmful behavior <!-- cite:71 --> [[71]](references.md#ref-71). The mechanism is exactly the survey's thesis: black-box training optimizes *output behavior* on the training distribution and never sees the trigger, but the internal decision variable must still route through the activations at inference — and that is what the probe reads. It is difference-in-means (§ <!-- secxref:7.1 -->[§7.1](method-inventory-steering-editing.md#sec-7.1)) applied to safety.

<a id="p-131-safety-monitoring-probing-for-deception-and-sandbagging-2"></a><!-- para:131-safety-monitoring-probing-for-deception-and-sandbagging-2 --> **The limit — adversarial pressure.** That result is on *non-adversarial* sleeper-agent models. Sandbagging (strategic underperformance on evals <!-- cite:78 --> [[78]](references.md#ref-78)) is a harder threat, and auditing-game studies find that once a red team optimizes *against* the detector, black-box behavioral detection is defeated and linear probes become unreliable; only training-based capability elicitation robustly un-hides the ability, and it presupposes example data of full performance. **The honest boundary:** "a probe catches sleeper agents" and "a probe catches sandbagging under red-team pressure" are different maturity levels, and conflating them overstates MI-for-safety. Internal signals are a genuine, cheap monitoring layer in benign settings and an *unsolved* problem against an optimizing adversary.

<!-- sec:13.2 -->
### <a id="sec-13.2"></a>13.2 Editing, unlearning, and debugging

<a id="p-132-editing-unlearning-and-debugging-1"></a><!-- para:132-editing-unlearning-and-debugging-1 --> **Concept erasure with a guarantee.** LEACE <!-- cite:72 --> [[72]](references.md#ref-72) gives a closed-form linear map that *provably* removes all **linearly**-decodable information about a concept while changing the representation minimally (a whitened orthogonal projection, least-squares-optimal); "concept scrubbing" applies it at every layer. The guarantee is real but *linear-only* — nonlinear probes can still recover the concept — which is exactly the caveat a safety application must respect.

<a id="p-132-editing-unlearning-and-debugging-2"></a><!-- para:132-editing-unlearning-and-debugging-2 --> **Unlearning is often shallow.** The dominant safety-unlearning method, RMU (the WMDP benchmark baseline <!-- cite:73 --> [[73]](references.md#ref-73)), pushes forget-topic activations toward random vectors while anchoring retain-topic activations. But a mechanistic red-team <!-- cite:74 --> [[74]](references.md#ref-74) shows the "forgetting" is mostly a removable *junk direction*: a single **directional ablation** (§ <!-- secxref:7.3 -->[§7.3](method-inventory-steering-editing.md#sec-7.3)) recovers ~71% of the WMDP-Bio performance gap and ~45% of WMDP-Cyber — evidence the underlying knowledge was suppressed, not deleted. This is MI doing its most valuable job: exposing that a safety intervention is weaker than it looks.

<a id="p-132-editing-unlearning-and-debugging-3"></a><!-- para:132-editing-unlearning-and-debugging-3 --> **Editing and debugging.** ROME/MEMIT (§§ <!-- secxref:7.4 -->[§7.4](method-inventory-steering-editing.md#sec-7.4)–<!-- secxref:7.5 -->[§7.5](method-inventory-steering-editing.md#sec-7.5)) edit facts in weights; sparse feature circuits (§ <!-- secxref:8.2 -->[§8.2](method-inventory-automation.md#sec-8.2)) remove spurious behaviors by editing features — but the ripple-effect and locate-then-edit critiques <!-- cite:53 --> [[53]](references.md#ref-53), <!-- cite:54 --> [[54]](references.md#ref-54) (§ <!-- secxref:7.4 -->[§7.4](method-inventory-steering-editing.md#sec-7.4)) mean editing is not yet a reliable substitute for retraining.

<!-- sec:13.3 -->
### <a id="sec-13.3"></a>13.3 Steering products and the maturity verdict

<a id="p-133-steering-products-and-the-maturity-verdict-1"></a><!-- para:133-steering-products-and-the-maturity-verdict-1 --> **Product-level control exists.** Golden Gate Claude <!-- cite:8 --> [[8]](references.md#ref-8), <!-- cite:75 --> [[75]](references.md#ref-75) shipped feature clamping as a public demo, and feature/direction steering is a real, if coarse, control surface (§§ <!-- secxref:7.1 -->[§7.1](method-inventory-steering-editing.md#sec-7.1), <!-- secxref:7.6 -->[§7.6](method-inventory-steering-editing.md#sec-7.6)). Feature-based classifiers (the sleeper-agent probe) are already useful monitors.

<a id="p-133-steering-products-and-the-maturity-verdict-2"></a><!-- para:133-steering-products-and-the-maturity-verdict-2 --> **The maturity verdict.** Read together, the applications say MI has legible, sometimes shippable wins for **steering** and **non-adversarial monitoring**, but has **not** replaced behavioral/black-box safety methods (RLHF, red-teaming, evals) as the primary safety mechanism, and is explicitly **not robust to an adversarial threat model**. Anthropic's own framing is that a reliable "MRI for AI" is a *bet* 5–10 years out, possibly slower than the capabilities it would police <!-- cite:76 --> [[76]](references.md#ref-76). This survey treats that gap as the field's defining open challenge (§ <!-- secxref:15 -->[§15](open-problems-and-roadmap.md#sec-15)), not a solved problem — the difference between "interpretability produced a compelling demo" and "interpretability certified this model is safe" is the entire remaining distance.
