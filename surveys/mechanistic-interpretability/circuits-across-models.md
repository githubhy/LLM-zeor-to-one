## <a id="sec-9"></a>9 Circuits across models

<a id="p-9-circuits-across-models-1"></a><!-- para:9-circuits-across-models-1 --> This section is the survey's "various models" spine: worked circuit case studies, each tagged with the *model it was found on* and whether it *transports* across scale and family — from toy/algorithmic transformers, through GPT-2 (small/XL) and the Pythia suite, to Gemma 2 and Claude 3, with the field's InceptionV1/CLIP vision roots as origin context (§ <!-- secxref:1.3 -->[§1.3](introduction-and-scope.md#sec-1.3)).

<!-- sec:9.1 -->
### <a id="sec-9.1"></a>9.1 Induction heads (toy → GPT-2 → large) [headline]

<a id="p-91-induction-heads-toy-gpt-2-large-headline-1"></a><!-- para:91-induction-heads-toy-gpt-2-large-headline-1 --> **The circuit.** An induction head implements in-context copying $[A][B]\dots[A]\!\to\![B]$ via two heads in different layers <!-- cite:80 --> [[80]](references.md#ref-80). A layer-$\ell$ **previous-token head** writes token $t{-}1$'s identity into position $t$; a later **induction head** K-composes with it (§ <!-- secxref:2.2 -->[§2.2](fundamentals.md#sec-2.2)) so that its query (the current token $A$) matches the key at the position after the earlier $A$, and its OV circuit copies the token that followed — predicting $B$. It is the cleanest two-layer demonstration of composition and of the QK/OV factorization in action.

<a id="p-91-induction-heads-toy-gpt-2-large-headline-2"></a><!-- para:91-induction-heads-toy-gpt-2-large-headline-2 --> **The transport story (why it is in this section).** Induction heads are the field's strongest *universality* claim: Olsson et al. give six lines of evidence that they drive a large fraction of in-context learning "for models of any size," with strong causal (ablation) evidence in small attention-only models and correlational co-occurrence evidence in large models with MLPs <!-- cite:80 --> [[80]](references.md#ref-80). The signature is a **phase change** during training — a visible bump where the model's ability to use long-range context suddenly improves, co-occurring with induction-head formation. *(The exact training-token window and pre/post-bump context-length figures are search-derived in the evidence ledger and confirmed against <!-- cite:80 --> [[80]](references.md#ref-80) in the citation-audit pass.)*

> <a id="p-91-induction-heads-toy-gpt-2-large-headline-3"></a><!-- para:91-induction-heads-toy-gpt-2-large-headline-3 --> **Intuition — a matched filter for repetition.** The induction head is a learned matched filter: its QK circuit correlates the present against the past to find the last occurrence of the current token, and its OV circuit reads out "what came next." In-context learning, at least in part, is the model noticing "this pattern happened before" and replaying the continuation.

### <a id="sec-9.2"></a>9.2 The IOI circuit (GPT-2 small) [headline]

<a id="p-92-the-ioi-circuit-gpt-2-small-headline-1"></a><!-- para:92-the-ioi-circuit-gpt-2-small-headline-1 --> **The circuit.** Indirect Object Identification — "When Mary and John went to the store, John gave a drink to ___" → "Mary" — is computed in GPT-2 small (12 layers × 12 heads) by a circuit of **26 attention heads in 7 functional classes**, found by path patching <!-- cite:35 --> [[35]](references.md#ref-35). The end-to-end story: **Duplicate-Token** and **Induction** heads detect that the subject name (John) is repeated and write that signal forward; **S-Inhibition** heads read it at the final position and modify the *queries* of the **Name-Mover** heads to push attention away from the duplicated name; **Name-Mover** heads then attend to and copy the indirect-object name (Mary) to the logits; **Negative Name-Mover** heads suppress a candidate (calibration); and **Backup Name-Mover** heads take over if the primaries are ablated (redundancy).

<a id="p-92-the-ioi-circuit-gpt-2-small-headline-2"></a><!-- para:92-the-ioi-circuit-gpt-2-small-headline-2 --> **Why it is a landmark.** IOI is the canonical proof that a nontrivial LM behavior decomposes into a legible, hand-verifiable wiring diagram — and, via its **backup heads**, the first clear demonstration of **self-repair** (§ <!-- secxref:10.2 -->[§10.2](evaluation-and-metrics.md#sec-10.2)): heads that do nothing detectable in the clean model spring into action under ablation, so the "circuit" as read off the clean model is already incomplete. It is also the standard testbed for automated circuit discovery (§ <!-- secxref:5.4 -->[§5.4](method-inventory-causal.md#sec-5.4)) and for the faithfulness-metric critique (§ <!-- secxref:10.1 -->[§10.1](evaluation-and-metrics.md#sec-10.1)). *(Its reported ~87%-logit-difference-recovered figure came back internally inconsistent in the evidence ledger — flagged and resolved against <!-- cite:35 --> [[35]](references.md#ref-35) in the citation-audit pass; the qualitative "recovers most, not all" is robust.)*

### <a id="sec-9.3"></a>9.3 Grokking and modular addition (a toy transformer) [headline]

<a id="p-93-grokking-and-modular-addition-a-toy-transformer-headline-1"></a><!-- para:93-grokking-and-modular-addition-a-toy-transformer-headline-1 --> **The fully reverse-engineered algorithm.** A one-layer transformer trained on $a + b \bmod p$ (with $p = 113$) is understood *completely* <!-- cite:55 --> [[55]](references.md#ref-55): it embeds each input on a small set of circles via a sparse subset of Fourier frequencies $w_k = 2\pi k/p$, and computes the sum by composing rotations through trigonometric identities. Writing the readout logit for candidate answer $c$:

<a id="eq-1"></a><!-- eq:9-1 -->
$$
\text{logit}(c) \;\propto\; \sum_{k}\cos\!\big(w_k(a + b - c)\big), \tag{1}
$$

<a id="p-93-grokking-and-modular-addition-a-toy-transformer-headline-2"></a><!-- para:93-grokking-and-modular-addition-a-toy-transformer-headline-2 --> which is maximized, by constructive interference across the chosen frequencies, exactly when $c \equiv a + b \pmod p$ — a correlation / matched-filter readout in the frequency domain. This is the strongest existence proof that gradient descent can find a crisp, human-legible algorithm, and it is signal-processing-native: the network learned a discrete Fourier transform and the angle-addition identity $\cos(w_k a)\cos(w_k b) - \sin(w_k a)\sin(w_k b) = \cos(w_k(a{+}b))$.

<a id="p-93-grokking-and-modular-addition-a-toy-transformer-headline-3"></a><!-- para:93-grokking-and-modular-addition-a-toy-transformer-headline-3 --> **Progress measures and the three phases.** Grokking — test accuracy jumping long after training accuracy saturates — looks abrupt but is not: Nanda et al. define **restricted loss** (ablate all non-key frequencies) and **excluded loss** (ablate the key frequencies) as *progress measures* that reveal three phases — **memorization** (train loss down, circuit absent), **circuit formation** (the Fourier circuit forms gradually while test loss is still flat), and **cleanup** (weight decay removes the redundant memorized solution, and test loss finally drops) <!-- cite:55 --> [[55]](references.md#ref-55). The lesson generalizes: a flat external metric can hide continuous internal progress, which is exactly what a good mechanistic measure exposes. Full derivation: Appendix <!-- secxref:B.3 -->[§B.3](appendix-b-superposition.md#sec-B.3).

### <a id="sec-9.5"></a>9.5 Universality and transport [load-bearing]

<a id="p-95-universality-and-transport-load-bearing-1"></a><!-- para:95-universality-and-transport-load-bearing-1 --> The cross-cutting question this section is built to answer: **does a mechanism survive the jump across scale and family?** The evidence is mixed and method-dependent.

- <a id="p-95-universality-and-transport-load-bearing-2"></a><!-- para:95-universality-and-transport-load-bearing-2 --> **Mechanisms that transport.** Induction heads recur from toy models to the largest models tested <!-- cite:80 --> [[80]](references.md#ref-80); the refusal direction is a *single* direction across 13 chat models up to 72B and four families <!-- cite:49 --> [[49]](references.md#ref-49) (§ <!-- secxref:7.3 -->[§7.3](method-inventory-steering-editing.md#sec-7.3)); the residual-stream/QK-OV framework and superposition are architecture-general.
- **Mechanisms that are model-specific.** Detailed circuits (IOI's exact head indices, the greater-than head list) are GPT-2-small artifacts and are *not* claimed to transport verbatim; SAE dictionaries are trained per-model, and crosscoder model-diffing (§ <!-- secxref:6.4 -->[§6.4](method-inventory-dictionary.md#sec-6.4)) exists precisely because features do *not* line up across models for free.
- **Methods whose validity is scale-untested.** Self-repair magnitudes (§ <!-- secxref:10.2 -->[§10.2](evaluation-and-metrics.md#sec-10.2)) are largely GPT-2/Chinchilla-era measurements; whether they hold at frontier scale is open. Attribution graphs <!-- cite:20 --> [[20]](references.md#ref-20), <!-- cite:21 --> [[21]](references.md#ref-21) are the first circuit method demonstrated natively at frontier scale (Claude 3.5 Haiku), sidestepping the transport question by working directly on the target model.

<a id="p-95-universality-and-transport-load-bearing-3"></a><!-- para:95-universality-and-transport-load-bearing-3 --> The honest summary: **the *framework* (residual stream, features-as-directions, superposition, composition) is universal; specific *circuits* are usually not, and must be re-derived or re-trained per model.** This is why the field's scalable methods (SAEs, attribution graphs) run on the target model rather than porting a GPT-2 circuit — and why the comparison matrix (§ <!-- secxref:11.1 -->[§11.1](comparison-and-tradeoffs.md#sec-11.1)) scores "model-access needed" as a first-class cost.
