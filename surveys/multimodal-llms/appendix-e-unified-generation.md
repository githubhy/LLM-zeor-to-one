## <a id="sec-E"></a>E Unified generation: the three likelihoods

<a id="p-e-unified-generation-the-three-likelihoods-1"></a><!-- para:e-unified-generation-the-three-likelihoods-1 --> Section <!-- secxref:6 -->[§6](multimodal-generation.md#sec-6) described the discrete-AR and continuous-diffusion routes and the decoupling middle path; this appendix writes the three governing objectives explicitly and notes the attention structure each requires.

<!-- sec:E.1 -->
### <a id="sec-E.1"></a>E.1 Chameleon: one autoregressive likelihood over a mixed vocabulary

<a id="p-e1-chameleon-one-autoregressive-likelihood-over-a-mixed-vocabulary-1"></a><!-- para:e1-chameleon-one-autoregressive-likelihood-over-a-mixed-vocabulary-1 --> Chameleon <!-- cite:8 -->[[8]](#ref-8) tokenizes an image into discrete codes (§ <!-- secxref:D -->[§D](appendix-d-visual-tokenization.md#sec-D)), merges them with the text BPE vocabulary into a single vocabulary of $65{,}536$ symbols, and models any interleaved sequence $s=(s_1,\dots,s_T)$ — text and image tokens freely mixed — with one autoregressive likelihood and one cross-entropy loss:

<a id="eq-1"></a><!-- eq:E-1 -->
$$
p_\theta(s) = \prod_{\ell=1}^{T} p_\theta(s_\ell \mid s_{<\ell}), \qquad \mathcal{L} = -\sum_{\ell=1}^{T} \log p_\theta(s_\ell \mid s_{<\ell}) \tag{1}
$$

<a id="p-e1-chameleon-one-autoregressive-likelihood-over-a-mixed-vocabulary-2"></a><!-- para:e1-chameleon-one-autoregressive-likelihood-over-a-mixed-vocabulary-2 --> There is no modality-specific term: the same causal transformer, the same softmax over the same $65{,}536$-way vocabulary, predicts the next symbol whether it is a word or an image code, with begin/end-image markers the only signal of a modality switch. This total uniformity is the design's whole appeal — and its difficulty. Training a single softmax over text and image tokens is *unstable* at scale, because the two modalities induce very different logit norms; Chameleon's contribution is as much the stabilization (query-key normalization and reordered layer norms that keep the mixed-modal logits in range) as the architecture. Generation samples from Equation <!-- ref:E-1 -->[(1)](#eq-1) left to right, emitting an image as a $1024$-code span between markers (§ <!-- secxref:6.1 -->[§6.1](multimodal-generation.md#sec-6.1)) and decoding it with the VQ de-tokenizer.

### <a id="sec-E.2"></a>E.2 Transfusion: two objectives, one transformer, a mixed mask

<a id="p-e2-transfusion-two-objectives-one-transformer-a-mixed-mask-1"></a><!-- para:e2-transfusion-two-objectives-one-transformer-a-mixed-mask-1 --> Transfusion <!-- cite:10 -->[[10]](#ref-10) refuses to quantize images and instead carries them as continuous VAE latents, generated by diffusion while text stays discrete and autoregressive — the combined objective of § <!-- secxref:4.13 -->[§4.13](method-inventory.md#sec-4.13) (Eq 3 there), a language-modeling loss on text tokens plus a DDPM denoising loss (§ <!-- secxref:6.2 -->[§6.2](multimodal-generation.md#sec-6.2)) on image patches, summed over shared parameters. The non-obvious ingredient is the **attention mask**: text tokens use a causal mask (each attends only to the past, as autoregression requires), but the patches *within a single image* attend *bidirectionally* to each other, because a diffusion model denoises the whole image jointly and must see all of it at once. So one transformer runs two attention regimes at once — causal across the sequence, bidirectional inside each image block — which is the structural price of hosting both objectives in one network. Generation correspondingly interleaves two procedures: autoregressive decoding for text, and, when an image region is reached, a full reverse-diffusion sampling loop (§ 6.2) over that block before decoding resumes.

### <a id="sec-E.3"></a>E.3 Janus: decoupled encoders, one cross-entropy

<a id="p-e3-janus-decoupled-encoders-one-cross-entropy-1"></a><!-- para:e3-janus-decoupled-encoders-one-cross-entropy-1 --> Janus <!-- cite:18 -->[[18]](#ref-18) (§ <!-- secxref:6.3 -->[§6.3](multimodal-generation.md#sec-6.3)) keeps the simplest possible objective — a single autoregressive cross-entropy $\mathcal{L}=-\sum_i\log p_\theta(x_i\mid x_{<i})$, on text ids for understanding and on image ids for generation — and puts all of its design budget into the *inputs*. Two encoders feed one transformer: a SigLIP **understanding** encoder supplies high-level semantic features (for reading an image), and a VQ **generation** tokenizer supplies fine-grained discrete codes (for drawing one), each through its own adaptor, with a text head and an image head on the output. The claim Janus tests is that the representation good for understanding (semantic abstraction) and the one good for generation (spatial detail) genuinely differ, so a single shared visual encoder — Chameleon's choice — is a compromise that caps understanding quality. Training proceeds in three stages (train the adaptors and image head with encoders and LLM frozen; unified pretraining with the LLM unfrozen; supervised fine-tuning), and the reported result — a small model competitive on *both* understanding and generation benchmarks — is the evidence that decoupling the encoder, not the objective, is what the unified-generation problem was missing. Across all three designs the pattern is clear: the objective can be as simple as one cross-entropy (Chameleon, Janus) or as rich as a two-loss hybrid (Transfusion); what differs is whether the image is made discrete, kept continuous, or encoded twice.
