## <a id="sec-Q"></a>Q Reader's questions

<a id="p-q-readers-questions-1"></a><!-- para:q-readers-questions-1 --> The questions a careful reader asks, answered in one place with pointers back to the derivations. Each answer is a compressed version of a result established in the body; the cross-reference is where to go for the full treatment.

<!-- sec:Q.1 -->
### <a id="sec-Q.1"></a>Q.1 Why a frozen vision encoder?

<a id="p-q1-why-a-frozen-vision-encoder-1"></a><!-- para:q1-why-a-frozen-vision-encoder-1 --> Because it already learned the hardest part. A CLIP- or SigLIP-pretrained encoder has seen billions of image–text pairs and arranged its features by *linguistic* meaning (§ <!-- secxref:2.4 -->[§2.4](fundamentals.md#sec-2.4)), which is exactly the geometry a downstream LLM can read — so freezing it preserves that hard-won alignment while the connector and LLM do the comparatively easy job of consuming it. Unfreezing risks degrading the alignment on far less data than it was trained on, and buys little: the encoder's representations are already close to what is needed. The same logic explains why the connector, the *newest* component, is the one trained at every stage (§ <!-- secxref:5.4 -->[§5.4](training-and-alignment.md#sec-5.4)) — touch the most-pretrained part least.

<!-- sec:Q.2 -->
### <a id="sec-Q.2"></a>Q.2 Why do high-resolution images cost so many tokens?

<a id="p-q2-why-do-high-resolution-images-cost-so-many-tokens-1"></a><!-- para:q2-why-do-high-resolution-images-cost-so-many-tokens-1 --> Because tokens scale with the *area* of the image, and attention with the *square* of the tokens. An image is cut into $P\times P$ patches, so the patch count is $N_v = HW/P^2$ — double the resolution and you quadruple the tokens (§ <!-- secxref:2.2 -->[§2.2](fundamentals.md#sec-2.2)). Each token then occupies a slot in the LLM's context and the LLM's self-attention costs $O(n^2)$ in the sequence length, so a single high-resolution image can cost more attention than a long document (§ <!-- secxref:8.1 -->[§8.1](inference-and-serving.md#sec-8.1)): a $1344$-px image is already a couple of thousand tokens. This single fact — quality is bought in tokens, tokens are paid for quadratically — drives token compression (§ <!-- secxref:8.2 -->[§8.2](inference-and-serving.md#sec-8.2)) and is the binding constraint of multimodal serving.

### <a id="sec-Q.3"></a>Q.3 Discrete versus continuous image generation — what actually breaks?

<a id="p-q3-discrete-versus-continuous-image-generation-what-actually-breaks-1"></a><!-- para:q3-discrete-versus-continuous-image-generation-what-actually-breaks-1 --> Different things at each pole. **Discrete-autoregressive** generation (Chameleon, Emu3) quantizes the image into codebook tokens and predicts them like words; what breaks is *fidelity* — the vector quantizer is lossy, so fine texture and especially text-in-images degrade, an OCR weakness the models' own authors flag (§ <!-- secxref:6.1 -->[§6.1](multimodal-generation.md#sec-6.1)). **Continuous-diffusion** generation (Transfusion) keeps the image as continuous latents and denoises them, preserving fidelity; what breaks is *simplicity* — it needs a second objective and a multi-step sampler living inside the model, and one transformer must run two attention regimes at once (§ <!-- secxref:E.2 -->[§E.2](appendix-e-unified-generation.md#sec-E.2)). Neither pays both costs, which is why generation is the one stage of the stack where practice has not converged (§ <!-- secxref:6.4 -->[§6.4](multimodal-generation.md#sec-6.4)).

### <a id="sec-Q.4"></a>Q.4 Why does early fusion beat cross-attention in practice?

<a id="p-q4-why-does-early-fusion-beat-cross-attention-in-practice-1"></a><!-- para:q4-why-does-early-fusion-beat-cross-attention-in-practice-1 --> Mostly simplicity and adaptability, on the data budgets open models now have. Early fusion (project the visual features to look like tokens, concatenate, run one unmodified transformer) adds almost no new machinery and lets *every* layer of the LLM attend to the image through its own self-attention, and — crucially — it *fine-tunes the LLM*, so the model integrates vision into its own representations (§ <!-- secxref:3.4 -->[§3.4](architecture-building-blocks.md#sec-3.4)). Deep cross-attention (Flamingo) keeps the LLM frozen, which protects language ability but adds many new parameters and leaves a model that can only *react* to vision through bolted-on ports, never reshape itself around it. When there is enough instruction data to fine-tune safely, early fusion's deeper integration wins; cross-attention retains its edge only where the LLM must stay frozen or inputs are many-image and interleaved (§ <!-- secxref:10.2 -->[§10.2](comparison-and-tradeoffs.md#sec-10.2)). An ablation that switched a model from frozen cross-attention to fine-tuned early fusion and saw a clear gain is part of what settled open practice here (§ <!-- secxref:4.17 -->[§4.17](method-inventory.md#sec-4.17), Idefics2).

### <a id="sec-Q.5"></a>Q.5 What is M-RoPE, and why native resolution?

<a id="p-q5-what-is-m-rope-and-why-native-resolution-1"></a><!-- para:q5-what-is-m-rope-and-why-native-resolution-1 --> **Native resolution** means dropping the fixed-grid assumption: instead of resizing every image to one canonical size (and tiling when it is too big, § <!-- secxref:4.10 -->[§4.10](method-inventory.md#sec-4.10)), the encoder processes the image at whatever size it is and emits as many tokens as its size warrants (§ <!-- secxref:4.7 -->[§4.7](method-inventory.md#sec-4.7)). This feeds document and chart detail without a resolution ceiling, but it means the token count — and the *shape* of the token grid — varies per image, which breaks a position encoding designed for one fixed grid. **M-RoPE** (multimodal rotary position encoding) is the fix: it extends rotary position embeddings to assign each token a *separate* coordinate along the temporal, height, and width axes, so a token carries its 2-D (or, for video, 3-D) position regardless of the image's overall size (§ <!-- secxref:7.2 -->[§7.2](modality-breadth.md#sec-7.2)). Native resolution is the quality lever; M-RoPE is the position-encoding machinery that makes a variable-size token grid work.
