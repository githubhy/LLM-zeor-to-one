## <a id="sec-3"></a>3 Architecture building blocks

<a id="p-3-architecture-building-blocks-1"></a><!-- para:3-architecture-building-blocks-1 --> Section 2 built one perception pathway — encode the image, align the encoder to language, project the aligned features into the LLM's token space. This section opens that pathway up into the *design space* it actually is. Every vision–language model is a choice on the three axes of § 1 — where the signal enters (entry point), how the visual vectors meet the text (fusion), and how many tokens the bridge emits — and the competing realizations of those choices are the building blocks inventoried here. We treat the connector taxonomy and the fusion mechanism at *headline* depth, because they are where the real architectural disagreement in the field lives; the entry-point axis is load-bearing but shorter, and discrete native-token generation is introduced here only as a fusion pattern, with its tokenizer machinery deferred to the generation section and Appendix D.

<!-- sec:3.1 -->
### <a id="sec-3.1"></a>3.1 The canonical stack and where it varies

<a id="p-31-the-canonical-stack-and-where-it-varies-1"></a><!-- para:31-the-canonical-stack-and-where-it-varies-1 --> Almost every model in this survey instantiates the same three-box template: a **vision encoder** $f_v$ turns the image into patch features, a **connector** $g_\phi$ maps those features toward the LLM, and the **language model** $p_\theta$ consumes the result and generates text. What distinguishes the models is *which box is frozen, which box does the mixing, and how wide the pipe between the second and third box is*. Holding the template fixed and varying these three knobs organizes the entire inventory: the entry point (is there a pretrained encoder $f_v$ at all, or are raw patches fed straight to $p_\theta$?), the connector (is $g_\phi$ a token-preserving projection or a token-reducing resampler, and how many tokens does it emit?), and the fusion mechanism (does visual information enter $p_\theta$ by being concatenated onto its input, cross-attended from inside its frozen layers, or quantized into the same vocabulary the LM already predicts?). A fourth, cross-cutting knob — which boxes are trained versus frozen, § <!-- secref:3.5 -->[§3.5](#sec-3.5) — is not an independent axis so much as the consequence of the other three, and it is where the central engineering tension of the field, *capability versus catastrophic forgetting*, plays out.

<!-- sec:3.2 -->
### <a id="sec-3.2"></a>3.2 Entry point: encoder-based versus encoder-free

<a id="p-32-entry-point-encoder-based-versus-encoder-free-1"></a><!-- para:32-entry-point-encoder-based-versus-encoder-free-1 --> The default, inherited from § <!-- secxref:2.4 -->[§2.4](fundamentals.md#sec-2.4), is **encoder-based**: a separately pretrained, usually frozen, CLIP- or SigLIP-style vision transformer converts the image to language-aligned patch features before the LLM sees anything. This is the lineage of Flamingo <!-- cite:3 -->[[3]](#ref-3), BLIP-2 <!-- cite:4 -->[[4]](#ref-4), and LLaVA <!-- cite:5 -->[[5]](#ref-5), and it is dominant for a concrete reason: the encoder front-loads the hardest part of perception (turning pixels into meaning) using a model pretrained on billions of image–text pairs, so the connector and LLM inherit a strong visual prior for free. The cost is a fixed input resolution and patch grid baked into the encoder, and a representation optimized for image-level alignment rather than the dense, fine-grained reading some tasks need.

<a id="p-32-entry-point-encoder-based-versus-encoder-free-2"></a><!-- para:32-entry-point-encoder-based-versus-encoder-free-2 --> The **encoder-free** alternative removes $f_v$ entirely: raw image patches are flattened, linearly projected, and fed straight into the LLM's first layer, letting the language model itself learn to see. Fuyu <!-- cite:14 -->[[14]](#ref-14) is the canonical instance — there is no vision tower, only a linear patch projection into the decoder. The appeal is structural simplicity and *native* handling of arbitrary image sizes and aspect ratios: with no encoder fixing the grid, an image of any resolution is just a longer or shorter sequence of patches. The price is that the LLM must learn visual representation from its own (text-dominated) training signal rather than inheriting a CLIP prior, which historically demands more multimodal data to reach the same perception quality. The entry-point choice therefore trades a strong-but-rigid prior against a flexible-but-data-hungry blank slate; the encoder-based branch wins in practice today, and the rest of this section assumes an encoder is present.

<!-- sec:3.3 -->
### <a id="sec-3.3"></a>3.3 The connector: token-preserving projection versus token-reducing resampler

<a id="p-33-the-connector-token-preserving-projection-versus-token-reducing-resampler-1"></a><!-- para:33-the-connector-token-preserving-projection-versus-token-reducing-resampler-1 --> This is the single most consequential block, because the connector decides *how many tokens the image costs* — and from § <!-- secxref:2.2 -->[§2.2](fundamentals.md#sec-2.2) we know tokens are the currency the LLM pays in, quadratically, at every step. The connectors in use fall into two families. A frozen CLIP ViT-L/14 at $224$ px emits $257$ patch features (256 patches + class token), each of width $d_v = 1024$; three connectors take that same input and hand the LLM wildly different token bills.

<a id="p-33-the-connector-token-preserving-projection-versus-token-reducing-resampler-2"></a><!-- para:33-the-connector-token-preserving-projection-versus-token-reducing-resampler-2 --> A **linear or MLP projector** is *token-preserving*. LLaVA's connector is one matrix $W\in\mathbb{R}^{d_v\times d}$ — or, in LLaVA-1.5, a two-layer MLP <!-- cite:6 -->[[6]](#ref-6) — applied to *every* patch feature. The grid passes through unchanged: $\approx 256$ features in, $\approx 256$ visual tokens out, growing to $576$ at $336$ px. Detail is preserved (every patch survives), but the token count is the encoder's and scales with resolution squared. A **query-based resampler** is *token-reducing*: BLIP-2's Q-Former and Flamingo's Perceiver Resampler introduce a small set of *learned query vectors* that cross-attend to the patch features and emit a *fixed* number of output tokens, independent of how many patches came in — BLIP-2 uses $M = 32$ queries, Flamingo uses $M = 64$, so a $257$-feature image becomes $32$ or $64$ tokens regardless of resolution. **Pooling** is the crudest token-reducer: average- or stride-pool the patch grid (e.g. $2\times 2 \to 1$), cutting token count by the pool factor with no learned parameters — common as a cheap high-resolution stopgap.

<a id="p-33-the-connector-token-preserving-projection-versus-token-reducing-resampler-3"></a><!-- para:33-the-connector-token-preserving-projection-versus-token-reducing-resampler-3 --> **The Perceiver Resampler, derived.** Flamingo's resampler <!-- cite:3 -->[[3]](#ref-3) takes a variable-length set of visual features $\mathbf{X}\in\mathbb{R}^{N_v\times d_v}$ (a 2-D image grid, or a 3-D video grid with learned temporal embeddings, flattened to 1-D) and a fixed bank of $M$ learned latent queries $\mathbf{L}\in\mathbb{R}^{M\times d_v}$, and runs cross-attention with the queries as the query side:

<a id="eq-1"></a><!-- eq:3-1 -->
$$
\mathbf{R} = \mathrm{CrossAttn}\big(Q=\mathbf{L},\ K=V=[\mathbf{X};\mathbf{L}]\big) \in \mathbb{R}^{M\times d_v} \tag{1}
$$

<a id="p-33-the-connector-token-preserving-projection-versus-token-reducing-resampler-4"></a><!-- para:33-the-connector-token-preserving-projection-versus-token-reducing-resampler-4 --> Because the *output* shape is the query bank's shape $M\times d_v$, not the input's $N_v\times d_v$, the resampler is a learned *down-sampler*: it reads however many visual features arrive and writes exactly $M$. The signal-processing reading is direct — the $M$ latent queries are a learned analysis basis, each one a matched filter that pools the visual grid into a single summary coefficient, exactly a projection onto a fixed-size learned subspace. BLIP-2's Q-Former <!-- cite:4 -->[[4]](#ref-4) is the same idea with more machinery: $32$ queries of width $768$ pass through a BERT-initialized transformer, attending to each other by self-attention and to the frozen image features by cross-attention every other block, producing an output $\mathbf{Z}\in\mathbb{R}^{32\times 768}$ — a representation $8\times$ smaller than the $257\times 1024$ it summarizes. A linear layer then lifts $\mathbf{Z}$ into the LLM's width, and the result is *prepended to the text embeddings as a soft visual prompt*:

<a id="eq-2"></a><!-- eq:3-2 -->
$$
\mathbf{Z} = \mathrm{QFormer}(\mathbf{L}_q, \mathbf{X}) \in \mathbb{R}^{M\times d_q}, \qquad \text{soft prompt} = W_p\,\mathbf{Z} \in \mathbb{R}^{M\times d} \tag{2}
$$

<a id="p-33-the-connector-token-preserving-projection-versus-token-reducing-resampler-5"></a><!-- para:33-the-connector-token-preserving-projection-versus-token-reducing-resampler-5 --> **Worked example — the token bill.** Put the three connectors on the same $336$-px image, which a ViT-L/14 turns into $576$ patch features. The LLaVA MLP projector hands the LLM $576$ visual tokens; a single such image already rivals a $500$-word passage in context cost, and at the LLM's $O(n^2)$ attention this is the dominant term for short prompts. The Q-Former hands it $32$; the Perceiver Resampler, $64$. The resamplers cut the visual token bill by $9$–$18\times$ — and, crucially, *hold it constant* if the image is enlarged, whereas the projector's bill keeps growing. That is the whole tradeoff in one example: the projector keeps every patch (best detail, worst token economy); the resampler bounds the budget (best economy, lossy compression). It is no accident that BLIP-2 calls $\mathbf{Z}$ an *information bottleneck* — the $32$-token ceiling is exactly what forces the queries to keep only the most text-relevant visual content and discard the rest <!-- cite:4 -->[[4]](#ref-4).

<a id="p-33-the-connector-token-preserving-projection-versus-token-reducing-resampler-6"></a><!-- para:33-the-connector-token-preserving-projection-versus-token-reducing-resampler-6 --> **Tradeoff and current practice.** The bottleneck cuts both ways. Q-Former's $32$ tokens are wonderful for context economy and terrible for tasks that need fine spatial detail — dense OCR, small-text documents, precise localization — because detail below the queries' resolution is gone, and the Q-Former is itself a non-trivial module that needs its own two-stage pretraining (§ <!-- secref:3.4 -->[§3.4](#sec-3.4)). The token-preserving projector keeps the detail and needs almost no training, which is why the open-source field has largely converged on the **MLP-projector / early-fusion** recipe and addressed its resolution cost a different way: *tiling* a high-resolution image into sub-images, encoding each, and concatenating — the "AnyRes" pattern — or using a native-resolution encoder as in Qwen2-VL <!-- cite:7 -->[[7]](#ref-7). The query-resampler family persists where a fixed, small token budget is the binding constraint (long videos, many interleaved images) — precisely Flamingo's original setting.

### <a id="sec-3.4"></a>3.4 Fusion: early concatenation, deep cross-attention, native tokens

<a id="p-34-fusion-early-concatenation-deep-cross-attention-native-tokens-1"></a><!-- para:34-fusion-early-concatenation-deep-cross-attention-native-tokens-1 --> Where § <!-- secref:3.3 -->[§3.3](#sec-3.3) decided *how many* visual tokens exist, fusion decides *how they touch the text*. Three patterns dominate, and they differ in where, in the language model's computation, the modalities are allowed to interact.

<a id="p-34-fusion-early-concatenation-deep-cross-attention-native-tokens-2"></a><!-- para:34-fusion-early-concatenation-deep-cross-attention-native-tokens-2 --> **Early fusion — concatenate, then run an ordinary transformer.** This is the § <!-- secxref:2.6 -->[§2.6](fundamentals.md#sec-2.6) pattern: project the visual features into the LLM's token space and lay them in the sequence ahead of the text, then a *standard, unmodified* decoder processes the union with one self-attention stack. LLaVA and BLIP-2's second stage both do this — BLIP-2's soft prompt of Equation <!-- ref:3-2 -->[(2)](#eq-2) is literally prepended to the text embeddings. The virtue is radical simplicity: no new attention machinery, the visual tokens are first-class citizens that every text token attends to through the LM's own self-attention, and the only new parameter is the connector. The cost is the token budget of § <!-- secref:3.3 -->[§3.3](#sec-3.3) (visual tokens occupy real context) and that the LLM usually must be *fine-tuned* to read the new token type, raising the catastrophic-forgetting question of § <!-- secref:3.5 -->[§3.5](#sec-3.5).

<a id="p-34-fusion-early-concatenation-deep-cross-attention-native-tokens-3"></a><!-- para:34-fusion-early-concatenation-deep-cross-attention-native-tokens-3 --> **Deep fusion — inject visual cross-attention inside a frozen LM.** Flamingo takes the opposite stance: keep the language model entirely frozen and splice *new* cross-attention layers between its existing blocks, so that text tokens attend to the visual tokens from inside the network, at every depth, without the visual tokens ever entering the text sequence. The inserted **GATED XATTN-DENSE** block updates the running language representation $\mathbf{y}$ with a gated cross-attention to the visual features $\mathbf{x}$, followed by a gated dense layer, before the frozen self-attention and feed-forward run as usual <!-- cite:3 -->[[3]](#ref-3):

<a id="eq-3"></a><!-- eq:3-3 -->
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{y} &\leftarrow \mathbf{y} + \tanh(\alpha_{\text{xattn}})\cdot \mathrm{XAttn}(q{=}\mathbf{y},\, kv{=}\mathbf{x}), \\
\mathbf{y} &\leftarrow \mathbf{y} + \tanh(\alpha_{\text{dense}})\cdot \mathrm{FFW}(\mathbf{y}),
\end{aligned} \tag{3}
$$

<a id="p-34-fusion-early-concatenation-deep-cross-attention-native-tokens-4"></a><!-- para:34-fusion-early-concatenation-deep-cross-attention-native-tokens-4 --> with $\alpha_{\text{xattn}}, \alpha_{\text{dense}}$ *layer-specific learnable scalars initialized to zero*. The zero-initialized $\tanh$ gate is the load-bearing trick, and it deserves a worked look. At the start of training every $\alpha = 0$, so $\tanh(\alpha) = \tanh(0) = 0$, and both new terms in Equation <!-- ref:3-3 -->[(3)](#eq-3) vanish exactly: $\mathbf{y}\leftarrow \mathbf{y} + 0$. The inserted block is the identity map at initialization, so the conditioned model produces *bit-for-bit the same output as the original frozen language model* on step zero — the freshly initialized, randomly weighted cross-attention layers cannot corrupt the pretrained LM's behavior. As training proceeds the scalars drift away from zero and the gate opens smoothly, letting visual conditioning enter gradually rather than as a destabilizing shock to a model that has never seen it. This is the same move as a residual scaling or a learning-rate warmup, expressed architecturally: begin as a known-good function and deform continuously toward the target. It is why Flamingo can bolt vision onto a frozen $70$B Chinchilla and stay stable.

<a id="p-34-fusion-early-concatenation-deep-cross-attention-native-tokens-5"></a><!-- para:34-fusion-early-concatenation-deep-cross-attention-native-tokens-5 --> The structural payoff is that, because the visual tokens are *cross-attended* rather than concatenated, they never consume the LM's text-sequence budget — deep fusion's token economy is independent of how many images are in play, which is exactly what lets Flamingo handle interleaved, many-image, few-shot prompts. Flamingo formalizes the whole interleaved-input semantics as a product over text positions, each token conditioned on all preceding text and the images that precede it:

<a id="eq-4"></a><!-- eq:3-4 -->
$$
p(y \mid x) = \prod_{\ell=1}^{L} p\big(y_\ell \mid y_{<\ell},\, x_{\leq\ell}\big) \tag{4}
$$

<a id="p-34-fusion-early-concatenation-deep-cross-attention-native-tokens-6"></a><!-- para:34-fusion-early-concatenation-deep-cross-attention-native-tokens-6 --> where $x_{\leq\ell}$ is the set of images appearing before token $y_\ell$ in the interleaved sequence — a per-image attention mask makes each text token attend directly to the most recent image, with dependence on earlier images carried by the LM's own self-attention <!-- cite:3 -->[[3]](#ref-3). Deep fusion's price for keeping the LM frozen is parameter count and compute: the cross-attention layers are many new weights, and the frozen LM cannot adapt its *own* representations to the visual modality — it can only react to it through the bolted-on ports.

<a id="p-34-fusion-early-concatenation-deep-cross-attention-native-tokens-7"></a><!-- para:34-fusion-early-concatenation-deep-cross-attention-native-tokens-7 --> **Native-token fusion — quantize the image into the LM's own vocabulary.** The third pattern erases the modality boundary entirely. Instead of keeping visual features as continuous vectors that need a connector, it *discretizes* the image into integer tokens drawn from a learned visual codebook (the vector-quantization machinery of § <!-- secref:3.5 -->[§3.5](#sec-3.5) and Appendix D), interleaves those visual token ids with text token ids in one sequence, and trains a single transformer on the union with one next-token objective — no encoder, no connector, no cross-attention, just a bigger vocabulary. Chameleon <!-- cite:8 -->[[8]](#ref-8) is the canonical mixed-modal early-fusion instance. The elegance is total uniformity: image and text are the same kind of object, so the model can both read and *write* either one with identical machinery, which is why this pattern is the natural substrate for any-to-any generation (the generation section). The costs are that the model generally must be trained from scratch (a frozen text-only LLM has no visual tokens in its vocabulary), and that quantizing the image into discrete tokens throws away detail the way any lossy codec does — the resolution-versus-fidelity tension of § <!-- secxref:2.2 -->[§2.2](fundamentals.md#sec-2.2), now paid at the tokenizer.

### <a id="sec-3.5"></a>3.5 Frozen versus trained, and the forgetting tension

<a id="p-35-frozen-versus-trained-and-the-forgetting-tension-1"></a><!-- para:35-frozen-versus-trained-and-the-forgetting-tension-1 --> The fusion choice forces a training choice, and the training choice is governed by one fear: a pretrained LLM that is fine-tuned on a new modality can *forget* the language competence that made it valuable. The three fusion patterns sit at different points on this risk curve. Deep fusion (Flamingo) is the conservative extreme — the LM is *frozen*, so its language ability is preserved by construction and the zero-gate of Equation <!-- ref:3-3 -->[(3)](#eq-3) ensures even the new layers start harmless; forgetting is essentially designed out, at the cost of new parameters and a LM that cannot reshape its internals around vision. BLIP-2 occupies a clever middle: *both* the image encoder and the LLM are frozen, and the only trained module is the Q-Former, whose information-bottleneck role is explicitly to "reduce the burden of the LLM to learn vision-language alignment, thus mitigating the catastrophic forgetting problem" <!-- cite:4 -->[[4]](#ref-4) — the bottleneck does the adapting so the LLM never has to. Early fusion (LLaVA) takes the most risk and the most reward: it typically *fine-tunes the LLM* (after a projector-only warmup stage), letting the model deeply integrate vision into its own representations and reach strong instruction-following, but it must defend against forgetting with careful data mixing and staged training rather than architectural guarantees. Native-token fusion sidesteps the question by not starting from a frozen LM at all — it trains the unified model from scratch, paying full pretraining cost to buy a clean, forgetting-free slate.

<a id="p-35-frozen-versus-trained-and-the-forgetting-tension-2"></a><!-- para:35-frozen-versus-trained-and-the-forgetting-tension-2 --> There is a deeper unifying view worth stating explicitly, because it recurs throughout the survey. Every connector in § <!-- secref:3.3 -->[§3.3](#sec-3.3) is ultimately producing **soft prompts** — vectors in the LLM's input space that *are not* discrete tokens but stand where tokens stand — and every fusion mechanism in § <!-- secref:3.4 -->[§3.4](#sec-3.4) is a choice about *where and how* those soft prompts (or, for native-token fusion, hard discrete tokens) are allowed to influence the language model's computation. Seen this way the inventory is not a zoo of unrelated tricks but a small set of answers to two questions: *how compressed is the visual signal* (token-preserving versus token-reducing) and *how deeply is it wired into the LM* (concatenated at the input, cross-attended throughout, or merged into the vocabulary). The method inventory that follows catalogs the named systems; this section gave the axes along which to read them, and the next sections turn to how such a stack is *trained* and, finally, run in reverse so the model can *generate* a modality as well as perceive one.
