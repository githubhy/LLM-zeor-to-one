## <a id="sec-9"></a>9 Evaluation and benchmarks

<a id="p-9-evaluation-and-benchmarks-1"></a><!-- para:9-evaluation-and-benchmarks-1 --> How do we know a multimodal model is good — and, more sharply, how do we know we are measuring its *vision* rather than its language? This section inventories the benchmarks the field reports, the special case of hallucination measurement, and a validity crisis serious enough that a headline number on a popular benchmark can be largely a memorization artifact. The whole section is load-bearing depth: the benchmarks are a landscape to map, and the pitfalls are the load-bearing caution every number in § on SOTA must be read against.

<!-- sec:9.1 -->
### <a id="sec-9.1"></a>9.1 The benchmark landscape

<a id="p-91-the-benchmark-landscape-1"></a><!-- para:91-the-benchmark-landscape-1 --> Multimodal benchmarks cluster by the capability they stress, and a working taxonomy is more useful than a list. **General multimodal reasoning** is led by MMMU <!-- cite:11 -->[[11]](#ref-11) — $11{,}550$ college-to-expert questions across six disciplines and thirty heterogeneous image types (charts, diagrams, medical images, music sheets), scored by micro-averaged accuracy. MMMU is the field's most-watched number and its anchored deep-dive here, because it exposes the headroom that remains: human experts reach a best of $88.6\%$ on the validation set, while the strongest models sit near $69\%$ — a roughly $19$-point gap that no amount of scaling has yet closed <!-- cite:11 -->[[11]](#ref-11). Alongside it, MMBench probes fine-grained ability dimensions with a CircularEval protocol (each question asked under every cyclic permutation of its options, to defeat option-position bias), and MME scores perception and cognition across fourteen manually-authored subtasks. **Document and OCR** understanding is measured by DocVQA (questions over document images, scored by ANLS, the edit-distance-tolerant metric that rewards near-exact OCR matches), TextVQA (reading text in natural scenes), ChartQA (chart reasoning under a $\pm5\%$ relaxed-accuracy tolerance), and AI2D (science diagrams). **Visual mathematics** is the province of MathVista (geometry, figures, and chart-grounded math). The metric varies with the task — exact-match accuracy, ANLS, relaxed accuracy, multiple-choice accuracy — and one rigor note carries throughout: these are *rate* statistics on finite splits, so two models a point apart on a $900$-item set may not differ at all once a binomial confidence interval is drawn, and headline gaps should be read with that interval in mind.

<!-- sec:9.2 -->
### <a id="sec-9.2"></a>9.2 Measuring hallucination

<a id="p-92-measuring-hallucination-1"></a><!-- para:92-measuring-hallucination-1 --> Hallucination — describing objects, attributes, or relations that are not in the image — needs its own evaluation, because a fluent wrong answer scores well on accuracy benchmarks that do not probe for it. POPE <!-- cite:12 -->[[12]](#ref-12) is the standard instrument, and its design is a small lesson in evaluation craft. Rather than parse free-form captions, POPE reduces hallucination to a *balanced binary* question — "Is there a ⟨object⟩ in the image?" — with a $1\!:\!1$ ratio of present to absent objects, and reports F1 as the primary metric (plus the raw "Yes"-rate as a bias indicator). The cleverness is in *how the absent objects are chosen*, across three escalating settings: **random** (any absent object), **popular** (the most frequent COCO objects), and **adversarial** (objects that most often co-occur with what *is* present). Performance falls monotonically from random to popular to adversarial, which is itself the finding: models hallucinate exactly the objects their training co-occurrence statistics lead them to expect. POPE made the severity legible — many instruction-tuned models answer "Yes" to nearly every probe, a runaway positive bias <!-- cite:12 -->[[12]](#ref-12) — and it is the benchmark that the preference-alignment methods of § <!-- secxref:5.3 -->[§5.3](training-and-alignment.md#sec-5.3) are tuned against, closing the loop between the metric and the fix.

### <a id="sec-9.3"></a>9.3 The validity crisis: are we measuring vision?

<a id="p-93-the-validity-crisis-are-we-measuring-vision-1"></a><!-- para:93-the-validity-crisis-are-we-measuring-vision-1 --> The most important thing to know about multimodal benchmarks is that many of their questions can be answered *without looking at the image*. The diagnostic is one subtraction — the **multimodal gain**, the accuracy a model earns from actually seeing the image:

<a id="eq-1"></a><!-- eq:9-1 -->
$$
\mathrm{MG} = \mathrm{Acc}_{\text{image}+\text{text}} - \mathrm{Acc}_{\text{text-only}} \tag{1}
$$

<a id="p-93-the-validity-crisis-are-we-measuring-vision-2"></a><!-- para:93-the-validity-crisis-are-we-measuring-vision-2 --> If $\mathrm{MG}$ is small, the benchmark is testing language and world knowledge, not perception. And it is often alarmingly small. MMStar <!-- cite:48 -->[[48]](#ref-48) documents that a strong closed model scores $42.9\%$ on MMMU *with no visual input at all* — well above chance — and that across six benchmarks, text-only LLMs beat the random baseline by more than $24$ points on average, simply because answers are inferable from the question, the options, or memorized world knowledge. Worse, the gap is partly **training-data leakage**: one model scores $43.6\%$ on MMMU without images while *surpassing its own language backbone by $17.9$ points*, which can only mean benchmark samples leaked into multimodal training. The two effects compound — a high reported score can be vision-irrelevant questions answered by a memorized text prior — and they inflate exactly the headline numbers the field competes on. MMStar's response is constructive: $1{,}500$ samples hand-filtered to be *vision-indispensable*, plus two reporting metrics (a multimodal gain and a multimodal-leakage estimate) so a benchmark's own validity is measured alongside the model's score.

<a id="p-93-the-validity-crisis-are-we-measuring-vision-3"></a><!-- para:93-the-validity-crisis-are-we-measuring-vision-3 --> Three further pitfalls round out the caution. **Prompt sensitivity**: scores swing, sometimes by large margins, under minor rephrasings, so a single-prompt number is a point estimate of a noisy quantity. **LLM-as-judge bias**: open-ended answers scored by an LLM judge inherit the judge's position bias (favoring the first option), verbosity bias (favoring longer answers), and self-enhancement bias (favoring its own model family), and judges miss logic errors human experts catch. **Contamination beyond leakage**: image-text pairs from benchmark sets recur in web-scraped pretraining corpora at non-trivial rates. The upshot for the rest of this survey is a discipline, not a dismissal: benchmark numbers are useful but must be read with the multimodal gain, the confidence interval, and the contamination caveat attached — which is exactly how the SOTA section treats them. *[established]*
