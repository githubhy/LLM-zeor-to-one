## <a id="sec-8"></a>8 Inference and serving

<a id="p-8-inference-and-serving-1"></a><!-- para:8-inference-and-serving-1 --> Every design choice in this survey eventually arrives at one bill: the **visual token count**. Section 2 established that an image becomes $N_v = HW/P^2$ tokens and that the LLM pays for them quadratically; this section is what that costs at serving time and how the field claws it back. The vision-token cost problem is treated at headline depth because it is the dominant systems constraint of multimodal serving; compression and KV-cache handling are load-bearing.

<!-- sec:8.1 -->
### <a id="sec-8.1"></a>8.1 The vision-token cost problem

<a id="p-81-the-vision-token-cost-problem-1"></a><!-- para:81-the-vision-token-cost-problem-1 --> The trouble is that the strategies that improve *quality* — higher resolution, AnyRes tiling (§ <!-- secxref:4.10 -->[§4.10](method-inventory.md#sec-4.10)), native resolution (§ <!-- secxref:4.7 -->[§4.7](method-inventory.md#sec-4.7)) — all inflate the token count, and the LLM's prefill cost is quadratic in the total sequence length. Writing $T = R^2/P^2$ for the tokens a sub-image of side $R$ yields at patch size $P$, an $a\times b$ AnyRes tiling plus a thumbnail produces $N_v$ visual tokens, and the prefill self-attention compute scales as the square of the full sequence:

<a id="eq-1"></a><!-- eq:8-1 -->
$$
C_{\text{prefill}} \;\propto\; \big(N_v + m\big)^2\, d, \qquad N_v = (a\,b + 1)\,\frac{R^2}{P^2} \tag{1}
$$

<a id="p-81-the-vision-token-cost-problem-2"></a><!-- para:81-the-vision-token-cost-problem-2 --> with $m$ the text-token count and $d$ the model width. The numbers are sobering. A single $1344\times1344$ image at native resolution (Qwen2-VL, $14$-px patches merged $2\times2$, effective $28$-px stride) is $(1344/28)^2 \approx 2304$ tokens; LLaVA-NeXT's default five-tile AnyRes is $5\times576 = 2880$ tokens <!-- cite:33 -->[[33]](#ref-33); Qwen2-VL's dynamic range runs to $16{,}384$ tokens for a large image <!-- cite:7 -->[[7]](#ref-7). **Worked figure.** One high-resolution image can therefore cost more context — and, by Equation <!-- ref:8-1 -->[(1)](#eq-1), *quadratically* more attention — than a long document: a $2880$-token image dwarfs a typical few-hundred-token text prompt, so for image-heavy or video workloads the visual tokens, not the text, dominate the prefill. Empirically prefill becomes the bottleneck at scale — in long-context video inference it can be the large majority of end-to-end latency, with KV-cache handling the largest slice of *that*. The lever that helps quality is exactly the lever that hurts cost, and reconciling them is the whole of multimodal serving. *[established]*

### <a id="sec-8.2"></a>8.2 Token compression and pruning

<a id="p-82-token-compression-and-pruning-1"></a><!-- para:82-token-compression-and-pruning-1 --> Because the cost is set by token count, every serving optimization is a way to carry *fewer* visual tokens, and they sit at three points in the stack. The cleanest mental model: reduce tokens *before* the LLM (architectural), *inside* the encoder (merging), or *inside* the LLM (pruning).

<a id="p-82-token-compression-and-pruning-2"></a><!-- para:82-token-compression-and-pruning-2 --> **Architectural reduction (recap).** The query-resamplers of § <!-- secxref:3.3 -->[§3.3](architecture-building-blocks.md#sec-3.3) — BLIP-2's Q-Former ($32$ tokens), Flamingo's Perceiver Resampler ($64$) — *are* token compressors, fixing the budget by construction. Their cost is the § 3.3 fidelity loss; their virtue is a hard ceiling independent of resolution.

<a id="p-82-token-compression-and-pruning-3"></a><!-- para:82-token-compression-and-pruning-3 --> **Encoder-side merging.** ToMe <!-- cite:46 -->[[46]](#ref-46) merges similar tokens *inside* the ViT, training-free, using a bipartite soft-matching on key vectors that is as cheap as pruning but more accurate — combining, say, the many patches of a uniform sky into one — for a $2$–$3\times$ encoder throughput gain at a $0.2$–$0.3\%$ accuracy cost. The signal-processing reading is plain: adjacent patches are highly correlated, so the token grid is *compressible*, and merging is lossy compression that exploits exactly that redundancy.

<a id="p-82-token-compression-and-pruning-4"></a><!-- para:82-token-compression-and-pruning-4 --> **LLM-side pruning.** The sharpest insight is FastV's <!-- cite:45 -->[[45]](#ref-45): inside the LLM, visual tokens are attended to far less than text tokens in the *deep* layers — after layer 2 they receive only about $0.21\%$ of the attention that system-prompt tokens do, because shallow layers have already aggregated the image's information onto a few non-image "anchor" tokens. If the deep layers barely look at the image tokens, drop them: FastV prunes half the visual tokens after layer 2, cutting roughly $45\%$ of FLOPs with almost no accuracy loss, and even letting a 13B model run under a 7B FLOP budget at higher accuracy. Adaptive variants such as LLaVA-PruMerge <!-- cite:47 -->[[47]](#ref-47) combine attention-based selection with key-similarity merging to take a 576-token image down to a few dozen tokens. The catch, worth flagging for a deployer, is that attention-score pruning needs the explicit attention matrix, which fights FlashAttention's fused kernels — a real implementation tension between the algorithmic saving and the kernel that makes the rest of inference fast. *[reported]*

### <a id="sec-8.3"></a>8.3 KV-cache and the serving stack

<a id="p-83-kv-cache-and-the-serving-stack-1"></a><!-- para:83-kv-cache-and-the-serving-stack-1 --> Two facts make the multimodal KV-cache its own problem rather than a reuse of the text-LLM solution. First, **vision tokens are heterogeneous cache citizens**: they are spatially continuous and semantically sparse compared with dense language tokens, so the standard text-LLM eviction policies (keep recent, keep high-attention) misfire — naively evicting "low-attention" visual tokens degrades comprehension, because a token can be quiet for most of the sequence and decisive for one question. Per-modality eviction policies are an active research direction. Second, **streaming video has no fixed prompt**: frames arrive continuously, each adding tens to hundreds of vision tokens and triggering an incremental prefill, so the KV cache grows without bound and temporal redundancy between adjacent frames becomes wasted compute — the same compressibility ToMe exploits spatially, now along time. The throughline is that multimodal serving is a token-budget management problem end to end: the architecture sets the budget (§ 3), compression reclaims it (§ <!-- secref:8.2 -->[§8.2](#sec-8.2)), and the cache must spend it without discarding the few visual tokens that a future question will turn out to need. The long-video and real-time-omni regimes of § <!-- secxref:7.3 -->[§7.3](modality-breadth.md#sec-7.3) are where this is least solved, and they reappear in the open-problems section as the vision-token-efficiency gap. *[reported]*
