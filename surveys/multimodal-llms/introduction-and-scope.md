## <a id="sec-1"></a>1 Introduction and Scope

<a id="p-1-introduction-and-scope-1"></a><!-- para:1-introduction-and-scope-1 --> A language model is, at heart, a machine for one narrow task: read a sequence of discrete symbols — text tokens — and predict the next one. Everything a modern large language model (LLM) does is built on that single autoregressive reflex, repeated billions of times over text scraped from the world. A *multimodal* large language model (MLLM) keeps that text-token engine exactly as it is, but lets signals that are not text — a photograph, a spectrogram, a video clip — enter the same computation, and in the most general case lets them leave it too. This survey is about the machinery that makes that possible: how a continuous image becomes something a token-based transformer can read, how those visual tokens are fused with text, how the whole system is trained, and how — in the newest models — the same network learns to *emit* an image or a waveform, not just describe one.

<a id="p-1-introduction-and-scope-2"></a><!-- para:1-introduction-and-scope-2 --> It helps to start from the obstacle, because it is sharper than it first appears. Text arrives pre-discretized: a tokenizer chops a string into a finite vocabulary of sub-word units, and each unit is a row in a learned embedding table. A transformer never sees characters, only integer indices and the vectors they point to. An image is the opposite kind of object — a dense, continuous field of pixel intensities with no natural alphabet and no natural ordering. For a reader from signal processing the situation is familiar: the transformer is a *discrete-sequence processor*, and an image (or an audio waveform) is a continuous-domain signal. Feeding the latter to the former is, in essence, a sampling-and-embedding problem — partition the signal into pieces, summarize each piece as a feature vector, and lay the vectors out as a sequence, exactly as a multirate front-end turns a continuous waveform into a stream of frames a downstream block can consume. The art of multimodal LLMs is largely the art of designing that front-end and deciding where in the network its output is allowed to mix with text.

### <a id="sec-1.1"></a>1.1 What makes a model "multimodal"

<a id="p-11-what-makes-a-model-multimodal-1"></a><!-- para:11-what-makes-a-model-multimodal-1 --> Three verbs separate the cases, and keeping them distinct prevents most of the confusion in the literature. A model may **perceive** a non-text modality (it can take an image as input), it may **generate** one (it can produce an image as output), or it may do **both**. The overwhelming majority of systems people call "multimodal LLMs" today are perception-only on the non-text side: they read images and write text — visual question answering, captioning, document understanding, chart reading. A smaller but rapidly growing family is *any-to-any*: a single network that both reads and writes across modalities, so that "describe this photo" and "draw a photo of this description" are two directions of one model. We treat the perceive-only case as the backbone of the field and the generate-and-perceive case as its frontier, and the survey is organized so that the second is reached as a natural extension of the first rather than a separate subject.

### <a id="sec-1.2"></a>1.2 The three design axes

<a id="p-12-the-three-design-axes-1"></a><!-- para:12-the-three-design-axes-1 --> Almost every architectural choice in a multimodal LLM can be placed on one of three axes, and this triple is the organizing spine of the whole survey.

<a id="p-12-the-three-design-axes-2"></a><!-- para:12-the-three-design-axes-2 --> The first axis is the **entry point**: *where* the non-text signal is turned into vectors. Nearly all systems use a dedicated *encoder* — a vision transformer pretrained on images, an audio encoder pretrained on speech — to convert the raw signal into a short sequence of feature vectors before the LLM ever sees it; this is the lineage of CLIP <!-- cite:1 -->[[1]](#ref-1) and the Vision Transformer <!-- cite:2 -->[[2]](#ref-2). A minority feed raw patches straight into the LLM with no separate encoder at all, letting the language model's own first layer do the embedding, as in Fuyu <!-- cite:14 -->[[14]](#ref-14).

<a id="p-12-the-three-design-axes-3"></a><!-- para:12-the-three-design-axes-3 --> The second axis is the **fusion mechanism**: *how* the visual vectors are allowed to interact with the text stream. Three patterns recur. In **deep fusion** the visual features are injected into the *interior* of a frozen language model through added cross-attention layers, so the text tokens attend to the image at every block — the design of Flamingo <!-- cite:3 -->[[3]](#ref-3). In **early fusion** the visual features are projected to look like ordinary token embeddings and simply *concatenated* onto the front of the text sequence, after which a standard transformer processes the mixture with no architectural change — the design of LLaVA <!-- cite:5 -->[[5]](#ref-5) and, with a learned compressor in between, BLIP-2 <!-- cite:4 -->[[4]](#ref-4). In the **native-token** pattern there is no separate modality boundary at all: image patches are quantized into discrete tokens drawn from their own vocabulary and interleaved with text tokens in a single stream, so one transformer trains on the union with one next-token objective, as in Chameleon <!-- cite:8 -->[[8]](#ref-8). The signal-processing reading is that these differ in *where the side-information is mixed in*: deep fusion injects it at every stage of the processing chain, early fusion prepends it to the input stream, and the native-token approach refuses to distinguish side-information from signal at all.

<a id="p-12-the-three-design-axes-4"></a><!-- para:12-the-three-design-axes-4 --> The third axis is **what is generated**: text only, or any modality. A perceive-only model's output head is the ordinary text-token softmax. An any-to-any model must additionally be able to emit a non-text signal, and here two sub-strategies compete — predict *discrete* visual tokens autoregressively, exactly like text, as in Chameleon <!-- cite:8 -->[[8]](#ref-8) and Emu3 <!-- cite:9 -->[[9]](#ref-9); or attach a *continuous* diffusion head that denoises image patches while the same transformer still predicts text tokens, as in Transfusion <!-- cite:10 -->[[10]](#ref-10). That tension — discrete-autoregressive versus continuous-diffusion image generation — is one of the central open design questions of the field, and we return to it in depth later.

### <a id="sec-1.3"></a>1.3 A brief lineage

<a id="p-13-a-brief-lineage-1"></a><!-- para:13-a-brief-lineage-1 --> The modern field has a clean four-step arc. It begins in 2021 with **contrastive pretraining**: CLIP <!-- cite:1 -->[[1]](#ref-1) showed that training an image encoder and a text encoder to agree on which caption goes with which image yields a vision encoder whose features are already aligned to language — the component almost every later system reuses — while the Vision Transformer <!-- cite:2 -->[[2]](#ref-2) supplied the patch-token backbone for that encoder. In 2022 the first true vision-*language* models appeared: Flamingo <!-- cite:3 -->[[3]](#ref-3) bolted a vision encoder onto a frozen LLM through cross-attention, and BLIP-2 <!-- cite:4 -->[[4]](#ref-4) introduced a lightweight learned bridge between the two. In 2023 **visual instruction tuning** democratized the recipe: LLaVA <!-- cite:5 -->[[5]](#ref-5) showed that a single linear projection plus a modest set of GPT-generated instruction examples turns a frozen encoder and a frozen LLM into a capable assistant, and the design became the open-source default <!-- cite:6 -->[[6]](#ref-6). From 2024 onward the frontier moved two ways at once: toward *native-resolution, larger* perception models such as Qwen2-VL <!-- cite:7 -->[[7]](#ref-7), and toward *unified* models that generate as well as perceive — Chameleon <!-- cite:8 -->[[8]](#ref-8), Emu3 <!-- cite:9 -->[[9]](#ref-9), Transfusion <!-- cite:10 -->[[10]](#ref-10) — alongside closed "omni" systems that fold speech and real-time interaction into the same model, exemplified by GPT-4o <!-- cite:13 -->[[13]](#ref-13).

### <a id="sec-1.4"></a>1.4 Scope of this survey

<a id="p-14-scope-of-this-survey-1"></a><!-- para:14-scope-of-this-survey-1 --> This is a *learner-register* survey: it derives the prerequisites it needs, motivates each result before stating it, and leans on signal-processing analogies wherever they sharpen the intuition. The base transformer and the autoregressive language-modeling objective are taken as given rather than re-derived from scratch; a reader who wants that foundation built from first principles will find it in the companion survey on [language models from first principles](../llms-for-coding/language-models-from-first-principles.md) and its [attention appendix](../llms-for-coding/appendix-a-qkv-first-principles.md). Everything specific to *multimodality* — encoders, connectors, fusion, multimodal training, generation, and evaluation — is built here from the ground up.

<a id="p-14-scope-of-this-survey-2"></a><!-- para:14-scope-of-this-survey-2 --> **In scope.** Perception and generation of images, audio, and video by transformer language models, at full derivation depth: the encoder→connector→LLM stack, contrastive and generative pretraining, visual instruction tuning and multimodal alignment, discrete and continuous generation, inference cost, and evaluation. The treatment aims at *omni-modal parity* — vision is the best-documented case and leads the exposition, but audio, video, and any-to-any generation each receive their own first-principles development rather than a passing mention.

<a id="p-14-scope-of-this-survey-3"></a><!-- para:14-scope-of-this-survey-3 --> **Out of scope.** Text-only LLMs (the subject of the companion survey above); pure image-generation models such as latent diffusion systems, except where one is fused into an LLM; contrastive embedding models beyond their role as encoders; and vision-language-action / embodied-robotics policies, which inherit this machinery but add control-theoretic concerns we do not treat. Domain-specific medical or document-AI verticals appear only as illustrations.
