## <a id="sec-7"></a>7 Modality breadth: audio, video, and omni

<a id="p-7-modality-breadth-audio-video-and-omni-1"></a><!-- para:7-modality-breadth-audio-video-and-omni-1 --> Vision has led this survey because it is the best-documented modality, but the thesis of the field is *omni-modal parity* — the same transformer engine, fed any signal. This section earns that claim by developing audio and video from first principles rather than as afterthoughts, and then the omni case that folds them together in real time. The organizing insight is that nothing in § 1–§ 6 was specific to images: the partition → summarize → align template of § <!-- secxref:2.1 -->[§2.1](fundamentals.md#sec-2.1) is modality-agnostic, and audio and video are just different signals plugged into the same three stages, with different front-ends. Audio is treated at headline depth (its front-end is a clean signal-processing derivation); video and omni are load-bearing.

<!-- sec:7.1 -->
### <a id="sec-7.1"></a>7.1 Audio: the front-end is a spectrogram

<a id="p-71-audio-the-front-end-is-a-spectrogram-1"></a><!-- para:71-audio-the-front-end-is-a-spectrogram-1 --> An audio waveform is a one-dimensional continuous signal — amplitude sampled in time — and the § <!-- secxref:2.1 -->[§2.1](fundamentals.md#sec-2.1) problem is identical to the image case: a token transformer cannot consume raw samples, so we must partition, summarize, and lay out a sequence. For audio the partition-and-summarize stage has a century of signal processing behind it, and the dominant choice is the **log-mel spectrogram**. Slice the waveform into short overlapping frames, take each frame's short-time Fourier transform, collapse the fine frequency bins into a coarse, perceptually-spaced **mel** filterbank, and take the log:

<a id="eq-1"></a><!-- eq:7-1 -->
$$
\begin{aligned}
X_m[f] &= \sum_{n} x[n]\,w[n - mH]\,e^{-j 2\pi f n / N}, \\
S[m,k] &= \log\!\Big(\textstyle\sum_{f} \lvert X_m[f]\rvert^2\, H_k[f] \;+\; \varepsilon\Big)
\end{aligned} \tag{1}
$$

<a id="p-71-audio-the-front-end-is-a-spectrogram-2"></a><!-- para:71-audio-the-front-end-is-a-spectrogram-2 --> where $w$ is the analysis window, $H$ the hop between frames, $X_m[f]$ the STFT of frame $m$ at bin $f$, and $H_k[f]$ the $k$-th triangular mel filter. The output $S$ is a 2-D array — time frames by mel channels — that *is* the audio's "image," and from here the pipeline is the one we already know: a small convolutional stem plus a transformer encoder turn $S$ into a sequence of feature vectors. This is exactly Whisper's front-end <!-- cite:19 -->[[19]](#ref-19): resample to $16$ kHz, an $80$-channel log-mel with a $25$ ms window and $10$ ms hop, two stride-2 conv layers, then a transformer (the encoder card is § <!-- secxref:4.15 -->[§4.15](method-inventory.md#sec-4.15)).

<a id="p-71-audio-the-front-end-is-a-spectrogram-3"></a><!-- para:71-audio-the-front-end-is-a-spectrogram-3 --> **Intuition — the mel spectrogram is patch embedding for sound.** The parallel to § <!-- secxref:2.2 -->[§2.2](fundamentals.md#sec-2.2) is exact and worth making explicit for a reader from signal processing. Patch embedding tiles an image and projects each tile to a feature vector; the STFT tiles a waveform *in time* and projects each frame onto a Fourier basis; the mel filterbank is a fixed (not learned) analysis bank that pools fine frequency bins into perceptual bands, the audio analogue of pooling pixels into a patch. Both turn a continuous field into a sequence of summary vectors; the only difference is that vision learns its filterbank end-to-end (§ 2.2) while audio inherits a designed one. Once $S$ is computed, "an audio token" means exactly what "a visual token" meant — a feature vector standing in the LLM's input sequence.

<a id="p-71-audio-the-front-end-is-a-spectrogram-4"></a><!-- para:71-audio-the-front-end-is-a-spectrogram-4 --> **Two ways to attach audio to an LLM.** With Whisper-style features in hand, the connector question of § <!-- secxref:3.3 -->[§3.3](architecture-building-blocks.md#sec-3.3) recurs verbatim. Qwen-Audio <!-- cite:20 -->[[20]](#ref-20) takes the early-fusion path: feed the encoder frames into the LLM with task tags, no resampler. SALMONN <!-- cite:21 -->[[21]](#ref-21) takes the token-reducing path: a *window-level Q-Former* compresses each $\sim0.33$ s window to a fixed token count (the temporal twin of BLIP-2's spatial resampler), and only the Q-Former plus a LoRA adapter on a frozen Vicuna are trained. The connector taxonomy of § 3 is modality-independent: projector versus resampler is the same decision for sound as for sight.

<a id="p-71-audio-the-front-end-is-a-spectrogram-5"></a><!-- para:71-audio-the-front-end-is-a-spectrogram-5 --> **The discrete-audio-token alternative.** A third design discretizes audio the way § <!-- secxref:6.1 -->[§6.1](multimodal-generation.md#sec-6.1) discretizes images, so that speech can be *generated* as well as read. AudioPaLM <!-- cite:22 -->[[22]](#ref-22) quantizes speech-encoder embeddings into a small vocabulary of audio tokens (on the order of a thousand, at $\sim25$ Hz) and *extends the text LLM's embedding matrix* to include them, so one decoder-only transformer predicts interleaved text and audio tokens with a single next-token objective — the native-token fusion of § <!-- secxref:3.4 -->[§3.4](architecture-building-blocks.md#sec-3.4), applied to sound. Strikingly, initializing from a text LLM transfers competence: AudioPaLM inherits translation ability it never saw in speech, doing zero-shot speech translation for language pairs present only in its text pretraining <!-- cite:22 -->[[22]](#ref-22). *[established]*

### <a id="sec-7.2"></a>7.2 Video: frames, and the token-budget wall

<a id="p-72-video-frames-and-the-token-budget-wall-1"></a><!-- para:72-video-frames-and-the-token-budget-wall-1 --> Video is the modality where the § <!-- secxref:2.2 -->[§2.2](fundamentals.md#sec-2.2) token-cost problem becomes acute, because a video is *many images*. The base recipe is straightforward — sample frames, encode each with a ViT, concatenate the per-frame tokens — but the arithmetic is brutal: a single $336$-px frame is $\sim576$ tokens, so even a modestly sampled clip explodes the context. The field's first answer is **sparse uniform sampling**: Video-LLaVA <!-- cite:23 -->[[23]](#ref-23) takes just eight frames per clip, encodes them through a vision tower pre-aligned to language (alignment *before* projection, so image and video share one feature space), and projects with a single shared layer. Sparse sampling keeps the token budget feasible at the cost of temporal detail — Video-LLaVA's authors acknowledge the eight-frame ceiling loses information in long videos.

<a id="p-72-video-frames-and-the-token-budget-wall-2"></a><!-- para:72-video-frames-and-the-token-budget-wall-2 --> Beyond sparse sampling, two levers manage the budget. **Temporal compression** pools or merges tokens across frames — average-pooling a frame grid, or token-merging redundant patches between adjacent frames, since consecutive frames are highly correlated (a compressibility a sampler ignores). **Time encoding** tells the model *when* each frame occurred: Qwen2-VL's <!-- cite:7 -->[[7]](#ref-7) M-RoPE extends rotary position encoding to separate temporal, height, and width axes, so a token carries its position in time as well as in space. The long-video frontier — hours of footage, thousands of frames — is unsolved and is precisely a token-efficiency problem, taken up in the inference section (§ on serving) and the open-problems section. The signal-processing framing is apt: video understanding is sampling theory for a token budget — how few frames (and how few tokens per frame) can you keep and still reconstruct the answer. *[reported]*

### <a id="sec-7.3"></a>7.3 Omni and real-time

<a id="p-73-omni-and-real-time-1"></a><!-- para:73-omni-and-real-time-1 --> The omni case folds every modality into one model and adds a hard constraint absent from the others: *time*. A model that converses by voice cannot batch-process a fixed clip; it must consume and produce a stream, and respond fast enough to feel live. GPT-4o <!-- cite:13 -->[[13]](#ref-13) is the landmark — a single network trained end-to-end across text, audio, image, and video, accepting and emitting any combination, with reported audio-response latency around $320$ ms, near human conversational tempo; its internals are undisclosed, so this is a capability landmark rather than a design reference (a *[contested]* source, carrying no load-bearing claim here). The open, documented frontier is **full-duplex** speech: Moshi <!-- cite:38 -->[[38]](#ref-38) processes user audio, system audio, and a time-aligned system *text* stream simultaneously — an "inner monologue" that predicts text tokens alongside speech to keep generation grounded — over a streaming neural codec, with no explicit turn-taking (silence is just another token). The architectural shift from the rest of this survey is from *encode-then-respond* to *interleave-and-stream*: the partition → summarize → align template still holds per modality, but the model now runs it continuously, on overlapping input and output streams, under a latency budget. Real-time omni-modality is where the field's omni-parity ambition meets its hardest systems constraints, and it is the least settled corner of the map. *[reported]*
