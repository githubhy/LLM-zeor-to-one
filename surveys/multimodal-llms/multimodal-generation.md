## <a id="sec-6"></a>6 Multimodal generation: any-to-any

<a id="p-6-multimodal-generation-any-to-any-1"></a><!-- para:6-multimodal-generation-any-to-any-1 --> So far the survey has run the pipeline in one direction — pixels in, text out. An *any-to-any* model runs it in reverse as well: text in, pixels out, in the same network. This section is about that reverse direction, and it is organized around a single, still-unsettled design question. Once an image is no longer the input but the *output*, the model must produce a high-dimensional continuous object one decision at a time, and there are two fundamentally different ways to do that — predict *discrete* image tokens autoregressively, exactly as it predicts words (§ <!-- secref:6.1 -->[§6.1](#sec-6.1)), or denoise *continuous* image patches with a diffusion process bolted onto the same transformer (§ <!-- secref:6.2 -->[§6.2](#sec-6.2)). A third design recognizes that *reading* and *drawing* an image may want different visual representations and decouples them (§ <!-- secref:6.3 -->[§6.3](#sec-6.3)). We treat the two main routes at headline depth and close on the tension between them (§ <!-- secref:6.4 -->[§6.4](#sec-6.4)), which is one of the live open questions of the field. The discrete tokenizer that both the AR route and the decoupled route depend on was derived in § <!-- secxref:4.11 -->[§4.11](method-inventory.md#sec-4.11) (Appendix D for the full treatment); we build on it here rather than repeat it.

<!-- sec:6.1 -->
### <a id="sec-6.1"></a>6.1 The discrete-autoregressive route

<a id="p-61-the-discrete-autoregressive-route-1"></a><!-- para:61-the-discrete-autoregressive-route-1 --> The cleanest way to make a language model draw is to give it nothing new to learn how to do. If an image can be turned into a sequence of integer tokens — which is exactly what the VQ tokenizer of § <!-- secxref:4.11 -->[§4.11](method-inventory.md#sec-4.11) does — then *generating* an image is just next-token prediction over those integers, identical in form to generating text. Concretely, let an image be encoded as a sequence of codebook ids $\mathbf{v} = (v_1,\dots,v_K)$; the model factorizes their joint distribution autoregressively, conditioned on whatever text context $c$ precedes them, and a VQ *de*-tokenizer renders the sampled ids back to pixels:

<a id="eq-1"></a><!-- eq:6-1 -->
$$
p_\theta(\mathbf{v}\mid c) = \prod_{k=1}^{K} p_\theta(v_k \mid v_{<k},\, c), \qquad \hat{x} = \mathrm{Dec}(\mathbf{v}) \tag{1}
$$

<a id="p-61-the-discrete-autoregressive-route-2"></a><!-- para:61-the-discrete-autoregressive-route-2 --> This is the **native-token fusion** of § <!-- secxref:3.4 -->[§3.4](architecture-building-blocks.md#sec-3.4) used generatively. Chameleon <!-- cite:8 -->[[8]](#ref-8) is the reference instance: its tokenizer encodes a $512\times512$ image into $K=1024$ discrete tokens drawn from a codebook of $8192$ entries, and these image codes are merged with the text BPE vocabulary into one unified vocabulary of $65{,}536$ symbols, over which a single transformer is trained from scratch on $\sim10$T tokens of interleaved data with one next-token objective. Special *begin-image* / *end-image* markers tell the model when to switch modalities; to generate an interleaved document it simply samples tokens, emitting text codes between markers and image codes within them, and the de-tokenizer turns each $1024$-token image span back into a picture. Emu3 <!-- cite:9 -->[[9]](#ref-9) pushes the same recipe to video by adding temporal visual tokens, keeping the "next-token prediction is all you need" objective intact.

<a id="p-61-the-discrete-autoregressive-route-3"></a><!-- para:61-the-discrete-autoregressive-route-3 --> **Worked figure.** A single generated image is a $1024$-token span — roughly the cost of writing a $700$-word essay — produced one token at a time, each a draw from a softmax over (the image portion of) the $65{,}536$-way vocabulary, every token conditioned on all the image tokens drawn so far. The image is *built raster-like*, code by code, the visual analogue of writing a sentence left to right.

<a id="p-61-the-discrete-autoregressive-route-4"></a><!-- para:61-the-discrete-autoregressive-route-4 --> **Intuition and limits.** The appeal is total uniformity — one vocabulary, one loss, one architecture both reads and writes — which is exactly why this route is the natural substrate for any-to-any models. The cost is inherited from the tokenizer: quantizing a $512\times512$ image into $1024$ codes is lossy, and Chameleon's authors explicitly flag reconstruction of images containing *text* as the tokenizer's weak point, upper-bounding the model's OCR-in-generation ability <!-- cite:8 -->[[8]](#ref-8). Discrete-AR generation can only be as sharp as its codebook allows — the § <!-- secxref:2.2 -->[§2.2](fundamentals.md#sec-2.2) resolution-versus-fidelity tension, now paid at the moment of drawing. *[established]*

### <a id="sec-6.2"></a>6.2 The continuous-diffusion route

<a id="p-62-the-continuous-diffusion-route-1"></a><!-- para:62-the-continuous-diffusion-route-1 --> The alternative refuses to quantize. Instead of snapping the image to a codebook, it keeps the image as *continuous* latent patches and generates them with a **diffusion** process — a different generative mechanism that the same transformer can host. Diffusion is worth deriving from first principles because it is otherwise opaque, and because it is the engine inside Transfusion.

<a id="p-62-the-continuous-diffusion-route-2"></a><!-- para:62-the-continuous-diffusion-route-2 --> **The forward process — destroy structure on a schedule.** Diffusion defines a fixed *forward* process that gradually adds Gaussian noise to a clean image $\mathbf{x}_0$ over $T$ steps, until nothing but noise remains. The key analytic fact (Ho et al. <!-- cite:44 -->[[44]](#ref-44)) is that this whole chain collapses into a single closed form: the noised image at any step $t$ is a weighted blend of the clean image and one Gaussian sample,

<a id="eq-2"></a><!-- eq:6-2 -->
$$
\mathbf{x}_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,\mathbf{x}_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\epsilon}\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{I}) \tag{2}
$$

<a id="p-62-the-continuous-diffusion-route-3"></a><!-- para:62-the-continuous-diffusion-route-3 --> where $\bar\alpha_t$ decreases from $\approx1$ (almost-clean) to $\approx0$ (almost-pure-noise) along a fixed schedule (Transfusion uses a cosine schedule). For a signal-processing reader this is a controlled SNR sweep: $\bar\alpha_t$ is the signal power, $1-\bar\alpha_t$ the noise power, and $t$ indexes a glide from high SNR to zero.

<a id="p-62-the-continuous-diffusion-route-4"></a><!-- para:62-the-continuous-diffusion-route-4 --> **The reverse process — learn to denoise.** Generation runs this backward: start from pure noise $\mathbf{x}_T\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{I})$ and iteratively remove a little noise at each step until a clean image emerges. The network is trained to do the one thing this requires — predict the noise $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ that was added to produce $\mathbf{x}_t$ — under a strikingly simple regression objective:

<a id="eq-3"></a><!-- eq:6-3 -->
$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{simple}} = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}_0,\,t,\,\boldsymbol{\epsilon}}\Big[\big\lVert \boldsymbol{\epsilon} - \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t,\, t,\, c)\big\rVert^2\Big] \tag{3}
$$

<a id="p-62-the-continuous-diffusion-route-5"></a><!-- para:62-the-continuous-diffusion-route-5 --> with $\mathbf{x}_t$ given by Equation <!-- ref:6-2 -->[(2)](#eq-2) and $c$ the conditioning (the text). At inference the predicted noise is scaled by the schedule and subtracted, one step at a time, optionally sharpened by classifier-free guidance (contrast the conditioned and unconditioned predictions). **Why predict the noise and not the image?** Because the target $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ is always a unit-variance Gaussian regardless of $t$ or the image — a stationary, well-conditioned regression target — whereas predicting $\mathbf{x}_0$ directly would chase a target whose scale and difficulty vary wildly with the noise level. The $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$-parameterization is the trick that makes diffusion train stably.

<a id="p-62-the-continuous-diffusion-route-6"></a><!-- para:62-the-continuous-diffusion-route-6 --> **Transfusion — one transformer, two objectives.** Transfusion <!-- cite:10 -->[[10]](#ref-10) (the card is § <!-- secxref:4.13 -->[§4.13](method-inventory.md#sec-4.13)) puts Equation <!-- ref:6-3 -->[(3)](#eq-3) and the text language-modeling loss on the *same* network: text tokens are predicted autoregressively with a causal mask, while image patches — kept as continuous VAE latents, never quantized — contribute the diffusion loss of Equation <!-- ref:6-3 -->[(3)](#eq-3), with *bidirectional* attention *within* each image so its patches can denoise jointly. One transformer, two native objectives, no lossy tokenizer in the image path. **Worked figure.** Training samples a noise level $t$ per image, noises its latents in one shot via Equation <!-- ref:6-2 -->[(2)](#eq-2), and asks the transformer to regress the noise; generation seeds the image region with noise and runs the reverse steps while the text is decoded autoregressively around it. *[established]*

### <a id="sec-6.3"></a>6.3 Decoupling understanding from generation

<a id="p-63-decoupling-understanding-from-generation-1"></a><!-- para:63-decoupling-understanding-from-generation-1 --> Both routes so far reuse *one* visual representation for reading and for drawing. Janus <!-- cite:18 -->[[18]](#ref-18) argues this is a false economy. The representation a model wants for *understanding* an image is high-level and semantic — object categories, relations, the gist needed for reasoning — exactly what a SigLIP-style encoder (§ <!-- secxref:2.5 -->[§2.5](fundamentals.md#sec-2.5)) provides. The representation it wants for *generating* an image is the opposite: low-level, spatial, fine-grained detail, exactly what a VQ tokenizer's codes preserve. Forcing one encoder to serve both, as Chameleon's single tokenizer does, makes it a compromise that tends to drag down understanding. Janus therefore runs *two* visual pathways — a SigLIP **understanding encoder** and a VQ **generation tokenizer** — into a *single* autoregressive transformer, each through its own adaptor, with a text head for understanding outputs and an image head for generation outputs. Training is the same plain autoregressive cross-entropy $\mathcal{L} = -\sum_i \log p_\theta(x_i \mid x_{<i})$ (loss on text ids for understanding, on image ids for generation), staged in three phases (train the adaptors and image head with everything else frozen, then unified pretraining, then instruction tuning). The result: a small ($1.3$B) model that beats larger unified and even some task-specific models on both understanding and generation benchmarks <!-- cite:18 -->[[18]](#ref-18). The lesson generalizes beyond Janus — *understanding and generation are not the same visual task*, and a unified model need not force them through the same representation. *[reported]*

### <a id="sec-6.4"></a>6.4 The central tension: discrete-AR versus continuous-diffusion

<a id="p-64-the-central-tension-discrete-ar-versus-continuous-diffusion-1"></a><!-- para:64-the-central-tension-discrete-ar-versus-continuous-diffusion-1 --> The two routes of § <!-- secref:6.1 -->[§6.1](#sec-6.1)–<!-- secref:6.2 -->[§6.2](#sec-6.2) are not interchangeable, and choosing between them is one of the field's genuinely open design questions. Four axes separate them.

- <a id="p-64-the-central-tension-discrete-ar-versus-continuous-diffusion-2"></a><!-- para:64-the-central-tension-discrete-ar-versus-continuous-diffusion-2 --> **Fidelity.** Continuous diffusion never quantizes, so it sidesteps the codebook ceiling and tends to produce sharper images, especially on fine texture and text-in-image; discrete-AR inherits its tokenizer's lossiness (Chameleon's own OCR caveat). Advantage: diffusion.
- **Speed and cost.** Discrete-AR draws an image as $K$ sequential token predictions (Chameleon's $1024$ forward passes per image), the same per-token serial cost as long-text generation; diffusion runs $T$ denoising steps (tens, with modern samplers), each a full forward pass over the whole image. Both are iterative; neither is free, and which is cheaper depends on $K$ versus $T$ and on hardware. Roughly a wash, workload-dependent.
- **Unification simplicity.** Discrete-AR is the *simplest* possible unification — one vocabulary, one next-token loss, one architecture reads and writes everything (Chameleon, Emu3) — which is why it dominates the "pure any-to-any" thesis. Diffusion buys fidelity at the price of a second objective and a sampler living inside the model (Transfusion). Advantage: discrete-AR.
- **Maturity of tooling.** Autoregressive generation inherits the entire LLM serving stack (KV-cache, speculative decoding, batching — § on inference); diffusion inherits the mature image-generation stack (schedulers, guidance). Each route is "native" to a different, well-developed ecosystem.

<a id="p-64-the-central-tension-discrete-ar-versus-continuous-diffusion-3"></a><!-- para:64-the-central-tension-discrete-ar-versus-continuous-diffusion-3 --> There is no settled winner, and the most interesting recent systems refuse the dichotomy: Transfusion hybridizes (AR text, diffusion image, one backbone), and Janus decouples (semantic encoder for reading, detail tokenizer for drawing). The honest summary is that *unification* pulls toward discrete-AR and *fidelity* pulls toward diffusion, and the frontier is the search for an architecture that pays neither tax — a problem we return to in the open-problems section. For the working engineer the § <!-- secref:6.1 -->[§6.1](#sec-6.1)–<!-- secref:6.3 -->[§6.3](#sec-6.3) split is the decision tree: choose discrete-AR for the cleanest any-to-any unification, diffusion (or a Transfusion-style hybrid) when image quality is the binding constraint, and a decoupled encoder when a single model must be strong at both reading and drawing.
