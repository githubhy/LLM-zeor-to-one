## <a id="sec-5"></a>5 Training and alignment

<a id="p-5-training-and-alignment-1"></a><!-- para:5-training-and-alignment-1 --> A multimodal LLM is assembled from parts that were mostly pretrained separately — a vision encoder, a language model, and a connector between them — so "training" here means three distinct things in sequence: *pretraining* the components and aligning them to a shared geometry, *instruction tuning* the assembled stack to follow multimodal prompts, and *preference alignment* to make its answers trustworthy. This section walks that pipeline in order. The first two stages are at headline depth — they are the load-bearing recipe almost every open model follows — and the third, preference alignment for hallucination, is developed at load-bearing depth because it is where the most active method design currently sits. Throughout, the components and the forgetting tension are the ones from § <!-- secxref:3.5 -->[§3.5](architecture-building-blocks.md#sec-3.5); this section is how that tension is *managed in training* rather than designed away in architecture.

<!-- sec:5.1 -->
### <a id="sec-5.1"></a>5.1 Pretraining: objectives and the data that feeds them

<a id="p-51-pretraining-objectives-and-the-data-that-feeds-them-1"></a><!-- para:51-pretraining-objectives-and-the-data-that-feeds-them-1 --> Two pretraining objectives do almost all the work, and we have already met both. The first is **contrastive** alignment — CLIP's symmetric InfoNCE (§ <!-- secxref:2.4 -->[§2.4](fundamentals.md#sec-2.4)) — which produces the language-aligned vision encoder the whole stack is built on. The second is **generative**: next-token prediction over image–text data, either captioning (predict the caption given the image) or, in the interleaved setting, predicting text tokens conditioned on the images that precede them — the Flamingo objective of § <!-- secxref:3.4 -->[§3.4](architecture-building-blocks.md#sec-3.4) (Eq 4 there). Contrastive pretraining buys *alignment*; generative pretraining buys *fluency and grounding*; modern stacks use both, at different stages.

<a id="p-51-pretraining-objectives-and-the-data-that-feeds-them-2"></a><!-- para:51-pretraining-objectives-and-the-data-that-feeds-them-2 --> What changed the field as much as the objectives is the **data**. Three datasets illustrate the three regimes a VLM's pretraining draws on. **LAION-5B** <!-- cite:39 -->[[39]](#ref-39) is the web-scale contrastive corpus: $5.85$ billion image–text pairs scraped from Common Crawl and filtered by CLIP cosine similarity (a self-bootstrapping filter — an earlier CLIP keeps only pairs it already scores as aligned, retaining roughly the top tenth), of which $2.32$B are English. It is the fuel for CLIP-style encoders and diffusion models alike. **MMC4** <!-- cite:40 -->[[40]](#ref-40) is the *interleaved* corpus that makes few-shot multimodal prompting possible: $101.2$M documents with $571$M images woven into $43$B tokens of cleaned web text, where each image is assigned to its best-matching sentence by a CLIP-based linear assignment ($88\%$ of images are topically relevant, $80\%$ well-aligned to their sentence). Interleaved data of this shape is what Flamingo-style models train on, because single image–caption pairs cannot teach a model to reason across several images in one context. **ShareGPT4V** <!-- cite:41 -->[[41]](#ref-41) represents the *re-captioning* trend: $1.2$M densely descriptive captions, seeded from $100$K written by GPT-4-Vision and expanded by a caption model trained on them, replacing terse, noisy web alt-text with long, accurate descriptions — and measurably improving alignment, since a connector can only learn the geometry its captions describe.

<a id="p-51-pretraining-objectives-and-the-data-that-feeds-them-3"></a><!-- para:51-pretraining-objectives-and-the-data-that-feeds-them-3 --> **Intuition and the data-quality lever.** The arc from LAION's noisy alt-text to ShareGPT4V's dense captions is the multimodal version of a lesson from language modeling: at fixed scale, *caption quality* moves the needle as much as caption *quantity*, because the contrastive and captioning objectives can only align an image to the words it is paired with. A picture captioned "a photo" teaches almost nothing about its content; the same picture with a paragraph of accurate description teaches the connector exactly which patch features map to which concepts. This is why so much VLM progress is dataset progress wearing an architecture's name. *[established]*

### <a id="sec-5.2"></a>5.2 Visual instruction tuning: the LLaVA recipe

<a id="p-52-visual-instruction-tuning-the-llava-recipe-1"></a><!-- para:52-visual-instruction-tuning-the-llava-recipe-1 --> Pretraining yields a model that can *describe*; instruction tuning yields one that can *follow instructions about* an image — answer questions, reason, converse. The recipe that made this cheap and reproducible is LLaVA's <!-- cite:5 -->[[5]](#ref-5), and it has two ideas: a two-stage training schedule, and a way to manufacture instruction data without human annotators.

<a id="p-52-visual-instruction-tuning-the-llava-recipe-2"></a><!-- para:52-visual-instruction-tuning-the-llava-recipe-2 --> **The two stages.** Stage 1 (*feature alignment*) freezes both the vision encoder and the LLM and trains *only* the projector $g_\phi$ — on $\sim 595$K caption pairs (a filtered CC3M subset) for one epoch — so the connector learns to map encoder features into the LLM's token space before either heavyweight model is touched. Stage 2 (*instruction tuning*) unfreezes the LLM and trains it together with the projector on the instruction data below, for three epochs. The learning rates encode the asymmetry: $2\times10^{-3}$ for the projector-only stage, $2\times10^{-5}$ once the pretrained LLM is in the loop — two orders of magnitude gentler, precisely to avoid the catastrophic forgetting of § <!-- secxref:3.5 -->[§3.5](architecture-building-blocks.md#sec-3.5). LLaVA-1.5 <!-- cite:6 -->[[6]](#ref-6) keeps this skeleton and swaps in an MLP projector, a $336$-px encoder, and a $1.2$M-sample instruction mixture, reaching strong results on eleven benchmarks with a 13B model trained in about a day on eight A100s.

<a id="p-52-visual-instruction-tuning-the-llava-recipe-3"></a><!-- para:52-visual-instruction-tuning-the-llava-recipe-3 --> **Manufacturing the instruction data.** LLaVA's second idea sidesteps the absence of multimodal instruction data: feed a *language-only* GPT-4 the *symbolic* description of an image — its COCO captions and bounding boxes, as text, with no pixels — and ask it to generate questions and answers about the scene. The result is $158$K samples over $\sim 80$K images in three flavors: $58$K multi-turn conversations, $23$K detailed descriptions, and $77$K complex-reasoning Q&A <!-- cite:5 -->[[5]](#ref-5). The trick is that GPT-4 never sees the image — the captions and boxes are a *lossless-enough symbolic proxy* for generating grounded questions, and the student VLM then learns to answer them from the actual pixels.

<a id="p-52-visual-instruction-tuning-the-llava-recipe-4"></a><!-- para:52-visual-instruction-tuning-the-llava-recipe-4 --> **The objective, made precise.** Instruction tuning is ordinary autoregression with one twist: the loss is computed *only on the answer tokens*. For a training example with visual tokens $\mathbf{h}_{1:N_v}$ (the projected patch features of § <!-- secxref:2.6 -->[§2.6](fundamentals.md#sec-2.6)), an instruction $X_{\text{inst}}$, and a target answer of $L_a$ tokens, the model maximizes the answer's conditional likelihood,

<a id="eq-1"></a><!-- eq:5-1 -->
$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{IT}} = -\frac{1}{L_a}\sum_{i=1}^{L_a} \log p_\theta\big(x_{a,i} \,\mid\, \mathbf{h}_{1:N_v},\, X_{\text{inst}},\, X_{a,<i}\big) \tag{1}
$$

<a id="p-52-visual-instruction-tuning-the-llava-recipe-5"></a><!-- para:52-visual-instruction-tuning-the-llava-recipe-5 --> with the visual and instruction tokens serving as *conditioning only* — they are never prediction targets, because the model is being taught to produce answers, not to re-predict the prompt or hallucinate the image from text.

<a id="p-52-visual-instruction-tuning-the-llava-recipe-6"></a><!-- para:52-visual-instruction-tuning-the-llava-recipe-6 --> **Worked training step.** Take the canonical LLaVA example: an image of a man ironing clothes fixed to the roof of a moving taxi. The encoder yields $\mathbf{h}_{1:256}$ visual tokens; the instruction "What is unusual about this image?" is, say, $8$ tokens; the target answer "A man is ironing clothes on the back of a moving car." is $\sim 14$ tokens. The forward pass runs the LLM over the $256+8+14$-token sequence; the loss of Equation <!-- ref:5-1 -->[(1)](#eq-1) averages the negative log-probability over only the $14$ answer positions; backprop in stage 2 updates the projector and the LLM (at $2\times10^{-5}$) but not the frozen encoder. Repeat over $158$K such examples for three epochs and a frozen-encoder, frozen-then-unfrozen-LLM stack becomes a visual assistant. The whole second stage is hours, not weeks — the dividend of starting from two already-capable pretrained models. *[established]*

### <a id="sec-5.3"></a>5.3 Preference alignment and hallucination

<a id="p-53-preference-alignment-and-hallucination-1"></a><!-- para:53-preference-alignment-and-hallucination-1 --> An instruction-tuned VLM is fluent but not yet *trustworthy*: it hallucinates — describes objects, attributes, counts, and spatial relations that are not in the image. This is the multimodal form of the alignment problem, and the dominant fix is **preference optimization**, adapted from text LLMs with two multimodal-specific corrections.

<a id="p-53-preference-alignment-and-hallucination-2"></a><!-- para:53-preference-alignment-and-hallucination-2 --> **Background: DPO in the multimodal setting.** Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) aligns a model to pairwise human preference without training a separate reward model: given a preferred answer $y_w$ and a rejected one $y_l$ for an input, it directly raises the policy's relative log-likelihood of $y_w$ over $y_l$, anchored to a frozen reference model $\pi_{\text{ref}}$. In the multimodal case the input carries an image $m$ as well as the prompt $q$:

<a id="eq-2"></a><!-- eq:5-2 -->
$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}} = -\,\mathbb{E}_{(m,q,y_w,y_l)}\!\left[\log \sigma\!\Big(\beta \log\tfrac{\pi_\theta(y_w\mid m,q)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w\mid m,q)} - \beta \log\tfrac{\pi_\theta(y_l\mid m,q)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l\mid m,q)}\Big)\right] \tag{2}
$$

<a id="p-53-preference-alignment-and-hallucination-3"></a><!-- para:53-preference-alignment-and-hallucination-3 --> where $\beta$ controls how far the policy may drift from the reference <!-- cite:43 -->[[43]](#ref-43). Plugging multimodal preference pairs into Equation <!-- ref:5-2 -->[(2)](#eq-2) is the obvious move — and it has two failure modes that the next two methods fix.

<a id="p-53-preference-alignment-and-hallucination-4"></a><!-- para:53-preference-alignment-and-hallucination-4 --> **Fix 1 — fine-grained, segment-level credit (RLHF-V).** A whole-response preference label is a blunt instrument: if a long answer is mostly correct but hallucinates one object, ranking the whole response teaches the model little about *which span* was wrong, and the mis-allocated credit invites reward hacking. RLHF-V <!-- cite:42 -->[[42]](#ref-42) collects *segment-level corrections* instead — a human edits only the hallucinated spans — and trains with **Dense DPO (DDPO)**, which re-weights the per-token log-likelihood so that corrected segments count more:

<a id="eq-3"></a><!-- eq:5-3 -->
$$
\log \pi(y\mid x) = \frac{1}{N}\Big[\sum_{y_i\in y_u} \log p_\theta(y_i\mid x, y_{<i}) + \gamma \sum_{y_i\in y_c} \log p_\theta(y_i\mid x, y_{<i})\Big], \qquad N = \lvert y_u\rvert + \gamma\,\lvert y_c\rvert \tag{3}
$$

<a id="p-53-preference-alignment-and-hallucination-5"></a><!-- para:53-preference-alignment-and-hallucination-5 --> where $y_u$ are the unchanged segments, $y_c$ the corrected ones, and $\gamma>1$ concentrates the learning signal on exactly the spans the human flagged. The payoff is sample efficiency: $1.4$K fine-grained annotations cut object hallucination on the base model by about a third — beating a contemporaneous method trained on roughly seven times the (coarse) preference data <!-- cite:42 -->[[42]](#ref-42). The intuition is matched-filter-like: spend the gradient where the error actually is, not smeared across a mostly-correct response.

<a id="p-53-preference-alignment-and-hallucination-6"></a><!-- para:53-preference-alignment-and-hallucination-6 --> **Fix 2 — make the preference depend on the image (mDPO).** The second failure is subtler and specific to multimodality. Applied naively, Equation <!-- ref:5-2 -->[(2)](#eq-2) can be satisfied by *language-only* cues — the model learns "this phrasing is preferred" without ever consulting the image, an **unconditional-preference** pathology. mDPO <!-- cite:43 -->[[43]](#ref-43) demonstrates it starkly: a DPO run with the *images removed* performs about as well as one with images, proof that the standard objective is ignoring the visual modality. The fix adds two terms:

<a id="eq-4"></a><!-- eq:5-4 -->
$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{mDPO}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{CoPO}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{AncPO}} \tag{4}
$$

<a id="p-53-preference-alignment-and-hallucination-7"></a><!-- para:53-preference-alignment-and-hallucination-7 --> The *conditional* term $\mathcal{L}_{\text{CoPO}}$ builds preference pairs in which the image is the only thing that changes — same prompt, same answer, but a degraded image (the original with a small random crop that removes visual evidence) as the "rejected" condition — forcing the model to prefer the answer *because of* the better image. The *anchored* term $\mathcal{L}_{\text{AncPO}}$ patches a known DPO defect: because DPO optimizes only a *relative* margin, it can raise that margin by *lowering* the chosen answer's likelihood; the anchor regularizes the chosen reward to stay positive. Together they restore the dependence on pixels that Equation <!-- ref:5-2 -->[(2)](#eq-2) lost. *[established]*

<a id="p-53-preference-alignment-and-hallucination-8"></a><!-- para:53-preference-alignment-and-hallucination-8 --> **Complexity and where this sits.** Preference alignment is a *post-training* stage — small data ($1$–$10$K pairs), short runs (RLHF-V reports about an hour on eight A100s) — applied after instruction tuning, not a from-scratch cost. It is also the stage most directly measured by the hallucination benchmarks (POPE and Object HalBench, taken up in the evaluation section), which is why hallucination metrics and preference-alignment methods co-evolve. The open frontier — AI-generated feedback in place of human labels, and richer notions of "grounded" preference — is surveyed in the open-problems section.

### <a id="sec-5.4"></a>5.4 What is frozen, and when

<a id="p-54-what-is-frozen-and-when-1"></a><!-- para:54-what-is-frozen-and-when-1 --> Pulling the three stages together gives the field's default freezing schedule, and it reads directly off the forgetting tension of § <!-- secxref:3.5 -->[§3.5](architecture-building-blocks.md#sec-3.5). The vision encoder is usually frozen throughout — it was pretrained on far more images than any downstream stage will see, and unfreezing it risks degrading the very alignment that makes it useful (some high-resolution models unfreeze it late, carefully). The LLM is frozen during connector warmup and unfrozen, gently, for instruction tuning — the stage where it must actually learn to read the new token type. The connector is the one component trained at every stage, because it is the only part whose entire job is new. Deep-fusion models (Flamingo) keep the LLM frozen even through instruction tuning and let the gated cross-attention layers carry the adaptation; native-token models (Chameleon) freeze nothing because they start from scratch. The recurring principle is the same one the architecture section stated and this section operationalized: *touch the most-pretrained component least, and the newest component most* — freeze the encoder, warm up the connector, and bring the LLM in last and slowest.
